Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1866

Name: Lineage-State Replacement Theory of Residual Oncogene-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: After the major assay-missed driver classes are removed, the remaining oncogene-negative LUADs are not random leftovers. They segregate into lineage-state programs in which tumorigenesis is sustained by altered cell identity, chromatin state, and microenvironmental circuitry rather than by a single dominant RTK/RAS/RAF lesion. The most prominent residual programs are mucinous/gastrointestinal-like NKX2-1-low states, STK11/KEAP1-associated immune-cold or liver-like states, and alveolar intermediate/KRAS-signature precursor states.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Residual negative LUAD is enriched for lineage-program drivers rather than single-lesion oncogenes

Statement: When major missed canonical drivers are excluded, residual oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinoma preferentially organizes into lineage programs defined by altered master transcription-factor states and cell identity transitions, especially NKX2-1-low mucinous/gastrointestinal-like programs, gastric-like HNF4A-positive states, and alveolar intermediate precursor-like states, with these lineage programs often explaining histology and signaling better than recurrent coding lesions do.

Domain/Scope: Applies to residual or biologically unresolved lung adenocarcinoma subsets after exclusion of canonical RTK/RAS/RAF drivers, especially invasive mucinous adenocarcinoma, KRAS-associated KC-like tumors, and single-cell or spatially resolved precursor-state studies. Also applies to mouse models used to infer lineage mechanisms when those mechanisms are explicitly paralleled in human mucinous or TTF1-low tumors.

Special Cases

  1. Many of these lineage programs coexist with canonical mutations such as KRAS, so the law concerns explanatory dominance of lineage state, not complete genetic independence.
  2. Lineage-state explanations are strongest for mucinous and TTF1-low tumors and may be less explanatory for all nonmucinous residual negatives.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. KRAS-negative invasive mucinous adenocarcinoma was enriched for hidden fusions, but the broader mucinous class itself is strongly associated with lineage markers such as TTF1 negativity and HNF4A positivity, indicating that lineage context organizes residual disease biology. [e2230.0] [e2230.1] [e2230.2] [e2230.3] [e2230.4] [e2230.5]
  2. KRAS plus Nkx2-1 haploinsufficiency induces invasive mucinous adenocarcinoma in mice, with NKX2-1 loss de-repressing mucinous programs and AP-1-linked targets, showing that lineage-factor reduction can act as a central tumor-organizing mechanism. [e2228.0] [e2228.1]
  3. Nkx2-1 loss in Kras-driven mouse tumors de-represses a latent gastric differentiation program with HNF4A, GKN1, CTSE, PDX1, and mucinous differentiation, and the same gastric markers are enriched in human NKX2-1-negative mucinous LUADs. [e2212.0]
  4. The KC subtype of KRAS-mutant LUAD is defined not by a unifying recurrent co-driver but by near-uniform low NKX2-1/TTF1 expression, GI-like differentiation, invasive mucinous histology, and HNF4A/PDX1 activation, supporting a lineage-state explanation. [e2185.0] [e2185.1] [e2213.3]
  5. KRT8-positive alveolar intermediate cells (KACs) are enriched near LUAD, increase from normal to AAH to LUAD, show elevated KRAS signaling, and can carry KRAS G12D in precursor-like contexts, indicating that transformation can proceed through a lineage-intermediate program before overt malignant genomics dominate. [e2215.0] [e2215.1]
  6. The review on non-genetic evolution in LUAD histologic progression reports that progression from lepidic to papillary/acinar/solid patterns is not explained by recurrent pattern-specific genomic drivers, but by epigenetic and transcriptional reprogramming, supporting lineage-state primacy in part of residual disease. [e2210.0] [e2163.1]
  7. In never-smoker EGFR/ALK-wild-type LUAD, broad proteogenomic clustering identified four pathway-state subgroups rather than one rescued hidden driver, reinforcing that some negative tumors are best explained as state-defined subtypes rather than single-lesion cancers. [e2164.0]
  8. This law was logically induced by integrating mouse lineage-factor perturbation models, human mucinous and TTF1-low subtype data, and single-cell precursor-state evidence, all of which converge on cell-identity programs as explanatory units after canonical-driver depletion.

Statement 1: Immune-cold and metabolic residual negatives are often suppressor-state or proteomic-state tumors

Statement: A major subset of residual oncogene-negative LUAD is driven by suppressor-loss and proteomic-state mechanisms centered on STK11, KEAP1, PTEN, and downstream secreted or phospho-signaling programs; in these tumors, biologic behavior is better predicted by immune-cold, metabolic, oxidative-stress, estrogen-signaling, neutrophil-degranulation, FGL1/HNF1A/CPS1, or phospho-network states than by a single canonical oncogenic lesion.

Domain/Scope: Applies to driver-negative or non-actionable LUAD subsets, especially never-smoker EGFR/ALK-negative cohorts, PP/proximal-proliferative or structurally altered subtypes, and STK11/KEAP1-enriched cohorts with proteomic or phosphoproteomic data. It is most relevant after exclusion of missed canonical RTK, RAS, RAF, and fusion drivers.

Special Cases

  1. Some of these tumors still harbor known genomic lesions such as STK11 or KEAP1 mutations; the claim is that pathway-state readouts are more explanatory than mutation status alone.
  2. Not all immune-cold tumors are STK11/KEAP1-driven; the law concerns the recurrent residual subset highlighted in the evidence.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. STK11-mutant tumors show a proteomics-specific neutrophil degranulation program that is invisible at RNA level, demonstrating that important residual biology can exist as a protein-state mechanism rather than a genomic-driver call. [e2220.4]
  2. Proteogenomic characterization identifies KEAP1 mutations that alter KEAP1 protein abundance and NRF2 phosphorylation without corresponding RNA effects, proving that pathway activation can be hidden at the protein/post-translational level. [e2220.1]
  3. The STK11/FGL1/HNF1A/CPS1 Subtype 4 program shows that STK11 mutation alone does not explain the phenotype; STK11-wild-type tumors with reduced STK11 expression can enter the same liver-like, immune-evasive state. [e2216.0] [e2216.1] [e2216.2]
  4. Never-smoker EGFR/ALK-negative LUADs (NENA) show estrogen-signaling-associated hidden biology involving STK11 mutation, GPER1 copy-number effects, chromosome 14/21 deletions, and hypomethylation of LLGL2/ST14, again indicating pathway-state logic beyond one dominant canonical lesion. [e2249.0] [e2249.2] [e2249.3] [e2249.4]
  5. PTEN protein loss occurs in 13/20 oncogene-negative tumors without genomic PTEN mutations, directly showing that non-genomic suppressor inactivation helps create residual pathway-active negatives. [e2171.1]
  6. Onc-negative RAS/PI3K LUADs are explained at the pathway-state level by combined suppressor loss such as Nf1/Rasa1/Pten and show dual pERK/pAKT activation in roughly 45% of human oncogene-negative tumors, supporting a state model rather than a single lesion model. [e2178.0] [e2178.1] [e2171.0]
  7. TTF1-negative LUAD is described as a poor-prognosis subtype enriched for KEAP1/NRF2 and DNA-repair programs with distinctive therapy sensitivities, supporting residual classification by pathway state and lineage state jointly. [e2218.6]
  8. RPPA and phosphoproteomic analyses show MAPK or mTOR activation without obvious genomic lesions in some LUADs, indicating that residual negative biology can be expressed primarily as phospho-network state. [e2219.6] [e2220.3]
  9. This law was logically induced by combining suppressor-loss studies, phosphoproteomic studies, and never-smoker proteogenomic subtype studies showing that mutation status alone under-explains residual oncogene-negative behavior.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If residual negative LUADs are stratified by NKX2-1/HNF4A/KRT8/FGL1/CPS1 rather than by mutation alone, histology, immune phenotype, and drug-sensitivity patterns will become more coherent than under DNA-only grouping.
  2. If TTF1-low or mucinous residual negatives undergo single-cell and spatial profiling, they will show stronger convergence on gastric-like or alveolar-intermediate programs than on recurrent private coding drivers.
  3. If residual STK11/KEAP1/PTEN-like negatives are assayed with proteomics and phosphoproteomics, protein-state biomarkers will classify therapy response better than mutation status alone.
  4. If residual oncogene-negative tumors are tested for suppressor-loss pathway activity by IHC or phosphoproteomics, a substantial fraction will show convergent pERK/pAKT or NRF2-related activation despite heterogeneous DNA lesions.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If lineage-state perturbation is therapeutically central, forced restoration of NKX2-1-like programs in mucinous residual negatives may suppress tumor fitness even when no single genomic lesion is corrected.
  2. If alveolar intermediate/KAC programs are genuine precursors of some residual negatives, then airway-field or adjacent-normal sampling in at-risk patients will reveal detectable lineage-state abnormalities before conventional pathology does.
  3. If immune-cold residual negatives are organized by secreted-ligand circuits such as FGL1 or neutrophil degranulation proteins, circulating or bronchoalveolar lavage proteomics may capture driver-state signatures not visible genomically.
  4. If residual-negative classification is truly lineage-dominant, cross-cancer analogies with gastric-like, hepatocyte-like, or basal-like states may predict vulnerabilities better than lung-specific mutation schemas.

Negative Experiments

  1. After complete genomic rescue, test whether residual negatives still cluster by lineage and pathway state; if they do not, the theory overstates state-based organization.
  2. Compare mutation-based versus lineage/proteomic-state-based prediction of histology and therapy response in residual negatives; if state-based models do not improve performance, the theory is weakened.
  3. Experimentally perturb NKX2-1/HNF4A/FGL1/HNF1A axes in representative residual-negative models; if these programs do not alter tumor fitness, the causal role of lineage-state drivers is weakened.
  4. Test whether residual-negative tumors are enriched for noncoding, proteomic, and suppressor-loss states after rigorous exclusion of low-purity pseudo-negatives; if not, the residual-driver framing is weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some residual negative cohorts remain molecularly heterogeneous without clear assignment to either lineage-state or suppressor/proteomic-state groups, especially broad WES-based cohorts with many candidate genes but little functional validation. [e2165.0] [e2186.0]
