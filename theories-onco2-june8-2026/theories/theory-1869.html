Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1869

Name: Hidden Driver Architecture Theory of Oncogene-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: A major fraction of 'oncogene-negative' lung adenocarcinomas are not truly driverless; they are enriched for driver architectures that are systematically underdetected by hotspot DNA testing and even by standard exome workflows. These drivers fall into recurrent mechanistic classes: cryptic fusions, exon-skipping/regulatory loss, focal or complex amplifications, noncoding cis-regulatory lesions, and combinatorial loss of negative regulators that converge on RTK/RAS/RAF or parallel mitogenic pathway activation.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Driver negativity is often an assay artifact of lesion class mismatch

Statement: In lung adenocarcinoma, the probability that a tumor is labeled 'oncogene-negative' rises sharply when the dominant lesion class is structural, splice-altering, noncoding-regulatory, copy-number-defined, or combinatorial suppressor loss rather than a canonical hotspot SNV; expanding profiling across DNA structural variants, RNA fusions/splicing, copy number, and protein-state assays therefore systematically rescues a substantial subset of apparently negative tumors.

Domain/Scope: Applies to lung adenocarcinoma cohorts initially classified as negative or pan-negative by limited canonical-driver testing, especially EGFR/KRAS/ALK-focused assays, hotspot panels, WES-centered workflows, or incomplete RNA/fusion testing. Best supported in treatment-naive primary LUAD, never-smoker LUAD, invasive mucinous adenocarcinoma, and RTK/RAS/RAF-pathway-negative retrospective cohorts; not intended as a statement about tumors already exhaustively profiled with integrated WGS/RNA/proteomics/epigenomics.

Special Cases

  1. A minority of tumors remain unexplained even after expanded profiling, implying genuinely rare, distributed, epigenetic, microenvironmental, or still-unknown drivers.
  2. Some lesions are rescued only at the pathway-state level rather than as a single causal genomic event.
  3. Low tumor purity and subclonality can make even broad assays falsely negative.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Statement 1: Residual negatives are enriched for pathway-state drivers rather than single proto-oncogene lesions

Statement: After broad genomic rescue of canonical lesions, the remaining oncogene-negative LUADs are disproportionately explained by pathway states created by combinatorial suppressor loss, lineage-state rewiring, proteomic/phospho-network activation, and chromatin-regulatory alteration, rather than by a single recurrent hidden kinase mutation. These residual tumors should therefore be subclassified by pathway activation topology and lineage program instead of by gene-level recurrence alone.

Domain/Scope: Applies to the residual subset of LUAD remaining unexplained after at least exome-plus-RNA or WGS-level driver interrogation, especially RTK/RAS/RAF-pathway-negative, never-smoker, and histologically distinctive subsets. Not intended for canonical single-driver tumors such as EGFR-, KRAS-, ALK-, ROS1-, RET-, or ERBB2-driven adenocarcinomas.

Special Cases

  1. Some residual tumors still harbor ultra-rare single lesions that larger cohorts may eventually identify.
  2. Pathway states can coexist with weak or secondary genomic lesions, so classification is probabilistic rather than absolute.
  3. Subtype-defining states may be histology- or smoking-context-dependent.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If a pan-negative LUAD cohort is profiled with matched WGS, long-read RNA sequencing, methylation, quantitative proteomics, and phosphoproteomics, the number of tumors assigned to one of four mechanistic bins—cryptic structural driver, suppressor-loss pathway state, chromatin-regulatory state, or lineage-state program—will substantially exceed the number assigned to a newly discovered recurrent hotspot mutation.
  2. In residual WGS-negative LUAD, clustering on phospho-proteomic and lineage markers will produce therapeutically coherent groups with stronger drug-response associations than clustering on residual DNA lesions alone.
  3. Tumors with dual pERK/pAKT positivity but no proto-oncogene lesion will be enriched for NF1/RASA1/PTEN dysfunction by a combination of deep CN/SV analysis, protein loss, methylation, or allele-specific expression assays.
  4. Residual non-CAGA tumors with outlier ERBB2, CDK4, MDM2, CAV2, or FRS2 expression will show a higher burden of SE-SV overlap or local chromatin-topology disruption than expression-matched canonical-driver-positive LUAD.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. A sizeable subset of the remaining unexplained LUADs will prove to be driven by stable protein-state circuits with no obligate recurrent DNA lesion, analogous to phospho-state-defined kinase addiction states in other cancers.
  2. Some residual oncogene-negative LUADs will be classifiable by field-state or premalignant epithelial intermediates such as KAC-like programs, with the 'driver' being a maintained abnormal differentiation trajectory rather than a single tumor-private mutation.
  3. Integrated microbiome plus spatial phosphoproteomics may reveal that a fraction of residual mucinous or immune-cold LUAD depends on extracellular ligand stabilization or stromal/neutrophil secretory programs rather than tumor-cell-autonomous genomic lesions.
  4. Long-read and ecDNA-focused profiling may show that a nontrivial portion of the deepest residual group is explained by circular amplicons or complex rearrangements that short-read WGS still undercalls.

Negative Experiments

  1. If residual oncogene-negative LUADs, after exhaustive profiling, are dominated by newly found recurrent single-gene hotspot mutations rather than pathway states, this theory is weakened.
  2. If phospho-proteomic and lineage-state clustering adds little explanatory or predictive value beyond DNA lesion calls in residual tumors, the theory overstates the importance of non-genomic states.
  3. If NF1/RASA1/PTEN dysfunction is not enriched in dual pERK/pAKT oncogene-negative tumors, the suppressor-loss pathway-state component is weakened.
  4. If non-CAGA outlier-expression tumors do not show increased rates of local structural/chromatin-regulatory abnormalities relative to controls, the chromatin-state component is weakened.
