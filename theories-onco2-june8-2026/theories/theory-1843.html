Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1843

Name: Distributed Oncogenesis Theory of Driver-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: Oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinoma is not a single disease class but an admixture of three mechanistic reservoirs: (A) occult canonical or near-canonical oncogenic lesions missed by limited assays or tumor purity constraints, (B) noncanonical activation of growth pathways through negative-regulator loss, splice alterations, noncoding regulatory rewiring, or proteomic/phosphoproteomic activation states, and (C) state-dominant tumors in which progression is driven more by lineage plasticity, stromal or immune ecosystem programs, angiogenesis, or non-genetic evolution than by a single dominant kinase lesion. The apparent persistence of a driver-negative fraction after broader sequencing reflects mixing of these reservoirs under one label. The theory predicts that assay choice, subtype classification, and therapy selection should be reservoir-specific rather than uniform.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Residual driver-negativity is a mixture phenomenon, not a single biological category

Statement: The residual driver-negative fraction in LUAD persists after progressively broader profiling because the label aggregates at least three distinct classes: occult canonical-like lesions detectable with deeper/broader genomic assays, noncanonical pathway activators that require integrative multi-omic readouts, and state-dominant tumors whose biology is organized primarily by lineage, immune, stromal, or spatial programs rather than a single canonical oncogene.

Domain/Scope: Applies to lung adenocarcinoma cohorts called driver-negative, oncogene-negative, triple-negative, pan-negative, RTK/RAS/RAF-pathway-negative, EGFR/ALK-wild-type, or similar after routine or study-specific molecular testing. Most applicable to resected or treatment-naive human LUAD cohorts, including never-smoker-enriched cohorts, but also to metastatic cohorts where assay incompleteness and tumor heterogeneity affect driver detection. Not intended for clearly oncogene-addicted EGFR-, ALK-, ROS1-, RET-, METex14-, KRAS-, BRAF-, ERBB2- or NTRK-driven tumors once those lesions are definitively identified.

Special Cases

  1. Percentages are not directly comparable across studies because negative definitions vary substantially, ranging from EGFR/KRAS/ALK-only exclusion to broader RTK/RAS/RAF/fusion/exon-skipping panels.
  2. Some apparent driver-negative cases reflect incomplete testing or low tumor cellularity rather than distinct biology.
  3. Advanced treated tumors may acquire reservoir-B or reservoir-C features secondarily, so untreated primary tumors and heavily treated metastatic tumors should be analyzed separately when testing the theory.
  4. Never-smoker cohorts are more likely to over-represent fusion, ligand, lineage-state, and angiogenic/stromal mechanisms, whereas smoking-associated cohorts may over-represent tumor-suppressor-loss, CIN, APOBEC, and checkpoint-defective mechanisms.

Explanation: This is not a standard named theory in the cited literature; it is an integrative framework synthesizing multiple partially overlapping explanations into a single causal architecture for the residual negative fraction.

What Already Exists: The literature already shows that some apparently negative LUADs are explained by rare hidden drivers, some by pathway-state biology detectable only with multi-omics, and some by lineage or microenvironmental states. Individual studies, however, usually emphasize only one of these mechanisms at a time.

What is Novel: The novelty is the explicit claim that the persistent driver-negative fraction is durable because it is a mixed category composed of three separable reservoirs, and that the durability of the label is therefore expected rather than paradoxical.

Statement 1: Reservoir membership predicts the correct discovery and validation stack

Statement: Each mechanistic reservoir has a distinct optimal validation stack: occult canonical-like lesions require deeper and broader DNA/RNA assays with attention to purity and fusion/splicing detection; noncanonical pathway activators require integrated DNA/RNA/protein/phosphoprotein and often chromatin or 3D-genome assays; state-dominant tumors require single-cell, spatial, and perturbational analysis of lineage, stromal, and immune circuits. Therefore, profiling technologies are not interchangeable in driver-negative LUAD.

Domain/Scope: Applies to translational discovery design, biomarker development, and assay triage for unresolved or ostensibly driver-negative LUAD. Most relevant in fresh-frozen or well-preserved biospecimens where RNA, phosphoprotein, and spatial assays are feasible, but the prediction also applies to retrospective FFPE-driven workflows when assay ordering must be optimized. Not intended to replace standard targeted genotyping in clearly driver-positive disease.

Special Cases

  1. A single tumor may occupy more than one reservoir and therefore require more than one validation stack.
  2. Rare kinase fusions with strong genomic evidence may not require full spatial or proteomic profiling for initial therapeutic assignment, though such profiling may still explain resistance or phenotype.
  3. Fresh-tissue phosphoproteomics and spatial assays may be technically infeasible in many routine clinical samples, so some reservoir-B and reservoir-C cases will remain unresolved under FFPE-only workflows.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. In a prospective unresolved LUAD cohort, cases with mucinous histology, low purity, or discordant clinical suspicion for fusion-driven disease will disproportionately resolve with deep RNA sequencing and long-read or hybrid-capture fusion detection, consistent with reservoir A.
  2. In unresolved LUADs with strong pERK, pAKT, pS6, SHP2, SOS1, NRF2, or neutrophil-degranulation protein signatures but no canonical lesion, reanalysis with WGS plus proteogenomics and chromatin-aware methods will be enriched for negative-regulator loss, regulatory SVs, enhancer rewiring, or phospho-state drivers, consistent with reservoir B.
  3. Tumors that remain unresolved after broad DNA/RNA and phosphoproteomic profiling will cluster more strongly by spatial ecosystem features, lineage-state markers, or stromal architecture than by additional mutational burden, consistent with reservoir C.
  4. A triage workflow using four initial features—tumor purity, mucinous/lineage pathology, phosphoprotein outliers, and spatial stromal complexity—will solve more hidden-driver cases per sample than a uniform panel-plus-WES workflow.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. A low-cost preclassification model built from pathology, shallow RNA, selected phosphoproteins, and one spatial metric will be able to assign a dominant reservoir before full profiling and thereby improve discovery yield and trial enrollment efficiency.
  2. Some reservoir-C tumors will fail lesion-matched targeted therapy even when a rare kinase lesion is found, because lineage or ecosystem circuitry dominates phenotype and resistance from baseline.
  3. A subset of persistent unresolved cases will prove to be driven by transient or oscillatory signaling states detectable only in fresh-tissue phosphoproteomics or ex vivo perturbational phosphodynamics, not in static DNA/RNA assays.
  4. Reservoir B and C may be partially convertible states: blocking one stromal or lineage circuit may unmask latent oncogene addiction in a subset of tumors.

Negative Experiments

  1. Apply a uniform maximal profiling stack (deep WGS, long-read fusion/splicing analysis, proteomics/phosphoproteomics, ChIP-based enhancer mapping, and spatial/single-cell profiling) to a large unresolved LUAD cohort. If almost all tumors fall into one common mechanism rather than multiple separable classes, the reservoir-mixing claim is weakened.
  2. Test whether pre-specified features predict which assay stack solves a case. If purity, histology, phosphoprotein state, and spatial architecture fail to enrich for distinct solution modalities, the theory loses its translational specificity.
  3. Examine whether tumors unresolved by DNA/RNA/protein profiling are truly enriched for lineage/ecosystem explanations. If they are not, reservoir C is overstated.
  4. Compare outcomes of assay-triaged versus uniform workflows in a prospective study. If triage does not improve hidden-driver yield, time-to-solution, or subtype reproducibility, the theory's practical claim is undermined.
