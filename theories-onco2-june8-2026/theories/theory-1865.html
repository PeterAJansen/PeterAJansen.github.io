Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1865

Name: Mechanism-Compression Theory of Oncogene-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: Most apparent oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinomas are not explained by a long tail of unrelated genes, but by a compressed set of recurrent driver architectures that standard workflows under-detect: kinase and ligand fusions, splice-mediated receptor stabilization, non-hotspot receptor activation, focal/complex amplification, enhancer or super-enhancer rewiring, and loss of negative regulators of RAS/PI3K/Rho/NRF2 signaling. Thus, the central scientific problem is to classify tumors by driver mechanism family and assay failure mode, then reserve a smaller residual category for genuinely pathway-state or lineage-state tumors that are not reducible to one lesion.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Assay-negative status decomposes into pseudo-negative and residual-biologic classes

Statement: When lung adenocarcinomas are called driver-negative by hotspot-limited, mutation-centric, copy-number-insensitive, fusion-insensitive, splice-insensitive, or low-purity genomic workflows, a large fraction are later reclassified as driver-positive after integrated profiling; the remaining unresolved fraction is smaller and enriched for pathway-state, lineage-state, proteomic, noncoding, or combinatorial suppressor-loss mechanisms rather than a single missed canonical lesion.

Domain/Scope: Applies to primary lung adenocarcinoma cohorts, especially never-smoker, mucinous, and RTK/RAS/RAF-negative subsets, where an initial negative call was made by restricted clinical assays, incomplete WES/WGS interpretation, or incompletely integrated DNA/RNA testing and then reassessed by broader targeted DNA sequencing, RNA fusion/splice assays, WGS-SV, copy-number analysis, methylation, proteomics, or phosphoproteomics. It is most applicable to studies explicitly reporting pre- and post-expansion reclassification rates.

Special Cases

  1. Not every initially negative tumor is a technical false negative; several cohorts still retain substantial unresolved fractions after broad profiling.
  2. The pseudo-negative fraction is expected to be highest in cohorts defined by limited hotspot panels or restricted fusion testing, and lower in already deeply profiled WGS/RNA-seq cohorts.
  3. Low tumor purity, stromal admixture, and regional sampling can independently create false negatives even under broad assay designs.

Statement 1: Most rescued oncogene-negative LUAD collapses into a compact ontology of mechanism families

Statement: Across initially oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinoma, the majority of rescued drivers can be mapped to a small recurrent set of activation architectures rather than an unlimited set of gene-specific explanations: kinase-fusion dimerization, ligand-fusion ERBB activation, exon-skipping-mediated receptor stabilization, non-kinase-domain receptor structural activation, focal or complex dosage amplification, enhancer or super-enhancer rewiring, and loss of negative regulators of RAS/PI3K/Rho/NRF2/cell-cycle control. Mechanism family therefore has higher explanatory value than gene identity alone for classifying biology and predicting therapeutic logic in rare-lesion tumors.

Domain/Scope: Applies to lung adenocarcinoma studies that broaden driver discovery beyond canonical hotspot panels using RNA-seq, WGS structural-variant analysis, copy-number profiling, methylation, ChIP-seq, or proteogenomics, and to cross-cancer analogies when the same activation architecture recurs. It is strongest for lesions discovered after an initial negative call and weaker for well-known canonical positive tumors discovered upfront.

Special Cases

  1. Some lesions belong to more than one architecture, such as ALK fusions that are both structural kinase activators and phospho-state outliers, or NRG1 fusions that are both ligand-fusion and receptor-activation mechanisms.
  2. Some amplicons remain unresolved at the target-gene level even when the mechanism family is clear.
  3. This law does not claim that all residual unexplained tumors already fit one of these families; it claims that most rescued cases do.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If initially negative LUADs are prospectively annotated by assay-failure mode before re-profiling, then fusion_not_assayed and splice_event_not_assayed will dominate rescue in never-smoker and mucinous cohorts, whereas copy_number_not_interpreted and structural_complexity will dominate mixed-smoking WGS cohorts.
  2. If rare-lesion LUADs are grouped by mechanism family rather than gene name, therapy-response prediction will outperform gene-level grouping for fusion kinases, ligand-fusion ERBB states, and exon-skipping receptor states.
  3. If all residual WES-negative but not WGS-profiled LUADs undergo splice-aware RNA-seq plus targeted long-range SV assays, a measurable fraction will collapse into MET exon 14, cryptic kinase fusion, or non-hotspot RTK-activation families rather than entirely novel biology.
  4. If broad profiling is focused on KRAS-negative mucinous adenocarcinoma, ligand-fusion and fusion-based architectures will account for a higher fraction of rescued cases than classical hotspot mutations.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If long-read sequencing plus Hi-C is applied to residual RPA(-)G tumors, a clinically meaningful subset will prove to be enhancer-rewired or ecDNA/topology-driven cancers with no necessary coding oncogene mutation.
  2. If phosphoproteomic outlier states are used as primary discovery seeds rather than genomic negatives, additional recurrent driver families will emerge around SHP2, RhoGEFs, or ERBB phosphorylation networks in tumors lacking recurrent DNA lesions.
  3. If cross-cancer mechanism-family transfer is valid, LUAD should contain rare promoter-fusion or enhancer-hijacking activations of PI3K/SRC/MAPK effectors analogous to PIK3CA promoter fusions or WASF2-FGR, but at frequencies below current panel detection.
  4. If the residual unresolved class is truly biologically distinct, it will show stronger enrichment for lineage-state and microenvironmental states than for private coding drivers when matched for assay depth and tumor purity.

Negative Experiments

  1. Apply a fully integrated DNA/RNA/CN/SV/proteomic workflow to a new large negative LUAD cohort; if rescued cases do not concentrate into the proposed mechanism families, the compression claim is weakened.
  2. Compare mechanism-family labels versus gene-level labels for predicting drug response in rare lesions; if gene labels perform equally or better across cohorts, the central ontology claim is weakened.
  3. Remove low-purity and incomplete-testing samples from negative cohorts; if the apparent pseudo-negative component does not materially shrink, the assay-failure decomposition is weakened.
  4. Test whether mucinous, never-smoker, and nonmucinous residual negatives actually differ by mechanism-family enrichment; if not, the theory overstates architecture-specific stratification.
