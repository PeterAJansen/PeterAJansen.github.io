Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1860

Name: Distributed Weak-Driver Convergence in Driver-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: A large fraction of driver-negative LUAD is not truly oncogene-null; instead it is driven by distributed subthreshold lesions that converge on signaling output without creating a classic single dominant driver. These lesions include RasGAP loss, weak RTK copy gain, wild-type lineage factor amplification, enhancer hijacking, noncoding promoter mutations, exon skipping, and phosphoproteomic activation. The clinically important unit is therefore pathway-output state, not lesion class.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Convergence-without-recurrence law

Statement: In driver-negative LUAD, many tumors achieve oncogenic sufficiency through heterogeneous, individually rare lesions that converge on a limited set of outputs—RTK/RAS/MAPK activation, PI3K-AKT-mTOR activation, lineage-survival enforcement, or cell-cycle amplification—without any one substitute lesion recurring at high frequency.

Domain/Scope: Applies to panel-negative or WES-negative LUAD cohorts later reassessed by WGS, fusion analysis, copy-number profiling, noncoding analysis, or phosphoproteomics. Especially relevant to RPA(-), oncogene-negative, and non-CAGA cohorts.

Special Cases

  1. Some cohorts still contain hidden canonical drivers rather than true weak-driver convergence.
  2. Certain histology-linked subgroups such as NRG1-fusion IMA or RET/NTRK fusion disease are better viewed as rare strong drivers, not distributed weak drivers.
  3. The law concerns pathway sufficiency through convergence, not equal functional importance of every lesion.

Statement 1: Output-over-lesion law

Statement: In driver-negative LUAD, direct measurement of pathway output—phosphoproteomic MAPK activity, PI3K/AKT activation, ferroptosis resistance, or lineage-factor dependency—predicts tumor biology more reliably than the specific weak lesion used to generate that output.

Domain/Scope: Applies to residual or noncanonical LUAD cohorts with phosphoproteomic, proteogenomic, RPPA, or functional signaling readouts. Especially relevant when lesions are copy-number low, noncoding, or structurally complex.

Special Cases

  1. If a strong canonical driver is present, lesion identity can still dominate output.
  2. Bulk proteomic measures may blur tumor-cell and microenvironmental contributions in some cohorts.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. RPPA analysis in LUAD showed that many KRAS-wild-type tumors still had significant MAPK activation, meaning pathway output was not fully captured by canonical mutation status. [e1870.5]
  2. In the Taiwanese proteogenomic cohort, most EGFR-wild-type cases had low MAPK signaling but the minority with high MAPK signaling were enriched for KRAS mutations, showing that pathway activity can identify biologically coherent subsets beyond mutation labels. [e1893.1] [e1893.0]
  3. The oncogene-negative RAS/PI3K mouse-human convergence model classified human tumors by pERK and pAKT activation and by a mouse-derived expression signature, supporting pathway-output-defined subtyping over individual lesion identity. [e1863.0] [e1863.1] [e1863.2] [e1863.3] [e1863.4]
  4. KEAP1-mutant LUAD showed increased NFE2L2 phosphorylation and NRF2 signatures without RNA-level changes, demonstrating that post-transcriptional signaling output can be the critical biologic variable rather than lesion annotation alone. [e1900.3]
  5. EGFR-mutant and ALK-fusion tumors showed SHP2/PTPN11 and ALK phosphosite outliers respectively, illustrating a general principle that phosphosite activity can reveal actionable output states beyond DNA status. [e1900.1] [e1900.5]
  6. FeSig-high EGFR-wild-type LUAD was defined by ferroptosis-resistance output and fibroblast communication rather than by a common DNA lesion, yet this output state predicted prognosis and therapy sensitivity. [e1904.0] [e1904.4] [e1904.5] [e1904.6]
  7. This law was logically inferred from repeated discordance between genomic lesion identity and measured downstream signaling or stress-adaptation state across LUAD cohorts.

Statement 2: Lineage-survival substitution law

Statement: When no canonical RTK/RAS/RAF driver dominates, driver-negative LUAD can be stabilized by amplification or overexpression of wild-type lineage and survival regulators, producing dependency on normal developmental circuitry rather than mutationally activated kinases.

Domain/Scope: Applies to LUADs with focal amplification, enhancer hijacking, or overexpression of wild-type regulatory genes, especially NKX2-1- or ERBB2-centered cases and related SE-SV or copy-number-driven subgroups.

Special Cases

  1. Some wild-type overexpression events may still cooperate with other private lesions.
  2. Not all amplified genes in a region are functional drivers; some are passengers of structural complexity.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Focal 14q13.3 amplification identified NKX2-1 as a lineage-survival oncogene in LUAD; NKX2-1 knockdown reduced viability and colony formation in expressing lines while A549 cells lacking NKX2-1 protein were unaffected. [e1884.0]
  2. ERBB2 overexpression in non-CAGA LUAD arose through super-enhancer formation and structural rearrangement near HNF1beta without canonical ERBB2 mutation or focal amplification, directly demonstrating wild-type gene activation as a driver-like mechanism. [e1868.0] [e1868.1] [e1868.5] [e1868.6]
  3. The 23-gene SE-SV outlier set, including ERBB2, EGFR, CDK4, MDM2, FRS2, CAV2, and FGF3/4/19, associated with postoperative recurrence, implying that wild-type overexpression programs can behave as driver-like events in non-CAGA LUAD. [e1868.5] [e1868.2] [e1868.3] [e1868.4]
  4. TERT, VEGFA, CDK4/CCND1/CCNE1, and other recurrent amplifications in LUAD indicate that copy-number gain of wild-type survival or proliferative genes is a common noncanonical oncogenic mechanism. [e1884.1] [e1884.2] [e1884.3]
  5. The conceptual framework from the non-CAGA study explicitly proposed that wild-type genes may become drivers via overexpression induced by structural/epigenomic architecture, broadening the meaning of a driver event in driver-negative LUAD. [e1868.6]
  6. This law was logically inferred from the shared pattern that several driver-negative-enriched cohorts nominate wild-type overexpression of lineage, cell-cycle, or RTK signaling genes in the absence of classical activating mutations.

Statement 3: Assay-depth reclassification law

Statement: The observed size of the driver-negative LUAD compartment is inversely related to assay depth and modality breadth; however, the tumors that remain unresolved after deep profiling are disproportionately enriched for distributed weak drivers and pathway-output phenotypes rather than for one additional undiscovered recurrent hotspot.

Domain/Scope: Applies to cohort-level estimates of driver-negative LUAD generated by routine panels, WES, WGS, RNA-seq, fusion assays, and proteogenomic profiling.

Special Cases

  1. The law concerns population-level trends; individual tumors may still harbor strong undiscovered genomic drivers.
  2. Different panels classify driver-negative status differently, so exact percentages vary by platform and study era.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. In the standard-test-negative triple-negative cohort, CGP found alterations in 94% of cases and actionable events in 31%, including hidden EGFR/KRAS mutations and ROS1/RET/MET/ERBB2 alterations, showing that shallow testing inflates driver-negative classification. [e1849.0] [e1849.1] [e1849.2] [e1849.3]
  2. In never-smoker LUAD classified as oncogene-negative by WES, additional clinical sequencing, FISH, RNA-seq, and deeper WES raised driver detection to about 81%, and lower tumor cellularity explained many initial false negatives. [e1901.0]
  3. WGS of RPA(-)E LUAD reclassified one-third of samples as actually RTK/RAS/RAF-pathway-positive, again shrinking the residual compartment with deeper profiling. [e1864.0]
  4. The any-genotyping no-driver group in LCMC explicitly mixed true negatives with incompletely tested tumors, warning that crude no-driver frequencies overestimate biological driver-negativity. [e1882.1]
  5. Even after broad profiling, residual groups such as RPA(-)G or non-CAGA LUAD remained heterogeneous and lacked one dominant recurrent replacement lesion, supporting the second half of the law that true residuals are not simply waiting for one more hotspot discovery. [e1864.0] [e1868.0] [e1891.0]

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Across multi-institutional cohorts, the fraction labeled driver-negative will drop stepwise from routine panel to comprehensive DNA/RNA profiling, but the residual group will become more enriched for pathway-output biomarkers such as pERK/pAKT, NRF2 phosphosignaling, or fibroblast communication scores.
  2. Tumors with weak convergent lesions (for example low-level MET/ERBB2 gain, RasGAP loss, or SE-SV overexpression) will cluster together by downstream signaling state more strongly than by lesion identity.
  3. Residual WGS-negative tumors with ILF2 promoter mutation or complex amplicons will show disproportionate dependency on DNA-damage response or transcriptional buffering pathways compared with rare-fusion-positive controls.
  4. A combined classifier using phosphoproteomics plus copy-number/noncoding features will identify more reproducible therapeutic groups than mutation-only classifiers in the residual driver-negative population.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Some residual driver-negative LUADs will require at least two weak lesions from different classes—such as RasGAP loss plus wild-type RTK overexpression or noncoding promoter mutation plus cell-cycle amplification—to maintain tumorigenicity; single lesions will fail in engineered models.
  2. Enhancer-hijacked wild-type overexpression states and RasGAP-loss states will converge on a small set of phosphosite patterns that are targetable by the same drug combinations despite distinct upstream lesions.
  3. A substantial subset of clinically driver-negative tumors will be classifiable by ecDNA architecture or 3D-genome rewiring rather than by sequence changes, and these tumors will show selective sensitivity to transcriptional cofactor inhibition.
  4. Low-frequency lineage-factor amplifications such as NKX2-1 and noncoding ILF2 activation may cooperate in ways not yet recognized, producing mixed lineage-survival and genome-stability phenotypes.

Negative Experiments

  1. If residual deep-profiled driver-negative LUADs consistently reveal one high-frequency recurrent substitute oncogene in larger cohorts, the distributed weak-driver theory is weakened.
  2. If pathway-output measurements do not outperform lesion identity for biological clustering or drug-response prediction in residual cohorts, the output-over-lesion law is weakened.
  3. If wild-type overexpression events such as NKX2-1 amplification or ERBB2 SE-SV overexpression prove dispensable in endogenous models, the lineage-survival substitution law is weakened.
  4. If assay-depth expansion fails to materially reduce apparent driver-negative frequency in modern cohorts, the assay-depth reclassification law is weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. This theory does not explain purely state-dominant progression models in which no clear lesion convergence is needed, such as some non-genetic progression or L2S-only frameworks. [e1844.0] [e1844.3] [e1895.0]
  2. It only partially explains immune-risk and radiomics-only driver-negative classifiers that capture prognosis without directly specifying the weak-lesion architecture. [e1879.0] [e1879.1] [e1880.0] [e1880.1]
