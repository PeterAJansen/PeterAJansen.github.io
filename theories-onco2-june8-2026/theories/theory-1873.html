Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1873

Name: Network-Replacement Theory of Oncogenic Control in Driver-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: In driver-negative LUAD, oncogenic control is often achieved not by one dominant activated receptor or kinase, but by replacement of the missing canonical trunk driver with an equivalent control module at another biological layer. Three major replacement modules recur: (1) pathway-equivalence modules, in which suppressor loss recreates RAS/MAPK and PI3K signaling without a canonical oncogene; (2) regulatory-equivalence modules, in which enhancer hijacking, super-enhancer formation, methylation, or structural variation recreate oncogene output without coding activation; and (3) ecosystem-equivalence modules, in which immune exclusion, angiogenesis, fibroblast states, or lineage transitions stabilize malignant fitness. The theory predicts that driver-negative LUAD should be classified by the layer at which proliferative and survival control is restored.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Pathway-equivalence law

Statement: When canonical RTK/RAS/RAF driver lesions are absent, LUAD can recreate equivalent mitogenic output through combinatorial loss of negative regulators or rare pathway-member alterations, producing a driver-negative state that is biochemically RAS/MAPK- and/or PI3K/AKT-active.

Domain/Scope: Applies to oncogene-negative LUAD and related NSCLC subsets in which broad profiling excludes major canonical activating lesions but reveals suppressor loss or rare pathway-member abnormalities. Strongest for cohorts with direct pathway readouts by IHC, RPPA, phosphoproteomics, or functional perturbation.

Special Cases

  1. Some pathway-equivalent tumors are driven mainly by dual RAS plus PI3K activation, whereas others may be MAPK-dominant or mTOR-dominant.
  2. Certain pathway states may be exposure-linked, such as RASA1/NF1 co-loss in smoking-associated disease or STK11-linked estrogen signaling in EGFR/ALK-negative never-smokers.
  3. A minority of tumors may show pathway activation without yet-identified causal lesions.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. NF1/RASA1/PTEN co-inactivation efficiently initiates oncogene-negative LUAD in vivo and creates an explicit Onc-negative RAS/PI3K subtype with pERK and pAKT activation plus SHP2/AKT inhibitor sensitivity. [e2399.0] [e2399.1] [e2399.2] [e2392.0] [e2392.1] [e2392.2]
  2. RASA1 and NF1 co-loss defines a smoking-associated NSCLC subset mutually exclusive with canonical drivers and sensitive to MEK inhibition, demonstrating that dual RasGAP loss can substitute for a canonical oncogenic driver. [e2422.0]
  3. Oncogene-negative LUAD in TCGA was enriched for NF1, RIT1, RASA1, SOS1, VAV1, ARHGAP35, ERBB2 amplification, and MET alterations, indicating that hidden pathway-member lesions recur specifically in the negative set. [e2431.0] [e2431.1] [e2431.2] [e2431.3] [e2431.4] [e2391.0] [e2391.1] [e2391.2] [e2391.3] [e2391.4]
  4. RIT1 mutations in oncogene-negative LUAD activate MEK/ERK and PI3K/AKT signaling and transform cells, providing a rare pathway-member replacement driver. [e2381.0]
  5. MAPK and mTOR pathway activity often exceeds what can be explained by known DNA lesions, with KRAS-wild-type tumors still showing high MAPK activation and multiple mTOR states linked only partly to PIK3CA, STK11, AMPK, or AKT features. [e2431.5] [e2431.6] [e2427.1] [e2427.0] [e2464.4]
  6. Proteogenomic LUAD studies repeatedly nominate pathway-state vulnerabilities downstream of tumor suppressor loss, including PP subtype CDK/glycolysis states, STK11-KEAP1 metabolic-redox programs, and STK11-AMPK-HNF1A-FGL1 mTOR-linked immune evasion.   7. This law was logically inferred through induction from studies showing that negative tumors repeatedly converge on the same downstream pathways even when the causal lesions differ.

Statement 1: Regulatory-equivalence law

Statement: When coding oncogene activation is absent, LUAD can restore oncogenic output through cis-regulatory or 3D-genome rewiring that elevates oncogene or pathway-node expression; in such tumors, the functional driver is the regulatory architecture rather than a coding mutation.

Domain/Scope: Applies to no-known-actionable-driver or non-CAGA LUAD studied with WGS, enhancer mapping, methylation profiling, Hi-C, long-read sequencing, or integrated cis-structural analysis. Best supported in tumors with outlier expression linked to structural variants or altered enhancer states.

Special Cases

  1. Regulatory events can activate canonical oncogenes such as ERBB2 or MYC without requiring coding mutation or high-level gene-body amplification.
  2. Some regulatory events are silencing rather than activating, as in MAML2-associated suppressive programs, but still function as driver-replacement mechanisms by rewiring lineage or signaling output.
  3. Standard short-read clinical testing may miss many of these events.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Non-CAGA LUAD can overexpress ERBB2 through super-enhancer formation plus structural rearrangement, including an inversion near HNF1B; CRISPR modeling increased HER2 expression and PDXs showed sensitivity to HER2-directed agents. [e2380.0] [e2380.1] [e2380.2]
  2. MYC-LASE is a lung adenocarcinoma-specific super-enhancer amplicon that physically contacts the MYC promoter; CRISPRi or deletion of the e3 constituent enhancer lowers MYC expression and suppresses anchorage-independent and clonogenic growth. [e2455.0] [e2455.2] [e2481.0]
  3. MAML2-centered non-CAGA LUAD shows ablated H3K27ac enhancer activity, increased methylation, and reduced MAML2 expression linked to Notch/Wnt-related downstream programs, indicating a regulatory rather than coding driver architecture. [e2460.0] [e2460.1] [e2474.0]
  4. CESAM identifies hidden cis-regulatory drivers via breakpoint-expression coupling and validates enhancer-hijacking logic in cancer, providing a mechanistic framework transferable to driver-negative LUAD even when examples in the study are outside LUAD. [e2450.2] [e2450.1]
  5. Review-level evidence on structural variation and the 3D genome argues that tissue-specific enhancer context, ecDNA, and neo-TAD logic are plausible hidden-driver mechanisms when canonical coding lesions are absent. [e2442.0] [e2471.0]
  6. This law was logically inferred through abduction from recurring observations that outlier oncogene expression and tumor fitness can be causally linked to enhancer or structural architecture in tumors without coding activation.

Statement 2: Ecosystem-equivalence law

Statement: A subset of driver-negative LUAD sustains malignant progression through ecosystem modules in which stromal, vascular, immune, or lineage-transition programs functionally replace a dominant tumor-cell oncogene by stabilizing survival, invasion, or immune escape.

Domain/Scope: Applies to transcriptomic, proteogenomic, single-cell, and spatially resolved studies of LUAD and never-smoker LUAD where canonical drivers are absent, sparse, or non-explanatory relative to phenotype. Strongest for cohorts explicitly showing CAF, endothelial, fibroblast, or immune-state dominance.

Special Cases

  1. These ecosystem modules can coexist with weak or secondary tumor-intrinsic genomic lesions.
  2. Some modules are progression-specific and may be strongest in early lesions or subsolid nodules rather than established advanced carcinomas.
  3. Immune-hot and immune-cold ecosystem modules can both occur in driver-poor disease, but imply opposite therapeutic logic.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Angiogenesis-high EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD is enriched for angiogenic ligands, TGF-beta/cell-migration signaling, CAFs, immune exclusion, and poor prognosis, indicating a stromal-vascular ecosystem state rather than a hidden single mutation. [e2408.3]
  2. Immune-high EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD is defined by TCR signaling, TLS-like features, B/T-cell infiltration, and checkpoint expression, showing that an ecosystem state can dominate prognosis and candidate therapy selection. [e2408.2] [e2438.1]
  3. FeSig-high EGFR-WT LUAD links ferroptosis resistance to fibroblast-rich microenvironments, stronger fibroblast-tumor communication, IGF-related signaling, and in vivo sensitivity to 6-mercaptopurine. [e2420.0]
  4. THBS2-positive CAFs and COL6A3-positive fibroblast-associated states were identified as progression-linked stromal modules in LUAD-related disease, supporting fibroblast-dominant ecosystem control. [e2388.4] [e2388.3]
  5. The chaotic never-smoker LUAD subtype is characterized by EMT, CAF enrichment, mixed-lineage features, and lack of enriched canonical drivers, supporting an ecosystem-plus-lineage replacement mode. [e2389.0] [e2389.2]
  6. Early LUAD progression in never-smokers and in SSN-associated disease was linked to angiogenesis, matrix CAF programs, ER stress, and cholesterol/PCSK9 biology, indicating that progression can be sustained by ecosystem and proteostasis modules without requiring a newly acquired canonical oncogene. [e2417.0] [e2458.0] [e2482.0] [e2482.1]
  7. This law was logically inferred through induction from repeated observations that microenvironmental states predict prognosis and therapeutic hypotheses even when canonical driver assignment is absent or non-explanatory.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If broad-negative LUADs are grouped by replacement layer rather than by histology alone, pathway-equivalence tumors will be enriched for pERK/pAKT or equivalent signaling activity, regulatory-equivalence tumors for enhancer/SV-linked outlier expression, and ecosystem-equivalence tumors for strong stromal or immune-state signatures.
  2. If driver-negative LUAD organoids are grown with and without matched stromal or immune components, ecosystem-equivalence tumors will lose a larger fraction of their fitness in co-culture perturbation experiments than pathway-equivalence tumors.
  3. If long-read sequencing and enhancer profiling are added to broad-negative tumors with outlier ERBB2, MYC, or MAML2-axis expression, a high fraction will show structural or cis-regulatory abnormalities rather than unexplained RNA overexpression.
  4. If suppressor-loss pathway-equivalence tumors are treated by signaling-node combinations (for example SHP2 plus AKT or MEK plus PI3K) rather than monotherapy, synthetic efficacy will be greater than in canonical single-oncogene-driven tumors.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If ecosystem-equivalence states truly replace dominant oncogenes in some tumors, removing specific stromal or immune niches in vivo will produce regressions comparable to direct kinase inhibition in canonical driver-positive LUAD.
  2. If regulatory-equivalence is common, a substantial fraction of broad-negative LUAD will prove to be addicted to enhancer transcriptional machinery, making BET/CDK7/Mediator-axis blockade unusually effective in selected negative tumors.
  3. If replacement layers are evolutionarily ordered, some tumors will transition from regulatory-equivalence or pathway-equivalence to ecosystem-equivalence during progression or therapy pressure, and longitudinal sampling will show corresponding shifts in dependency.
  4. If lineage-plasticity is itself a replacement layer, forced restoration of lineage-fidelity regulators such as NKX2-1- or alveolar-state programs will reduce fitness in selected driver-negative subtypes even without directly targeting proliferation pathways.

Negative Experiments

  1. Profile a broad-negative cohort for phosphoproteomics, enhancer architecture, and spatial TME states. If most tumors cannot be assigned to any replacement layer or show no coherence within layers, the theory is weakened.
  2. Test whether regulatory-equivalence tumors actually depend on the nominated regulatory element or architecture. If enhancer/SV-linked outlier expression is not functionally required, the regulatory-equivalence law fails.
  3. Disrupt stromal or immune modules in ecosystem-dominant models. If tumor fitness is unchanged, the claim that ecosystem modules can replace missing dominant oncogenes is undermined.
  4. Directly compare therapy response between pathway-equivalence and canonical oncogene-positive models under matched pathway blockade. If response logic is not convergent, the equivalence framing may be too strong.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some papers describing driver-negative or broad-negative LUAD remain purely descriptive and do not establish whether the observed subtype states are causal replacements or merely correlates. [e2407.0] [e2416.0] [e2452.0] [e2473.0]
