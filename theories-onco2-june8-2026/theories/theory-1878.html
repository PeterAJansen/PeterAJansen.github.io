Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1878

Name: Convergent Pathway-State Theory of Oncogene-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: A large fraction of apparently oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinoma is not truly driverless; instead, these tumors converge on a limited set of activated signaling states through noncanonical mechanisms that are often missed by hotspot-centric genomics. The dominant recurrent states are dual RAS/PI3K activation via tumor-suppressor co-loss, ERBB activation via ligand presentation/fusion, and proteomic MAPK/mTOR activation that can exceed what is explained by canonical DNA lesions. Thus, the biologically relevant unit is often a pathway-activated state, not a single mutated oncogene.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Convergent-pathway substitution law

Statement: In LUAD lacking canonical RTK/RAS/RAF oncogene lesions, tumorigenesis can be sustained by combinations of non-oncogene alterations that recreate the same downstream signaling outputs, especially RAS/MAPK and PI3K/AKT/mTOR activation.

Domain/Scope: Applies to lung adenocarcinomas classified as oncogene-negative or RTK/RAS/RAF-pathway-negative by broad genomic assays, and to related preclinical models of oncogene-negative LUAD. Best supported in tumors lacking known activating EGFR/KRAS/BRAF/ALK/ROS1/RET/MET-class lesions at initial testing, then profiled by WES/WGS/RNA-seq and/or proteogenomics.

Special Cases

  1. A substantial subset of initially negative tumors are later rescued by deeper sequencing as rare canonical or near-canonical drivers; this law applies most strongly to the residual negative set after broad testing.
  2. The exact substituting mechanism differs across tumors: tumor-suppressor co-loss, noncanonical rare coding mutations, exon skipping, focal amplification, or ligand-driven signaling.
  3. Not all negative tumors converge on the same pathway; some are dominated by immune, metabolic, or lineage-state programs instead.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Oncogene-negative LUAD was shown to contain an explicit Onc-negative RAS/PI3K subtype, with pERK and pAKT positivity in human tumors and in mouse models driven by combinatorial Nf1/Rasa1/Pten loss, indicating that canonical downstream pathways can be recreated without canonical oncogene activation. [e2399.0] [e2392.0] [e2392.2] [e2399.2] [e2399.1] [e2392.1]
  2. Concurrent RASA1 and NF1 loss defined a smoking-associated NSCLC subset lacking other known mitogenic drivers and produced strong RAS/MAPK and PI3K/AKT activation with MEK-inhibitor sensitivity, showing that co-loss of RasGAPs can substitute for classical driver oncogenes. [e2422.0]
  3. In TCGA-style oncogene-negative LUAD, hidden RTK/RAS/RAF-pathway lesions were enriched, including MET and ERBB2 focal amplification, NF1 loss, and RIT1 mutation, implying that many negative tumors remain pathway-driven despite lacking the standard activating event set. [e2431.0] [e2431.1] [e2431.2] [e2431.3] [e2431.4]
  4. Proteomic RPPA analyses showed significant MAPK activation in many KRAS-wild-type tumors and significant mTOR activation across tumors not fully explained by known genomic alterations, directly supporting the idea that pathway state outruns mutation annotation. [e2431.5] [e2431.6]
  5. In East Asian never-smoker LUAD, phosphoproteomics showed heterogeneous MAPK activity across EGFR-wild-type and EGFR-mutant tumors, including high-MAPK EGFR-wild-type cases and low-MAPK EGFR-mutant/TP53-mutant cases, demonstrating that genotype alone incompletely specifies pathway activity. [e2427.0] [e2427.1]
  6. Whole-genome reanalysis of RTK/RAS/RAF-pathway-negative LUAD rescued many initially negative tumors by discovering KRAS hotspot mutations, ARAF/EGFR/MAPK1/SOS1 amplifications, NF1/RASA1 deletions, and NRG1 fusions, while the remaining negative tumors still showed recurrent tumor-suppressor loss and structural complexity. [e2383.0]
  7. The review of RPA-negative LUAD explicitly concludes that some WES-negative tumors are later found to harbor RTK/RAS/RAF-pathway lesions by WGS, while the residual group likely depends on noncanonical drivers, downstream amplifications, or epigenetic pathway activation. [e2390.0] [e2390.1] [e2390.2]
  8. Distinct patterns study of oncogene-negative LUAD found enrichment of rare pathway members such as SOS1, RASA1, VAV1, ARHGAP35, and NF1, implying that pathway membership rather than canonical gene identity is the key organizing principle of some negative tumors. [e2391.0] [e2391.1] [e2391.2] [e2391.3] [e2391.4]
  9. RIT1 mutations occurred exclusively in the oncogene-negative subset and experimentally activated MEK/ERK and PI3K/AKT signaling, providing a direct example of a rare coding substitute for canonical RTK/RAS/RAF oncogenes. [e2381.0] [e2431.4]
  10. MET exon 14 skipping, identified predominantly in driver-unknown tumors, is another example of a hidden pathway-restoring lesion that substitutes for missing canonical RTK activation. [e2409.4] [e2405.0] [e2431.1]
  11. This statement is additionally supported by induction/abduction across the above findings: multiple orthogonal lesion classes in negative LUAD repeatedly converge on the same signaling outputs (RAS/MAPK, PI3K/AKT/mTOR, ERBB), implying that pathway state is a better explanatory variable than lesion class alone.

Statement 1: Proteomic primacy law

Statement: In oncogene-negative LUAD, phosphoproteomic and protein-state readouts are often more faithful indicators of the operative driver than DNA genotype alone, because actionable signaling may arise from noncanonical lesions, regulatory rearrangements, or combined suppressor loss that are incompletely captured by standard driver panels.

Domain/Scope: Applies to LUAD cohorts with proteomic or phosphoproteomic data and to clinically negative tumors evaluated by deeper multi-omics. Especially relevant in EGFR/ALK-negative never-smoker LUAD, RTK/RAS/RAF-negative LUAD, and tumors lacking currently actionable drivers on standard testing.

Special Cases

  1. This law is strongest for pathway-state therapeutics and weaker for tumors truly driven by a single highly penetrant canonical kinase lesion.
  2. It does not imply DNA is uninformative; rather, it implies DNA alone is often insufficient in the negative subset.
  3. Some pathway states remain correlative until functionally perturbed.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. RPPA data showed MAPK and mTOR activity in many tumors that could not be fully accounted for by known genomic alterations, directly motivating proteomic interpretation of apparently driver-negative disease. [e2431.5] [e2431.6]
  2. In East Asian never-smoker LUAD, a co-phosphorylation MAPK signature stratified tumors more precisely than EGFR genotype alone, with EGFR-wild-type high-MAPK tumors and EGFR-mutant low-MAPK tumors. [e2427.0] [e2427.1]
  3. The LSCC CDK4/6 biomarker study showed that downstream RB phosphorylation predicted inhibitor response better than mutation status alone, providing a cross-lung-cancer principle that pathway-state biomarkers can outperform genotype-only biomarkers. [e2484.4]
  4. In LSCC, EGFR pathway activity correlated with ligand abundance rather than EGFR amplification alone, reinforcing that pathway activity can be driven by protein-context variables not captured by DNA alteration status. [e2484.1] [e2484.5]
  5. Proteogenomic subtype analyses of LUAD identified PP, PI, and TRU states with distinct kinase activities and therapeutic hypotheses, and explicitly noted that many LUADs without actionable genomic drivers still possess proteome-defined signaling dependencies. [e2374.0] [e2480.0] [e2480.1] [e2454.1] [e2473.0] [e2452.0] [e2433.0]
  6. In EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD, four proteogenomic subtypes (P, I, A, M) were defined by pathway state, prognosis, and candidate therapies rather than by a single hidden mutation, showing practical subclassification of clinically negative disease through proteogenomics. [e2408.0] [e2408.1] [e2408.2] [e2408.3] [e2408.4]
  7. The STK11-AMPK-HNF1A-FGL1/CPS1 axis in proteome subtype 4 illustrates a driver-relevant state discovered primarily at protein level, with genotype alone insufficient because some STK11-wild-type tumors also showed low STK11 expression and downstream program activation. [e2453.3] [e2453.6]
  8. SHP2/PTPN11 and SOS1 phosphosite outliers in LUAD reveal actionable signaling nodes downstream of EGFR, ALK, and KRAS that are more visible in phosphoproteomics than in RNA or total protein abundance. [e2433.2] [e2433.3]
  9. ARHGEF5 phosphosite activation in non-smoking LUAD represents a hidden pathway state inferred at the phosphosite level rather than from a canonical coding lesion, supporting the broader principle that phosphoproteomics reveals non-genomic drivers. [e2433.4]
  10. This law is further supported by induction from multiple studies showing that clinically negative LUAD can be partitioned into reproducible transcriptomic/proteomic states with therapeutic implications even when no single genomic driver is identified.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If broad driver-negative LUAD cohorts are stratified by phosphoproteomic signatures rather than DNA status, at least three reproducible actionable classes will emerge: dual RAS/PI3K-active, ERBB-ligand-active, and proliferation/CDK-active tumors.
  2. If pERK, pAKT, pRB, and pERBB3 IHC panels are applied to clinically negative LUAD, these markers will predict deeper hidden-driver classes better than hotspot mutation panels alone.
  3. If residual RPA-negative tumors are subjected to long-read WGS plus phosphoproteomics, a substantial subset will be reassigned to noncanonical structural, splicing, or suppressor-loss pathway classes rather than remaining biologically unclassified.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If pathway-state classification is used prospectively in oncogene-negative LUAD, treatment matching by phosphoproteomic state may outperform treatment matching by expanded DNA panel alone in response rate and progression-free survival.
  2. A residual subset of phospho-MAPK-high, genomically negative LUAD may prove to be driven by allele-specific expression or 3D-genome rewiring of pathway regulators rather than coding events.
  3. Some clinically negative LUADs may alternate between pathway states over time, such that serial phosphoproteomics will reveal state-switching under therapy even without acquisition of new canonical driver mutations.

Negative Experiments

  1. Show in a large oncogene-negative LUAD cohort that phosphoproteomic pathway activity is fully explained by known genomic alterations and adds no predictive value beyond DNA; this would undermine the proteomic primacy law.
  2. Demonstrate that combined suppressor-loss models of oncogene-negative LUAD fail to activate the predicted downstream pathways or fail to phenocopy human negative tumors.
  3. Find that deeper long-read/WGS analyses do not reduce the unclassified fraction and that residual tumors do not cluster by pathway state; this would argue against convergence.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Purely immune-stratified driver-negative prognostic groups can be described without identifying a hidden oncogenic pathway, suggesting some negative tumors may be dominated by ecosystem state rather than tumor-intrinsic signaling convergence. [e2407.0] [e2416.0]
  2. Some lineage-plastic or evolution-dominated tumors, such as piano never-smoker cancers or histologic progression continua, may be governed more by cell-state dynamics than by the pathway substitution model. [e2406.0] [e2413.0] [e2388.1]
