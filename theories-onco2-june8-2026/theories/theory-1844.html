Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1844

Name: Convergent Output Theory of Driver-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: Most driver-negative LUADs converge onto a small number of phenotypic output states even when their initiating alterations differ. The dominant outputs are: (1) RTK/RAS/PI3K phospho-active growth signaling, (2) cell-cycle/checkpoint and DNA-repair addiction, (3) immune-evasive or stromal-excluded ecosystem states, and (4) lineage/metabolic reprogramming states such as mucinous-gastric, alveolar-intermediate, ferroptosis-resistant, or liver-like STK11/FGL1 programs. Hidden drivers matter clinically insofar as they generate one of these outputs. Therefore, subclassification of unresolved LUAD should prioritize measuring output states rather than only searching for ever-rarer initiating lesions.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Diverse hidden lesions converge onto a limited set of output states

Statement: Despite genetic heterogeneity, driver-negative LUAD recurrently converges onto a small set of functional output states—phospho-active RTK/RAS/PI3K signaling, checkpoint/cell-cycle and DNA-repair dependency, immune-excluded or immune-hot stromal ecosystems, and lineage/metabolic reprogramming—and these outputs are more reproducible across cohorts than any single rare hidden lesion.

Domain/Scope: Applies to unresolved or weakly explained LUAD after standard driver assessment, especially cohorts with multi-omic, transcriptomic, proteomic, spatial, or single-cell measurements. Most applicable to primary adenocarcinomas, including never-smoker and EGFR/ALK-wild-type cohorts, but also relevant to smoking-associated NSCLC when pathway output dominates over individual rare lesions. Not intended for tumors already cleanly explained by a single strong canonical oncogene with concordant downstream state.

Special Cases

  1. A tumor may occupy more than one output state simultaneously, for example RTK/RAS phospho-activation plus stromal exclusion or metabolic rewiring plus immune coldness.
  2. Some outputs are assay-dependent; phospho-active states require fresh or high-quality proteomic material, whereas ecosystem and lineage states can often be seen in RNA or spatial data.
  3. High-TMB and highly unstable tumors may show noisy initiating lesions, making output-state measurement especially advantageous.

Statement 1: Therapy should be matched to output state before hidden lesion identity is complete

Statement: In driver-negative LUAD, therapeutic prioritization should be guided first by measurable output state—growth-signal phospho-activation, checkpoint/cell-cycle addiction, immune/stromal ecosystem state, or lineage/metabolic reprogramming—because these states predict vulnerability more directly than the exact hidden lesion and often remain targetable even when the initiating lesion is unknown or undruggable.

Domain/Scope: Applies to biomarker-guided therapeutic hypothesis generation in unresolved LUAD and adjacent NSCLC contexts where canonical actionable drivers are absent or insufficient to explain phenotype. Best suited to preclinical-to-translational settings and not yet a validated standard-of-care rule.

Special Cases

  1. When a directly druggable kinase fusion or canonical lesion is identified, lesion-matched therapy still has priority unless output-state data argue strongly for immediate combination therapy or likely resistance.
  2. Immune output states may not translate cleanly across histologies or treatment lines, especially in previously treated tumors.
  3. Output-state-directed treatment may fail if the state is reactive rather than causal.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Across independent unresolved LUAD cohorts, clustering on phosphoprotein, immune/stromal, and lineage/metabolic output features will be more reproducible than clustering on rare hidden genomic lesions alone.
  2. Response to pathway-targeted perturbation in organoids or ex vivo slices will correlate more strongly with output-state assignment than with the presence of any one rare hidden lesion.
  3. Tumors with immune-cold STK11/FGL1 or angiogenesis-high/CAF-rich outputs will show inferior monotherapy ICI response even when TMB or a rare targetable lesion is present.
  4. A subset of genomically heterogeneous mucinous or EGFR-wild-type tumors will share convergent ERK/AKT or metabolic output states and therefore share drug sensitivity despite different initiating lesions.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. An output-state atlas of unresolved LUAD built from joint phosphoproteomics, spatial transcriptomics, and perturbation will reveal a small number of recurrent therapeutic states that cut across smoking status, histology, and hidden-lesion identity.
  2. Some tumors currently considered resistant to targeted therapy because of absent actionable lesions will respond if assigned by output-state biomarkers alone, without complete genomic explanation.
  3. Transition between output states may explain early resistance more often than acquisition of a new genomic driver in a subset of driver-negative LUAD.

Negative Experiments

  1. Test whether output-state clustering is actually more reproducible across cohorts than lesion-based clustering. If not, the theory's central organizing principle is weakened.
  2. Directly compare whether ex vivo drug response correlates better with hidden lesion identity or with output-state assignment. If lesion identity consistently outperforms output states, the theory is weakened.
  3. Evaluate whether tumors assigned to the same output state but with different hidden lesions truly share vulnerabilities. If they do not, convergence is overstated.
  4. If spatial and proteomic outputs are mostly reactive consequences rather than stable tumor-intrinsic states, output-first therapy should fail prospectively.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some clinically observed responses are still clearly lesion-matched, such as ROS1- and ARAF-driven cases, and these can dominate over broader output-state logic in practice. [e1936.1] [e2001.1] [e1963.0]
