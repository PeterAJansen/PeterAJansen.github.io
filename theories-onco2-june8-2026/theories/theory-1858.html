Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1858

Name: Driver-Negative LUAD as State-Specific Ecologies Rather Than Hidden Single Lesions

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: A substantial subset of driver-negative lung adenocarcinomas is best explained not by a single missed oncogene, but by stable tumor-cell-state and ecosystem programs. In this view, some tumors are maintained by lineage plasticity, immune ecology, fibroblast-dependent ferroptosis resistance, angiogenic stroma, or non-genetic histologic progression states. Genomic lesions may still be present, but the dominant explanatory and therapeutic unit is the ecological state rather than the discrete mutation.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: State-dominance law

Statement: In a substantial subset of canonical-driver-depleted LUAD, prognosis and biological behavior are explained more strongly by stable transcriptional/proteomic state programs than by the presence or absence of an identifiable single oncogenic lesion.

Domain/Scope: Applies to resected or treatment-naive LUAD cohorts in which transcriptomic, proteomic, or spatial-state subtypes were defined, especially never-smoker, EGFR/ALK-wild-type, EGFR-wild-type, or histology-progressing cohorts. It does not claim that lesions are irrelevant, only that state can become the dominant explanatory variable.

Special Cases

  1. Some state programs are still rooted in a canonical oncogene but remain better predicted by the downstream ecological state than by mutation alone.
  2. In deeply metastatic or therapy-exposed tumors, treatment history may reshape state structure and reduce the explanatory strength of baseline state assignments.
  3. This law is less applicable to rare, strongly kinase-addicted tumors such as NTRK1- or RET-fusion cases where the lesion remains dominant.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. In EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD, integrated transcriptome/proteome profiling resolved four subtypes—proliferation, immune, angiogenesis, and metabolism—with distinct prognosis and vulnerabilities, despite no single alternative driver explaining the entire cohort. [e1857.0] [e1861.5] [e1857.4]
  2. In never-smoker NS-LUAD, the chaotic, proliferative, and steady subtypes predicted survival and immune behavior; the chaotic subtype had poor outcome without significant enrichment of canonical driver mutations or fusions. [e1850.0] [e1850.1] [e1850.2] [e1850.3]
  3. In EGFR-wild-type LUAD, the FeSig-high versus FeSig-low split predicted survival, fibroblast content, ferroptosis resistance, and drug sensitivity, indicating a strong biology organized by metabolic/TME state rather than by a canonical oncogene label. [e1904.0] [e1904.4] [e1904.5] [e1904.6] [e1904.1] [e1904.2] [e1904.3]
  4. The lepidic-to-solid transition model found that histologic progression and poor prognosis were associated with transcriptional and spatial-state reprogramming rather than recurrent pattern-specific genetic lesions. [e1895.0] [e1895.1] [e1895.2]
  5. Review-level synthesis explicitly states that LUAD progression and spatial heterogeneity can be driven primarily by non-genetic transcriptional state shifts and TME reprogramming rather than acquisition of new driver mutations. [e1844.0] [e1844.3]

Statement 1: Fibroblast-ferroptosis ecological law

Statement: A recurrent EGFR-wild-type/driver-poor LUAD ecology is organized by fibroblast-rich stroma, intercellular ligand communication, and ferroptosis resistance genes; in this ecology, stromal support and ferroptosis suppression jointly function as the effective tumor-maintaining mechanism.

Domain/Scope: Applies to EGFR-wild-type LUAD and especially FeSig-high tumors in bulk and single-cell analyses, with strongest evidence in cohorts analyzed for fibroblast infiltration, ligand-receptor interaction, and ferroptosis-gene expression. It is not yet established as universal across all driver-negative LUAD.

Special Cases

  1. The ecology may coexist with other oncogenic lesions; EGFR-wild-type is not equivalent to fully driver-negative.
  2. The specific mediating ligand pathways remain partly inferred and not all cohorts validated the same ligand-target signature.
  3. Fibroblast abundance may be necessary but not sufficient; the tumor cell must also express a ferroptosis-resistant program.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. FeSig-high EGFR-wild-type LUAD had worse survival and was defined by elevated DDIT4, NOX4, and GCLC, each of which promoted survival/migration and reduced ferroptosis sensitivity in cell assays. [e1904.0] [e1904.1] [e1904.2] [e1904.3]
  2. Across multiple datasets, fibroblast abundance correlated positively with FeSig, fibroblast marker genes increased with FeSig, and histology plus alpha-SMA staining confirmed more fibroblasts in FeSig-high tumors. [e1904.4] [e1904.0]
  3. Single-cell analysis showed a higher fibroblast fraction in FeSig-high tumors and stronger fibroblast-epithelial communication by CellChat, supporting fibroblasts as active organizers rather than passive bystanders. [e1904.4] [e1904.5]
  4. NicheNet/CellChat analysis implicated IGF-binding-related signaling as a candidate fibroblast-to-tumor communication axis, and the derived communication score correlated negatively with ferroptosis score in several cohorts. [e1904.5]
  5. 6-mercaptopurine preferentially suppressed FeSig-high A549 xenografts and reduced fibroblast infiltration, consistent with the idea that this ecological state is therapeutically targetable at the level of state support rather than classical mutation targeting. [e1904.6] [e1904.4] [e1904.0]

Statement 2: Lineage-plasticity initiation law

Statement: Some driver-negative-appearing LUADs originate from or are maintained by transitional lineage states—rather than by a dominant canonical oncogene—with KRT8-positive alveolar intermediates, NKX2-1-loss gastric-like states, and quiescence-exit/stemness programs serving as recurrent examples.

Domain/Scope: Applies to early-stage, never-smoker, mucinous, or lineage-reprogrammed LUAD contexts where single-cell, spatial, or lineage-factor evidence is available. It is strongest for early progression and mucinous/gastric-like biology, and weaker for advanced metastatic disease.

Special Cases

  1. These transitional states may later acquire canonical or noncanonical genomic drivers; the law concerns initiation/maintenance state, not exclusivity of mutation.
  2. Not all KRT8-positive or NKX2-1-low states are obligatorily tumorigenic.
  3. The exact causal sequence among injury, cell-state change, and mutation acquisition remains unresolved in several models.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. KRT8-positive alveolar intermediate cells (KACs) were enriched near tumors, correlated with KRAS-like programs, were less differentiated, increased along normal-AAH-LUAD progression, and were associated with poorer survival, supporting a transitional precursor-like state linked to tumorigenesis. [e1898.1] [e1898.2] [e1898.3] [e1898.4] [e1844.1]
  2. NKX2-1-negative LUAD adopted a gastric differentiation program in mouse and human mucinous tumors, indicating that lineage transcription factor loss can generate a tumor-maintaining identity shift rather than simply removing a marker. [e1889.0] [e1889.2] [e1909.0] [e1909.1] [e1909.2]
  3. The piano subtype in never-smoker lung cancer was genomically quiet, long-telomere, stemness-like, and often lacked known recurrent drivers; the authors explicitly hypothesized quiescence exit through ARID1A, NOTCH1, RET, KRAS, UBA1, or related regulators. [e1887.0] [e1887.2] [e1887.3] [e1887.4]
  4. Review and precursor-state studies argue that some progression states in LUAD are non-genetic and plastic, supporting the concept that lineage state itself can be a principal explanatory variable in lesions that seem genomically underdriven. [e1844.0] [e1844.3] [e1890.1]

Statement 3: Microenvironmental progression law

Statement: In early or canonical-driver-depleted LUAD, angiogenic and immune-remodeling ecosystems can act as progression engines independently of new recurrent driver mutation acquisition; tip endothelial cells, mCAFs, and immune-excluded solid-pattern niches are recurrent manifestations of this principle.

Domain/Scope: Applies primarily to precursor lesions, minimally invasive disease, and early-stage resected LUAD analyzed by bulk, single-cell, or spatial methods. It is a progression law, not necessarily an initiation law for all advanced tumors.

Special Cases

  1. These ecosystems may cooperate with canonical oncogenes rather than replace them entirely.
  2. Most evidence is spatial and associative rather than perturbational, so causal assignment remains provisional.
  3. The law is strongest in early progression states and may not extrapolate linearly to metastatic disease.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. AIS3 was associated with tip endothelial cells and matrix CAFs, with endothelial and stromal activity rising in the late AIS state and spatial enrichment of endothelial cells in invasive regions, supporting an angiogenic-stromal progression engine. [e1890.1] [e1890.2]
  2. MIA paired noninvasive and invasive analyses showed that endothelial-rich invasive programs emerge without needing a new recurrent mutational explanation, indicating progression through stromal/vascular remodeling. [e1890.2]
  3. Solid-pattern/L2S-high LUAD had immune exclusion, peripheral immune localization, and suppressive Treg/checkpoint signatures in tumor cores, indicating that ecological remodeling can define the aggressive state even when no pattern-specific recurrent genetic lesion is found. [e1895.0] [e1895.2]
  4. The general review-level synthesis states that LUAD spatial heterogeneity and progression can be driven primarily by TME reprogramming rather than new driver mutations. [e1844.0] [e1844.3]

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. In EGFR-wild-type or canonical-driver-depleted LUAD, fibroblast-rich tumors with high DDIT4/NOX4/GCLC will be more sensitive to combined ferroptosis induction plus CAF-disrupting therapy than to either alone.
  2. Single-cell multiome profiling of genomically quiet 'piano-like' or chaotic never-smoker LUAD will reveal recurrent lineage-transition programs even when no shared canonical driver lesion is found.
  3. Early-stage L2S-high or AIS3-like lesions will show greater benefit from anti-angiogenic or vessel-normalization interventions than L2S-low/lepidic lesions.
  4. KAC-high premalignant or tumor-adjacent lung tissue will carry higher short-term risk of LUAD emergence than KAC-low tissue independent of smoking history.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. A subset of driver-negative LUAD will depend on reversible ecologic circuits that disappear ex vivo, causing many current organoid models to miss the true driver state unless stromal and immune compartments are retained.
  2. There exists a lineage-plasticity subtype in which restoring NKX2-1-like pulmonary identity will suppress tumor fitness more effectively than direct pathway inhibition.
  3. Some piano/quiet tumors will prove to arise from stem-cell niche dysregulation rather than tumor-cell-autonomous mutation burden, making local niche-targeted prevention feasible.
  4. Solid immune-excluded progression states may be inducible without new genomic change by biomechanical or vascular cues alone, implying that some recurrence could be prevented by early ecological intervention.

Negative Experiments

  1. If fibroblast depletion or blockade of candidate fibroblast-tumor ligand axes does not change ferroptosis sensitivity in FeSig-high models, the fibroblast-ferroptosis ecology branch is weakened.
  2. If KAC-high or NKX2-1-low transitional states fail to predict progression or dependency after controlling for mutation burden and histology, the lineage-plasticity theory is weakened.
  3. If anti-angiogenic or vessel-normalization interventions do not preferentially affect AIS3/MIA endothelial-rich lesions in relevant models, the microenvironmental progression law is weakened.
  4. If integrated state classifiers do not outperform comprehensive genomic profiling in prognosis or response prediction for canonical-driver-depleted cohorts, the state-dominance law is weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Rare but clearly targetable kinase-fusion cases such as RET-, NTRK1-, or NRG1-driven tumors are better explained by lesion-dominant models than by state-dominant ecology. [e1885.0] [e1899.0] [e1915.2] [e1876.0]
  2. Residual RPA(-)G tumors with ILF2 promoter mutation, KEAP1/STK11 deletions, or complex structural variants may require a genomic architecture explanation rather than a purely ecological one. [e1864.0] [e1864.1] [e1864.2] [e1864.3] [e1864.4] [e1864.5]
