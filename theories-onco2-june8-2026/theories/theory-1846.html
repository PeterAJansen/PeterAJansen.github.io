Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1846

Name: Proteogenomic Output Theory of Hidden Oncogenic Dependence

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: In oncogene-negative LUAD, the decisive hidden driver often lies downstream of DNA sequence: phosphoproteomic output, ligand presentation, and regulatory architecture determine oncogenic dependence more faithfully than mutation status alone. This theory proposes that unresolved LUAD should be interrogated as an output system, where activated receptor signaling, kinase-node phosphorylation, enhancer hijacking, or ligand-receptor loops can reveal targetable dependence even when canonical genomic lesions are absent or insufficient.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Protein/phosphoprotein outliers reveal actionable hidden dependence missed by genotype alone

Statement: In LUAD, when canonical drivers are absent or incomplete, extreme phosphosite or protein outliers identify active oncogenic dependencies that may not be inferable from RNA abundance or routine genomic testing; therefore, pathway activation should be measured at the phosphoproteomic level in unresolved tumors.

Domain/Scope: Applies to treatment-naive LUAD profiled by deep proteomics or phosphoproteomics, especially tumors lacking clear actionable mutations or showing ambiguous genomic findings. Most relevant for signaling nodes downstream of RTKs, RAS, oxidative stress, and immune suppression programs.

Special Cases

  1. Some phosphosite outliers will still reflect known canonical drivers rather than hidden negative-disease biology.
  2. Phosphosite activation may require high-quality fresh-frozen or optimized proteomic material and may be harder to measure in small clinical biopsies.
  3. Not every activated phosphosite is therapeutically causal.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Proteogenomic characterization of LUAD explicitly argues that many tumors lacking known or targetable mutations can still be understood through phosphoproteomic outliers and pathway states not captured by RNA or genomics alone. [e2023.0] [e1947.0]
  2. EGFR-mutant tumors showed highly significant PTPN11/SHP2 Y62 phosphorylation without corresponding RNA or protein abundance change, illustrating that activation state can be invisible at the transcript level. [e1947.7] [e2023.1]
  3. ALK fusion tumors were marked by a dramatic phosphosite signal at ALK Y1507, validated by tumor-specific IHC and absent in ROS1/RET fusion samples and normal tissues, showing how phosphoproteomics can capture fusion dependence directly. [e1947.5] [e1947.6]
  4. KRAS-mutant tumors showed SOS1 S1161 phosphosite outlier activity, interpreted as a marker of active KRAS signaling and a possible therapeutic dependency not reducible to mutation status alone. [e2023.2]
  5. KEAP1-mutant tumors showed reduced KEAP1 protein together with increased NFE2L2 phosphosites, showing that protein/phosphosite state can define oxidative-stress pathway activation more directly than DNA alone. [e1947.8] [e2023.4]
  6. STK11-mutant tumors displayed a proteomics-only neutrophil degranulation signature not detectable at RNA level, indicating hidden functional state can emerge only at the protein layer. [e1947.10]
  7. Strict never-smoker tumors showed ARHGEF5 phosphosite elevation and SRGAP1 suppression, implicating Rho GTPase activation as a hidden signaling process detectable proteomically rather than genomically. [e1947.9] [e2023.3]
  8. This law was induced from repeated observations that phosphoproteomic and proteomic activation signals disclose therapeutically relevant pathway states not evident from RNA or conventional mutation calls.

Statement 1: Ligand-regulatory activation is a recurrent hidden driver mode parallel to kinase mutation

Statement: A recurrent subset of oncogene-negative LUAD is driven by noncanonical activation modes that preserve wild-type signaling proteins but alter how they are presented or regulated, including ligand-tethering fusions, exon-skipping-mediated receptor stabilization, and structural/enhancer rewiring that overexpresses intact oncogenes. These mechanisms can generate durable oncogenic dependence without classic kinase-domain mutation.

Domain/Scope: Applies to genomically unresolved LUAD and related NSCLC in which signaling output is produced by fusion ligands, splice alterations, or cis-regulatory structural changes rather than classic activating point mutations. Especially relevant to mucinous, never-smoker, and non-CAGA subsets.

Special Cases

  1. Some cases in this class still coexist with canonical mutations or amplifications and are not purely noncanonical.
  2. Regulatory activation may not always increase canonical phosphosite markers at the expected residue.
  3. Not all structural or splicing events create actionable dependence.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. CD74-NRG1 fusions in driver-negative invasive mucinous adenocarcinoma create membrane-tethered or secreted ligand presentation that activates ERBB2/ERBB3 with downstream AKT/S6K and ERK signaling, demonstrating ligand-fusion-driven oncogenesis rather than kinase-domain mutation. [e1952.0] [e1985.1] [e1995.0] [e2035.0]
  2. MET exon 14 skipping deletes the juxtamembrane Cbl-binding region, reduces ubiquitination, delays receptor downregulation, and sustains ligand-dependent MAPK signaling, showing a splicing-mediated stabilization mechanism for oncogenic activation. [e1948.0] [e1932.2] [e1938.3]
  3. ERBB2/HER2 can be overexpressed in non-CAGA LUAD through structural-variant-associated super-enhancer formation, creating a wild-type driver-like state sensitive to poziotinib and trastuzumab deruxtecan in PDX models despite absence of canonical HER2 mutation. [e1928.0] [e1928.1] [e1928.2]
  4. EGFR exon 25/26 deletion is an activating structural alteration that would be missed by hotspot testing but causes EGFR and AKT phosphorylation, transformation, and erlotinib sensitivity. [e1981.4] [e2022.5]
  5. Rare EGFR extracellular-domain mutation M277E causes ligand-independent receptor activation and preclinical sensitivity to erlotinib or cetuximab, illustrating noncanonical receptor activation outside standard kinase-domain hotspots. [e1933.0]
  6. The concept of cis-regulatory hidden drivers is reinforced by pan-cancer CESAM analyses identifying enhancer hijacking and TAD-boundary disruption as oncogenic activation mechanisms, even though key exemplars are outside LUAD or in LUSC/CRC. [e1986.2] [e1986.0] [e1986.1] [e2029.0] [e2029.1]
  7. This law was induced from convergent evidence that driver-negative tumors repeatedly activate intact signaling proteins via altered presentation, turnover, or regulation instead of canonical catalytic mutation.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. In WGS/RNA-negative LUADs, phosphoproteomic outlier detection will identify a clinically actionable signaling node in a measurable minority of cases even when no additional DNA driver is found.
  2. Tumors with ligand-regulatory activation modes such as NRG1 fusion, MET exon 14 skipping, or ERBB2 enhancer activation will cluster together by downstream phosphoproteomic output more than by genomic alteration class.
  3. Clinical biopsies with high phosphosite activation but no canonical driver will show better treatment matching if assigned using output-layer biomarkers than by DNA-only review.
  4. Mucinous driver-negative tumors will be enriched for noncanonical activation modes involving ligand presentation or receptor regulation rather than for classic intracellular kinase hotspots.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Some fully unresolved driver-negative LUADs may depend on stromally supplied or microbiome-induced ligands, producing phosphoproteomic RTK activation without any tumor-cell genomic lesion in the receptor pathway.
  2. A low-input clinical phosphoproteomic panel could prospectively identify hidden dependencies and outperform broad DNA panels for therapy assignment in a subset of advanced unresolved LUAD.
  3. Enhancer-hijacking or ecDNA-mediated activation of intact wild-type oncogenes may explain a clinically meaningful fraction of WGS-negative LUAD once long-read and 3D-genome assays are applied prospectively.
  4. Negative tumors with proteomics-only neutrophil degranulation or Rho-GTPase states may require pathway therapy directed at microenvironmental effectors rather than the tumor cell alone.

Negative Experiments

  1. If phosphoproteomic outliers in unresolved LUAD do not reproducibly correspond to functional pathway dependence or therapeutic sensitivity, the output-layer component of the theory is weakened.
  2. If ligand-fusion, exon-skipping, and enhancer-activation mechanisms produce no shared downstream signaling logic and behave as unrelated special cases, the unified regulatory-activation law fails.
  3. If DNA-only profiling consistently predicts therapy response as well as or better than phosphoproteomic state in unresolved tumors, the central claim of output-layer primacy is undermined.
  4. If noncanonical regulatory activation proves too rare to matter clinically after prospective whole-genome and RNA testing, the second statement is overstated.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Several phosphoproteomic examples come from canonical driver-positive tumors and therefore demonstrate method value more directly than they demonstrate biology of truly driver-negative tumors. [e1947.5] [e1947.6] [e1947.7] [e2023.1] [e2023.2]
  2. The evidence base for direct therapeutic translation of phosphoproteomic output in driver-negative LUAD remains mostly preclinical or inferential. [e2023.0] [e1928.1] [e1948.0]
