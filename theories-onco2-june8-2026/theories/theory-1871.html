Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1871

Name: Proteomic Driver-State Theory of Truly Unresolved LUAD

Type: specific

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: A subset of oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinomas is not primarily driven by a single recurrent DNA lesion, but by stable, selectable signaling states that are best measured at the protein and phosphoprotein level. In this view, the causal unit is a pathway state maintained by combinations of weak genomic lesions, negative-regulator loss, lineage context, secretory programs, and post-transcriptional or post-translational regulation. The key implication is that some 'driver-negative' tumors are only unresolved in DNA space; in protein-state space they are organized into recurrent, therapeutically meaningful driver-equivalent classes.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Protein-state explanatory primacy in a subset of oncogene-negative LUAD

Statement: When canonical RTK/RAS/RAF DNA lesions are absent or insufficient to explain pathway output, recurrent and pathway-coherent protein/phosphosite outliers can serve as the nearest mechanistic explanatory unit for LUAD behavior. In these tumors, the best predictor of dependency is not mutation status alone but the presence of a stable activated phospho-network or suppressed negative-regulator protein state.

Domain/Scope: Applies to lung adenocarcinomas, especially residual oncogene-negative or genomically under-explained tumors after broad DNA/RNA profiling, including never-smoker subsets, suppressor-loss tumors, and proteogenomically profiled cohorts. Strongest where phosphoproteomics or proteomics reveals pathway activation not recoverable from DNA-only or RNA-only assays. Not intended for tumors already fully and parsimoniously explained by a dominant canonical driver such as EGFR mutation, ALK fusion, or KRAS hotspot mutation, except insofar as those tumors illustrate the general principle that protein state can diverge from genotype.

Special Cases

  1. Not every phospho-outlier is causal; some are downstream consequences of an upstream lesion.
  2. A protein-state signal should be treated as driver-equivalent only when it is recurrent, pathway-coherent, biologically stable, and linked to a vulnerability or functional consequence.
  3. Bulk proteomic signals can reflect stromal or immune admixture; tumor-cell-intrinsic causality requires orthogonal validation.
  4. Some examples arise in tumors with known genomic drivers, showing the principle of protein-state causality but not necessarily proving independence from DNA lesions.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. ALK-fusion-positive LUADs were not only identified by RNA/WGS fusion calling but also showed a striking fusion-specific phosphosite signature, especially ALK Y1507 phosphorylation, plus downstream phosphotyrosine signaling on SND1, HDLBP, and ARHGEF5, demonstrating that a driver can be most directly observed as a phospho-state. [e2220.0]
  2. PTPN11/SHP2 Y62 phosphorylation was highly elevated in EGFR-mutant tumors without corresponding change in PTPN11 RNA or total protein, and ALK-fusion tumors showed SHP2 Y546/Y584 outlier phosphorylation, showing that critical pathway activation states can be invisible to DNA/RNA/total-protein abundance measurements. [e2220.3]
  3. ARHGEF5 Y1370 phosphorylation with reduced SRGAP1 was identified as a noncanonical signaling state in strict never-smoker LUAD, strongest in ALK-fusion cases, and explicitly described as something DNA-only testing would miss because the key event is phosphorylation-state dysregulation. [e2220.2]
  4. RPPA/phosphoproteomic analysis in LUAD found MAPK and mTOR pathway activation in tumors lacking an obvious genomic explanation, indicating that phospho-network output can remain uncaptured by DNA/RNA driver calling. [e2219.6]
  5. In oncogene-negative LUAD with activated RAS/PI3K signaling, about 45% of human tumors showed moderate to strong dual pERK/pAKT activation by IHC even when no single canonical proto-oncogene alteration was evident, supporting pathway-state-first classification. [e2178.0] [e2171.0]
  6. In 13 of 20 genomic-PTEN-wildtype oncogene-negative LUADs, PTEN protein was low by IHC despite absence of genomic PTEN mutation, demonstrating a clinically relevant pathway lesion that exists at the protein/regulatory level rather than as a coding event. [e2171.1]
  7. KEAP1-mutant LUAD showed cases in which functional consequence was clarified only proteogenomically: some mutations did not change RNA abundance but reduced KEAP1 protein or increased NFE2L2 phosphosites, and the BTB-domain G511V case implied disruption of KEAP1-NFE2L2 binding rather than simple expression change. [e2220.1]
  8. The EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD cohort was not fully explained by one rescued hidden lesion; instead, broader profiling resolved tumors into pathway states involving proliferation, immune, angiogenesis, and metabolism, reinforcing that for some negative cohorts the explanatory unit is a state rather than a single event. [e2164.0]
  9. The paper on histologic progression in LUAD concluded that progression from lepidic to solid patterns was not explained by recurrent genomic drivers but by epigenetic and transcriptional reprogramming, supporting a broader framework in which non-genetic state can dominate phenotype when genetic explanation is weak. [e2210.0]
  10. This law/statement was logically inferred through induction from the combined evidence that multiple studies independently report pathway activation detectable at the phosphoprotein/protein level without a matching or sufficient DNA lesion, including SHP2, ARHGEF5/SRGAP1, PTEN-low, RPPA MAPK/mTOR, and KEAP1/NRF2 examples.

Statement 1: Driver-equivalent protein states are often composite tumor-intrinsic plus microenvironmental circuits

Statement: In a subset of unresolved LUAD, the stable oncogenic state is maintained jointly by tumor-cell signaling and microenvironment-coupled secretory or immune programs. Therefore, hidden drivers can appear as composite circuits such as STK11-linked neutrophil degranulation, FGL1-HNF1A-CPS1 immune evasion, or MIF-HIF1A glycolytic reinforcement, rather than as isolated cell-autonomous mutations.

Domain/Scope: Applies to lung adenocarcinoma subsets in which proteomics, phosphoproteomics, transcriptomics, or single-cell profiling identify secretory, immune, or metabolic programs that are not well reconstructed from canonical genomic drivers alone. Especially relevant to STK11-altered tumors, never-smoker cohorts, and driver-negative cohorts analyzed with proteogenomic or single-cell methods. Less applicable where microenvironmental sampling is poor or where the tumor is fully explained by a dominant cell-autonomous kinase driver.

Special Cases

  1. Some secreted or degranulation proteins may originate from infiltrating immune cells rather than tumor cells; spatial or cell-type-resolved validation is needed.
  2. Certain programs may primarily modulate progression or therapy resistance rather than initiation.
  3. Association with a tumor suppressor lesion such as STK11 does not prove that the downstream secretory circuit is itself sufficient for tumor maintenance.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. STK11-mutant LUADs showed a proteomics-specific neutrophil degranulation program, with coherent overexpression of 16 degranulation proteins including CAMP, LTF, BPI, MMP8, MMP9, MPO, LCN2, ELANE, and ARG1, and the signal was explicitly absent at the RNA level because these proteins are granule-stored. [e2220.4]
  2. FGL1 was identified as a subtype-specific immune inhibitory ligand in NSCLC subtype 4, overexpressed in an immune-cold STK11-linked context and functionally linked to LAG3-mediated immune suppression, indicating that secreted ligand biology can be part of the disease-maintaining state. [e2216.2]
  3. HNF1A showed epigenetically regulated outlier expression in the same STK11-associated subtype and was linked to FGL1 and CPS1 expression, suggesting a lineage-like transcriptional module underlying the secretory immune-evasion state. [e2216.1]
  4. The integrated STK11-FGL1-HNF1A-CPS1 subtype 4 mechanism argued that STK11 mutation alone did not fully explain phenotype and that reduced STK11 mRNA/protein or epigenetic regulation in STK11-wildtype tumors could produce the same liver-like FGL1/CPS1 program. [e2216.0]
  5. In the NENA cohort, STK11 mutation, copy-number changes, GPER1-linked trans effects, and hypomethylation of LLGL2/ST14 were integrated into a broader estrogen-signaling state rather than a single-lesion explanation, further supporting composite-state drivers. [e2249.0] [e2249.3] [e2249.4]
  6. Driver-gene-negative LUAD showed a MIF-HIF-1α positive feedback axis, with MIF-high AT2-like cells showing higher glycolysis, and cell experiments showed MIF overexpression and HIF-1α activation increased lactate production, glucose uptake, LDH activity, and proliferation; ISO-1 partially reversed these effects. [e2166.0] [e2166.1]
  7. The review of TTF1-negative LUAD described a poor-prognosis subtype enriched for KEAP1 mutations, NRF2 activity, DNA repair proteins, cyclin B1, and SRGN overexpression, implying a lineage-state/immune-state composite not reducible to a single driver mutation. [e2218.6]
  8. Proteogenomic analysis of EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD identified four subgroups defined by pathway and microenvironmental states rather than a single rescued hidden lesion, including proliferation, immune, angiogenesis, and metabolic programs. [e2164.0]
  9. This law/statement was logically inferred through induction from the conjunction of STK11-neutrophil degranulation, FGL1/HNF1A/CPS1, NENA estrogen-signaling, and MIF-HIF1A results: in each case, the explanatory unit spans tumor-intrinsic signaling plus secretory or microenvironment-coupled programs that are incompletely captured by DNA lesion catalogues alone.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. In residual WGS-negative or panel-negative LUAD, unsupervised phosphoproteomic clustering will identify recurrent groups whose drug sensitivity is better predicted by phospho-network state than by mutation count, smoking status, or TMB.
  2. Among oncogene-negative pAKT-high LUADs lacking PTEN mutation, a large fraction will show either low PTEN protein, altered PTEN-complex stoichiometry, or phosphatase-network suppression by proteomics/spatial proteomics.
  3. FGL1-high or neutrophil-degranulation-high LUADs will show secretome profiles that cannot be reconstructed from bulk RNA abundance alone, because key proteins are granule-stored or post-transcriptionally regulated.
  4. A subset of never-smoker oncogene-negative LUADs will segregate into MIF-high glycolytic states with stronger sensitivity to MIF/HIF1A-axis perturbation than to broad RTK inhibition.
  5. In tumors with SHP2 Y62 or ARHGEF5 Y1370 phospho-outlier states, acute pathway inhibition will collapse downstream signaling even when no corresponding coding lesion is found in the target itself.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Spatial proteomics may reveal that some apparent tumor-cell phospho-driver states are stabilized by geographically restricted stromal or immune ligand niches, such that removing the niche abolishes the phospho-state without editing the tumor genome.
  2. Longitudinal relapse specimens from initially canonical-driver-positive LUAD may convert into protein-state-dominant survival states, explaining why some relapsed tumors appear genomically 'driver-negative' despite persistent pathway addiction.
  3. Multi-omic perturbation of ARHGEF5/SRGAP1, SHP2 phospho-states, and FGL1/HNF1A/CPS1 programs may uncover a common master stabilizer module, such as phosphatase rewiring or proteostasis circuitry, shared across otherwise different unresolved LUAD subclasses.
  4. Some tumors classified as genomically unresolved may prove to be maintained primarily by secretory autocrine loops detectable only in plasma/tumor interstitial fluid proteomics, not in tumor DNA or bulk RNA.
  5. Driver-equivalent protein states may define a new therapeutic taxonomy that cuts across histology and genotype, grouping STK11/FGL1, PTEN-low/pAKT-high, SHP2-high-phospho, and MIF-high tumors into intervention classes not visible from genomics alone.

Negative Experiments

  1. If residual unexplained LUADs do not form reproducible phosphoproteomic/proteomic clusters across independent cohorts and sample-preparation platforms, the theory's central claim of stable protein-state classes is weakened.
  2. If candidate phospho-outlier nodes such as SHP2 or ARHGEF5 can be selectively perturbed without affecting viability, pathway coherence, or drug sensitivity in relevant models, their proposed driver-equivalent role is undermined.
  3. If RNA or DNA features predict the same vulnerabilities as well as proteomic state in residual negative tumors, then the distinctive explanatory value of protein-state-first classification is reduced.
  4. If spatial or purified-cell analyses show that neutrophil degranulation, FGL1, or related signals are purely microenvironmental contaminants with no association to tumor-cell state or treatment response, the composite-circuit version of the theory is weakened.
  5. If PTEN-low genomic-wildtype tumors fail to show any reproducible downstream PI3K-AKT dependency or phosphatase-network abnormality, the claim that protein-level suppressor loss can act as a hidden driver-equivalent mechanism would be weakened.
