Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1876

Name: Substitute-Driver Convergence Theory for Oncogene-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: A substantial subset of bona fide oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinomas is not driverless but is driven by convergence onto core signaling output states through noncanonical architectures. In one branch, combinatorial loss of negative regulators substitutes for a canonical oncogene by jointly activating RAS/MAPK and PI3K/AKT signaling; in another branch, rare hidden lesions such as NF1/RASA1 loss, RIT1, SOS1, RASA1, VAV1, MET exon 14 skipping, focal MET/ERBB2 amplification, and related RTK/RAS/RAF-axis alterations produce the same functional output. The governing principle is that pathway-state sufficiency, not lesion identity, is the operative driver unit.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Suppressor-convergence substitute-driver law

Statement: In lung adenocarcinomas lacking known canonical proto-oncogene activation, concurrent inactivation of multiple negative regulators spanning the RAS and PI3K pathways can be sufficient to initiate and sustain malignant growth, with NF1 plus RASA1 plus PTEN loss constituting a validated substitute-driver configuration and RASA1 plus NF1 co-loss representing a human clinical analogue.

Domain/Scope: Applies to oncogene-negative or broad-no-known-actionable-driver lung adenocarcinoma in which canonical activating RTK/RAS/RAF lesions are absent or insufficient to explain tumor behavior. Strongest support is in autochthonous mouse LUAD models and human NSCLC/LUAD genomic subsets with concurrent loss of RasGAP and PI3K-suppressor function. Does not claim that all driver-negative LUAD uses this mechanism, only that it defines a major mechanistically coherent subclass.

Special Cases

  1. The exact suppressor set need not always be NF1, RASA1, and PTEN; human tumors may realize the same state through partial genomic loss, epigenetic silencing, low protein abundance, or related pathway-brake lesions.
  2. Double-loss states can be tumorigenic but are generally weaker than triple-loss states in the mouse models.
  3. Background genotype modifies penetrance and fitness landscape accessibility, especially TP53, KEAP1, and LKB1 status.
  4. Some human tumors with dual pERK/pAKT activity may instead be explained by hidden rare oncogenes or splice/fusion events rather than suppressor-loss convergence.

  2. The same work showed pERK and pAKT positivity in mouse tumors, and human oncogene-negative LUAD showed a substantial fraction with moderate-to-strong dual pERK/pAKT activation, supporting pathway-state convergence rather than lesion-specific dependence. [e2399.0] [e2392.0] [e2392.2]
  3. The fitness-landscape study demonstrated that all mutational paths toward Nf1/Rasa1/Pten triple loss were fitness-increasing, indicating that suppressor-loss substitute-driver evolution is unusually accessible in oncogene-negative LUAD. [e2385.0] [e2385.1] [e2385.2]
  4. Concurrent RASA1 and NF1 loss defined a distinct smoking-associated human NSCLC subset lacking other major mitogenic drivers and showing RAS-pathway dependence, providing a clinically observed human branch of the same general substitute-driver logic. [e2422.0]
  5. Oncogene-negative LUAD in TCGA/GENIE was explicitly framed as a clinically important subset in which pathway activation often arose through tumor suppressor inactivation rather than canonical oncogene mutation, with >60% of cases showing alterations in either RAS or PI3K pathway components and 22% in both. This law was logically generalized by induction from the validated NF1/RASA1/PTEN model and the human RASA1/NF1 co-loss subset: if multiple independent negative regulators of the same signaling outputs are recurrently lost in driver-negative LUAD, then pathway-state sufficiency is the conserved driver principle.

Statement 1: Dual-pathway therapy law

Statement: When oncogene-negative LUAD is maintained by convergent activation of both RAS/MAPK and PI3K/AKT outputs rather than by a single canonical oncogene, combined blockade of an upstream RAS-input node and a PI3K/AKT-output node should outperform monotherapy and should be more robust to lesion heterogeneity than lesion-matched single-agent therapy.

Domain/Scope: Applies to oncogene-negative LUAD with validated or strongly inferred dual-pathway activation, especially dual pERK/pAKT-positive tumors or tumors with suppressor-loss architectures affecting both branches. Strongest evidence is preclinical in mouse models, allografts, and human cell systems. It should not be extrapolated to driver-negative tumors whose main biology is microenvironmental, lineage-plastic, angiogenic, or immune-dominant without biochemical evidence of dual pathway activation.

Special Cases

  1. Tumors with dominant activation of only one branch may respond differently and may not require dual blockade.
  2. The optimal vertical pair may vary: SHP2 plus AKT, MEK plus PI3K, or other RAS/PI3K combinations could outperform one another depending on genotype and pathway topology.
  3. Clinical translation may be constrained by toxicity even if preclinical efficacy is superior.
  4. Canonical single-driver tumors rescued by deeper sequencing may still be better treated with lesion-matched monotherapy.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. In the oncogene-negative RAS/PI3K mouse and cell models, SHP2 inhibition with RMC-4550 plus AKT inhibition with capivasertib outperformed single agents, reducing tumor burden and synergistically suppressing growth. [e2399.0] [e2399.3] [e2392.0] [e2392.2]
  2. The same oncogene-negative substitute-driver work argued that these tumors are vulnerable to pharmacologic inhibition of both pathway branches, and the combination was more effective than monotherapy in autochthonous tumors, cell lines, and allografts. [e2399.0] [e2392.0] [e2392.2]
  3. In the human RASA1/NF1 co-loss subset, trametinib was active, and combination with PI3K inhibition or EGFR-pathway inhibition further improved suppression, supporting the general principle that dual-pathway substitute drivers require combination targeting. [e2422.0]
  4. The broad therapeutic review for RTK/RAS/RAF-negative LUAD proposed SHP2, SOS1, MEK, ERK, PI3K, AKT, and mTOR as rational combinatorial targets precisely because pathway-state activation can persist without a single dominant oncogene lesion. [e2390.2] [e2433.0] [e2431.6]
  5. RPPA and phosphoproteomic analyses in oncogene-negative or pathway-unexplained LUAD showed substantial MAPK and mTOR activation not fully explained by known DNA lesions, implying that pathway-state-guided rather than lesion-only therapy is necessary in this domain. [e2431.5] [e2431.6] [e2427.1]
  6. This law was logically inferred from the dual-activation substitute-driver law above and the preclinical superiority of SHP2+AKT and MEK+PI3K combinations in suppressor-loss contexts: if two signaling branches are jointly required for tumor fitness, then suppressing only one should leave residual fitness through the other branch.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If broad-negative LUADs are prospectively selected by concurrent moderate-to-strong pERK and pAKT staining, they will be enriched for combinations of NF1, RASA1, PTEN, STK11, KEAP1, or related pathway-brake deficiencies even when hotspot-driver sequencing is negative.
  2. If PTEN, NF1, and RASA1 protein abundance and promoter methylation are added to DNA-based testing in broad-negative LUAD, the fraction assignable to a substitute-driver class will increase beyond WES/WGS-only classification.
  3. If patient-derived organoids from oncogene-negative LUAD are stratified by dual-pathway activation, SHP2+AKT or MEK+PI3K combination therapy will outperform monotherapy mainly in the dual-high group and not in dual-low controls.
  4. If multiregion sampling is performed in substitute-driver tumors, branch-specific upstream lesions will vary but the tumors will recurrently converge on preserved dual RAS/PI3K pathway activation states.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If some residual WGS-negative LUADs are truly substitute-driver tumors, a subset will show purely epigenetic silencing of NF1/RASA1/PTEN-axis components with no coding or copy-number lesion but the same dual-pathway drug sensitivity as genomic triple-loss models.
  2. Single-cell phosphoproteomics of broad-negative LUAD may reveal that substitute-driver tumors contain parallel subclones using different upstream lesions yet occupying the same dual-pathway signaling attractor state, implying state-level rather than genotype-level clonality.
  3. In early-stage never-smoker driver-negative LUAD, rare lineage-plastic states such as chaotic/piano tumors may transition into substitute-driver states late in progression by accumulating suppressor-loss combinations rather than acquiring a canonical kinase driver.
  4. A clinically useful 'substitute-driver score' integrating pERK, pAKT, PTEN/NF1/RASA1 protein loss, and structural suppressor lesions may predict benefit from dual-pathway inhibition better than any single genotype.

Negative Experiments

  1. Identify a large cohort of rigorously broad-negative LUAD with strong dual pERK/pAKT activation, intact NF1/RASA1/PTEN-axis regulation at DNA, RNA, protein, and methylation levels, and no sensitivity to dual-pathway inhibition; this would substantially weaken the substitute-driver interpretation.
  2. Show in validated NF1/RASA1/PTEN or RASA1/NF1 substitute-driver models that single-agent therapy consistently matches combination therapy in suppressing tumor growth and pathway output; this would directly challenge the dual-pathway therapy law.
  3. Demonstrate that tumors with NF1/RASA1/PTEN triple loss no longer depend on RAS/MAPK or PI3K/AKT signaling after establishment, despite preserved genotype; this would undermine the claim that pathway-state convergence is the operative driver unit.
  4. Prospectively show that canonical rare-driver rescue events account for nearly all dual pERK/pAKT-positive broad-negative tumors, leaving no meaningful suppressor-loss subclass; this would collapse the theory into a pure hidden-oncogene model.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. This theory does not explain microenvironment-dominant driver-negative states such as immune-high, angiogenesis-high, or metabolism-high EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD, where no single suppressor-loss substitute driver is demonstrated. [e2408.0] [e2408.1] [e2408.2] [e2408.3] [e2408.4]
  2. It does not explain lineage-plastic or stem-like driver-poor states such as piano never-smoker LUAD or the chaotic NS-LUAD subtype, where the leading hypotheses are quiescence exit, lineage infidelity, EMT, and CAF-rich ecosystem remodeling rather than dual RAS/PI3K convergence. [e2406.0] [e2406.2] [e2389.0] [e2389.2]
