Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1842

Name: Regulatory-Rewiring Theory of Hidden Driver Activation in Oncogene-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: A major subset of oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinomas is driven not by undiscovered hotspot mutations in the usual kinase genes, but by rewiring of signal delivery and signal interpretation. This rewiring occurs through four under-recognized mechanisms: ligand presentation and juxtacrine/autocrine activation, noncoding cis-regulatory activation via enhancer hijacking or super-enhancer/structural-variant overlap, exon-skipping or other RNA-processing changes that remove negative regulation, and phosphoproteomic activation of signaling nodes without strong DNA-level cues. The central idea is that many driver-negative tumors are 'wiring-positive': the cancer circuit is activated, but the activating lesion resides in regulatory architecture or post-genomic state rather than in a canonical coding driver hotspot.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Ligand and splice rewiring can substitute for classical kinase-domain activation

Statement: In oncogene-negative LUAD, receptor pathway addiction can arise when regulatory alterations change ligand presentation or receptor turnover rather than directly mutating kinase domains; therefore, ligand-fusion events and splice-mediated loss of receptor negative regulation are mechanistically equivalent to classical activating kinase mutations within the same signaling axis.

Domain/Scope: Applies to driver-negative or pan-negative lung adenocarcinoma subsets in which canonical EGFR/KRAS/ALK/ROS1/RET/BRAF/HER2/MET hotspot drivers are absent by routine testing. Most strongly supported in invasive mucinous adenocarcinoma, MET-altered NSCLC, and rare ERBB-pathway hidden-driver cases. Does not imply that every hidden driver is ligand- or splice-based.

Special Cases

  1. Some ligand-fusion tumors also show strong lineage-state features, so receptor activation and lineage reprogramming may be co-primary.
  2. In MET exon 14 skipping, signaling remains ligand-dependent rather than strictly ligand-independent; the key lesion is loss of turnover control.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. CD74-NRG1 was recurrent in pan-negative invasive mucinous adenocarcinoma, especially in female never-smokers, and functions as a membrane-tethered ligand-presenting alteration that activates ERBB2/ERBB3 with downstream AKT and S6K signaling. [e1952.0] [e1995.0] [e1926.1]
  2. Independent work in KRAS-negative/pan-negative IMA showed CD74-NRG1-conditioned media induced HER2, HER3, ERK, and AKT phosphorylation, and this signaling was suppressed by lapatinib or afatinib, confirming ligand-driven ERBB activation. [e1985.1] [e2035.0]
  3. SLC3A2-NRG1 and other NRG1 fusion partners preserved the NRG1 EGF-like domain, indicating that partner interchangeability can preserve the same ligand-presentation mechanism. [e1985.2] [e2035.1] [e1938.0]
  4. NRG1 rearrangements with multiple 5-prime partners and NRG1 outlier expression were found in driver-unknown lung cancers, supporting the broader principle that ligand-driven ERBB signaling is a recurrent hidden-driver mode beyond one fusion partner or one histology. [e1938.0]
  5. MET exon 14 skipping removes the juxtamembrane Y1003 Cbl-binding site, reduces ubiquitination, delays receptor downregulation, and produces sustained HGF-dependent MET and MAPK signaling in tumors otherwise wild-type for KRAS, BRAF, EGFR, and HER2. [e1948.0]
  6. The MET exon 14-skipping discovery paper found the event concentrated in tumors lacking canonical point drivers or canonical fusions, emphasizing that altered receptor turnover can substitute for a classic oncogenic driver. [e1932.2] [e1938.3]
  7. The comprehensive molecular profiling study highlighted MET exon 14 skipping and focal MET amplification as hidden RTK activation mechanisms in oncogene-negative LUAD, reinforcing the generality of receptor regulatory rewiring. [e1961.3] [e1946.2]
  8. Rare EGFR extracellular-domain mutations in pan-negative LUAD and the EGFR M277E mutant produced ligand-independent receptor activation and sensitivity to EGFR-directed agents, illustrating that noncanonical receptor activation can occur outside standard kinase-domain hotspot logic. [e1926.2] [e1933.0]
  9. Rare HER2 extracellular-domain and transmembrane-domain mutations in pan-negative LUAD were also found outside routine kinase-domain screening, again showing that receptor-axis activation can be hidden in noncanonical regulatory domains. [e1926.3]

Statement 1: Noncoding and phosphoproteomic rewiring reveals a hidden driver class missed by mutation panels

Statement: When canonical coding drivers are absent, oncogenic dependency in LUAD can arise from cis-regulatory genome rewiring or from protein-state activation not predicted by RNA or DNA; therefore, enhancer architecture, structural variation, and phosphosite outliers should be treated as primary driver-discovery spaces rather than secondary annotations.

Domain/Scope: Applies to non-CAGA LUAD, RTK/RAS/RAF-pathway-negative LUAD, proteogenomic LUAD cohorts, and candidate hidden-driver subsets where outlier expression or phosphosite activation is present without a canonical coding explanation. Most relevant to tumors unresolved after conventional DNA-panel testing.

Special Cases

  1. The strongest direct cis-regulatory examples in the provided evidence are in LUAD for ERBB2 and in other cancers for IRS4/IGF2; cross-cancer generalization is supported mechanistically but not yet fully frequency-validated in LUAD.
  2. Some phosphosite states occur downstream of known drivers rather than replacing them; those cases support the importance of protein-state measurement but not necessarily driver-negative biology specifically.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. LUAD without clinically actionable genetic alterations showed super-enhancer and structural-variant overlap enriched in non-CAGA tumors, with outlier expression of cancer genes and recurrence risk associations, implying hidden driver activity in regulatory rather than coding space. [e1928.0] [e1928.2]
  2. ERBB2/HER2 overexpression in non-CAGA LUAD was directly linked to super-enhancer formation plus structural rearrangement; CRISPR engineering of the inversion increased HER2 expression, and PDXs responded to poziotinib and trastuzumab deruxtecan but not afatinib. [e1928.1]
  3. The same paper treats wild-type ERBB2 activation by structural/epigenetic rewiring as a hidden driver-like event, explicitly showing that actionable pathway addiction can arise without canonical HER2 mutation. [e1928.1]
  4. RPA(-)G LUAD identified recurrent ILF2 promoter mutations within open chromatin, increased ILF2 expression, and correlation with higher structural-variant burden, nominating noncoding promoter mutation as a hidden driver mechanism in RTK/RAS/RAF-negative LUAD. [e1940.0] [e1940.1]
  5. The CESAM framework and its pan-cancer validations show that cis-expression structural alteration mapping can recover hidden regulatory drivers missed by coding-centric methods, providing a methodological precedent for hidden cis-drivers in lung cancer. [e1986.2]
  6. Proteogenomic LUAD identified phosphosite outliers that were not captured by RNA abundance, including SHP2 Y62 phosphorylation in EGFR-mutant tumors, ALK Y1507 in ALK fusions, SOS1 S1161 in KRAS-mutant tumors, KEAP1/NRF2 phosphosite activation, and neutrophil degranulation uniquely at the protein level in STK11-mutant tumors. [e2023.0] [e1947.6] [e1947.5] [e1947.7] [e2023.1] [e2023.2] [e1947.8] [e2023.4] [e1947.10] [e1947.11]
  7. The proteogenomic study explicitly states that pathway activation in tumors lacking known targetable mutations may be detectable only at protein/phosphosite level, making phosphoproteomics a primary—not merely confirmatory—hidden-driver assay. [e2023.0] [e1947.0]
  8. Outlier kinase expression without detectable rearrangement, such as ROS1 or FGFR3 overexpression in driver-unknown tumors, suggests that some hidden drivers act through abnormal expression states even when the structural cause remains unresolved. [e1938.4]

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If canonical-driver-negative LUADs are profiled with matched RNA-seq, WGS, and H3K27ac/ATAC-based regulatory mapping, additional cases of wild-type oncogene activation by structural-regulatory rewiring will be found beyond ERBB2.
  2. If driver-negative LUADs with strong pERK or pAKT but no canonical coding lesion are analyzed for promoter/enhancer structural abnormalities and exon-skipping events, a substantial fraction will reveal regulatory or RNA-processing hidden drivers.
  3. If pan-negative mucinous LUAD is systematically screened for ligand-fusion biology rather than only kinase-fusion biology, the fraction of ERBB-axis-driven tumors will increase.
  4. If phosphoproteomic outlier analysis is added to routine evaluation of sequencing-negative LUAD, clinically relevant target hypotheses will emerge in a subset that is invisible to DNA panels alone.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If a prospective treatment trial assigns sequencing-negative LUAD to therapy based primarily on phosphosite outliers and regulatory rewiring signatures rather than DNA mutations, objective response may exceed historical empiric therapy response rates.
  2. If noncoding and phosphoproteomic hidden drivers are mapped together in single tumors, some cases may show regulatory rewiring upstream of the very phosphosite outlier that appears unexplained by DNA alone, linking chromatin and phosphoproteomic hidden drivers into one circuit.
  3. If lineage-reprogrammed LUADs such as NKX2-1-low mucinous tumors are profiled for cis-regulatory rewiring, they may show recurrent enhancer-state dependencies that are lineage-specific and therapeutically targetable.

Negative Experiments

  1. Profile a broad canonical-driver-negative LUAD cohort with WGS, RNA-seq, and phosphoproteomics; if regulatory abnormalities and phosphosite outliers are rare and non-recurrent, the theory is weakened.
  2. Test whether phosphosite outliers without canonical DNA lesions predict drug sensitivity better than chance; if not, phosphoproteomic rewiring may be epiphenomenal rather than driver-like.
  3. Engineer candidate cis-regulatory or splice alterations into model systems; if they fail to reproduce signaling activation or growth phenotypes, the regulatory-rewiring interpretation is weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The strongest pan-cancer enhancer-hijacking examples in the evidence involve colorectal cancer and lung squamous carcinoma rather than LUAD, so the LUAD frequency of this mechanism beyond ERBB2 remains uncertain. [e1986.0] [e1986.1] [e2029.0] [e2029.1]
  2. Some phosphoproteomic outliers cited in the evidence occur in canonical driver-positive tumors and may reflect downstream consequence rather than alternate driver mechanism. [e1947.5] [e1947.6] [e1947.7] [e2023.1] [e2023.2]
