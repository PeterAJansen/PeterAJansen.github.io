Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1883

Name: State-first oncogenesis in residual driver-negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: After hidden canonical and suppressor-convergent cases are removed, a residual class of lung adenocarcinomas appears to be driven less by a single dominant genomic driver and more by cell-state dysregulation. These tumors occupy developmental, lineage-plastic, metabolic, immune, or hormone-linked attractor states: quiescent/stem-like piano disease, KAC-rich precursor programs, NKX2-1-loss mucinous reprogramming, MIF-HIF-1α glycolytic states, and estrogen-SRC/GPER1 signaling in EGFR/ALK-negative never-smoker disease. In this class, progression follows state transitions and microenvironmental remodeling more than recurrent acquisition of new truncal oncogenes.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: State-dominance law

Statement: A residual subset of true driver-negative LUAD is better explained by stable but plastic epithelial state programs than by recurrent dominant genomic lesions; these tumors cluster by lineage identity, developmental score, immune architecture, or metabolic program, and their biology is more visible in RNA/protein/spatial data than in hotspot mutation catalogs.

Domain/Scope: Applies to residual LUADs after exclusion of canonical hidden drivers and overt suppressor-convergent RAS/PI3K states; strongest in never-smoker or low-SCNA/TMB tumors, early-stage multiregion disease, mucinous lineage-shift tumors, and transcriptomic/proteogenomic subtypes defined without a dominant oncogene.

Special Cases

  1. State-dominant tumors can still harbor occasional canonical drivers, especially EGFR, KRAS, or RET; the law concerns the principal organizing biology, not absolute absence of mutations.
  2. Bulk expression signatures can be confounded by stromal or immune admixture, so single-cell or spatial validation is important.
  3. This law is least applicable when clear pathway-high lesions or targetable fusions are already present.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Piano LCINS is a low-TMB, low-SCNA, low-SV, long-telomere, high-subclonality subtype with sparse known recurrent drivers and elevated developmental/stemness scores, explicitly motivating a quiescent adult stem-like origin hypothesis for tumors lacking a dominant known driver.   2. The steady never-smoker LUAD subtype shows low proliferation, high alveolar AT2 identity, fewer driver alterations, favorable prognosis, low immune-evasion scores, and TRU-like lineage fidelity, consistent with a driver-sparse developmental state rather than strong oncogenic pathway addiction. [e2264.1]
  3. KRT8-positive alveolar intermediate cells (KACs) are less differentiated transitional epithelial states enriched near tumors and through premalignant progression, associated with poor outcome, and proposed as an intermediate state in transformation from alveolar epithelium to LUAD. [e2321.0] [e2321.1] [e2321.2] [e2266.0]
  4. NENA (EGFR/ALK-negative never-smoker LUAD) is characterized not by a single hidden canonical driver but by elevated estrogen signaling linked to STK11 mutation, GPER1 CNA/trans effects, LLGL2/ST14 hypomethylation, and SRC overexpression, indicating a hormone-linked state mechanism. Statement 1: Non-genetic progression law

Statement: Within state-dominant driver-negative LUAD, histologic progression is governed primarily by shifts in epithelial identity and spatial ecology—angiogenic endothelial states, fibroblast states, immune exclusion or inflaming, and lineage reprogramming—rather than by recurrent acquisition of pattern-specific new genomic drivers.

Domain/Scope: Applies to early-stage, multiregion, pattern-heterogeneous LUAD and lineage-switch tumors, especially lepidic-to-solid progression, AIS/MIA transition, and NKX2-1-low mucinous reprogramming; strongest where spatial transcriptomics, methylation, and multiregion sequencing are available.

Special Cases

  1. Later canonical lesions can still emerge and accelerate progression, but they are not required to explain the main histologic transitions covered by this law.
  2. This law concerns progression within established tumor fields and may not describe the very first initiating event.
  3. It is most applicable to early-stage disease and may be partly obscured in heavily evolved metastatic tumors.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Multiregion histology-guided LUAD showed that drivers were predominantly clonal and not pattern-specific, while progression from lepidic to solid correlated with transcriptomic, methylation, and immune-remodeling changes; the authors explicitly treated pattern progression as not attributable to distinct region-specific canonical genomic drivers. [e2310.1] [e2310.2]
  2. In a TCGA pattern-annotated LUAD subset, no recurrent genetic lesion was enriched in any histologic pattern, whereas gene-expression and methylation changes robustly tracked the lepidic-to-solid continuum, arguing against a simple pattern-specific driver-mutation model. [e2310.0]
  3. AIS3 was defined as a late AIS subtype enriched for tip endothelial cells and matrix CAFs, and spatial validation in MIA supported angiogenesis and stromal remodeling as features of the transition toward invasion. [e2331.4] [e2331.5] [e2331.6]
  4. The review-level synthesis of LUAD progression states concluded that lepidic-to-solid evolution is primarily nongenetic, involving epithelial plasticity and TME remodeling rather than new driver mutations. [e2266.2]
  5. NKX2-1 loss models and human NKX2-1-negative mucinous adenocarcinomas show that lineage-factor disruption can reprogram lung tumors toward a gastric/mucinous identity with major phenotypic consequences without requiring a new canonical RTK driver. [e2306.0] [e2337.0] [e2337.1] [e2337.2]
  6. The lepidic-to-solid (L2S) signature reproduced progression and prognosis across large cohorts, supporting the idea that a transcriptomic state gradient captures progression better than a pattern-specific genomic lesion. [e2310.2]

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If residual WGS-negative or non-CAGA LUADs are profiled by single-cell multiome and spatial transcriptomics, they will segregate into a limited number of recurrent state programs—quiescent/alveolar, glycolytic AT2-like, hormone-SRC/GPER1, angiogenic-stromal, and lineage-switched mucinous—despite weak recurrence at the DNA-driver level.
  2. If early lesions from piano- or KAC-rich lungs are followed longitudinally, the first changes predicting progression will be shifts in epithelial-state composition, tip endothelial cells, and matrix CAFs rather than recurrent acquisition of new hotspot kinase mutations.
  3. If organoids from state-dominant driver-negative LUAD are perturbed, differentiation-state manipulation, hypoxia/hormone modulation, or stromal coculture changes will often alter growth more than single-agent RTK inhibition.
  4. If NENA and glycolytic driver-negative LUAD are treated with pathway-matched but noncanonical agents, SRC inhibition and MIF/HIF-1α axis perturbation will show stronger subtype selectivity than broad anti-EGFR strategies.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. A subset of residual driver-negative LUAD may be initiated by cyclical injury-repair programs in alveolar intermediates, so temporally patterned injury or pollution biomarkers could predict future emergence of state-first tumors years before diagnosis.
  2. Differentiation therapy that restores alveolar identity or NKX2-1-like chromatin occupancy may suppress progression of lineage-switched driver-negative LUAD even without producing immediate tumor-cell death.
  3. Some state-dominant tumors may depend on extracellular-vesicle-mediated communication from transitional epithelial cells or fibroblasts, making circulating vesicle cargo a stronger early biomarker than ctDNA.
  4. Hormone-linked never-smoker LUAD and glycolytic AT2-like driver-negative LUAD may share a deeper common dependency on stress-adaptation nodes such as SRC, HIF-1α, or unfolded-protein-response wiring despite very different transcriptomic appearances.

Negative Experiments

  1. In rigorously profiled residual driver-negative LUAD, test whether single-cell and spatial state programs outperform genomic lesions in predicting prognosis, histology, and treatment response; if not, the state-first theory is weakened.
  2. Longitudinally sample premalignant and early invasive lesions to test whether progression is preceded by transcriptomic/spatial state transitions rather than recurrent new drivers; failure of this prediction would weaken the non-genetic progression law.
  3. Experimentally perturb NKX2-1 restoration, MIF inhibition, SRC inhibition, or differentiation-state regulators in subtype-matched models; if these interventions do not selectively affect the proposed state-defined tumors, the theory is weakened.
  4. Compare residual driver-negative tumors with canonical-driver tumors for adjacent normal-tissue enrichment of KAC or developmental states; if enrichment is not selective, the quiescent/state-origin claim loses specificity.
