Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1868

Name: Hidden Regulatory-Structural Activation Theory of Oncogene-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: A substantial fraction of apparently oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinomas are not driven by undiscovered point-mutated kinases but by regulatory and architectural lesions that standard hotspot or exome-centric workflows systematically under-detect. These include enhancer hijacking, super-enhancer formation with structural variation, promoter mutation, promoter/TSS deletion, cryptic exon skipping, focal copy-number events whose operative target is misassigned, and ecDNA/double-minute amplicons. The functional commonality is not gene identity but altered transcriptional dosage or relief of negative regulation. Therefore, hidden-driver discovery in LUAD should prioritize genome architecture, noncoding cis-regulation, and dosage-output validation.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Residual oncogene-negative LUAD is enriched for regulatory and structural lesions that alter dosage or transcript architecture rather than coding hotspots

Statement: When standard hotspot and limited-panel testing is negative in LUAD, the probability increases that the true driver is a structural or regulatory lesion that changes transcript architecture, enhancer access, promoter usage, or copy-number output. The dominant hidden classes are exon skipping, cryptic fusion, promoter/enhancer mutation, promoter/TSS deletion, focal amplification with the wrong target initially presumed, and complex amplicon architecture.

Domain/Scope: Applies to lung adenocarcinoma cohorts initially negative by hotspot PCR, panel sequencing, standard EGFR/KRAS/ALK testing, or WES-plus-limited-RNA workflows, and to residual RTK/RAS/RAF-negative cohorts subjected to WGS, RNA-seq, copy-number profiling, or epigenomic integration. It is aimed at explaining hidden oncogenic activation or suppressor inactivation in adenocarcinoma, not all lung cancer histologies equally.

Special Cases

  1. Some residual cases are truly rare coding-driver cases outside standard panels, such as RIT1, ARAF, EGFR ectodomain, HER2 ECD/TM, or RAF1 CR2 mutations.
  2. Not every structural or regulatory lesion identified is functionally validated; some remain strong candidates rather than proven drivers.
  3. Some apparently structural-negative cases remain unexplained even after WGS, indicating that additional epigenetic, proteomic, or sampling-related mechanisms still exist.

Statement 1: Architectural dosage lesions converge on a limited set of functional outputs: enhancer rewiring, dosage amplification, and relief of negative regulation

Statement: Although the specific hidden lesions in oncogene-negative LUAD are heterogeneous, they converge onto three recurrent functional outputs: enhancer rewiring that increases oncogene transcription, copy-number amplification whose true driver must be identified by expression/protein consequence rather than locus proximity, and structural or regulatory loss of negative regulators that stabilizes receptor or pathway signaling. Therefore, driver assignment in residual LUAD should be based on output validation—expression, phosphoproteomics, allele-specific expression, or functional perturbation—rather than on lesion class alone.

Domain/Scope: Applies to residual LUAD hidden-driver discovery after broad genomic profiling, especially in non-CAGA, RPA(-), and clinically pan-negative cohorts, and to cross-cancer analogies used to infer lung-relevant mechanisms of enhancer hijacking, super-enhancer amplification, and architectural activation. It includes both oncogene activation and tumor-suppressor inactivation when mediated structurally or regulatively.

Special Cases

  1. Some focal events still have unresolved operative targets within amplicons or deletions.
  2. Cross-cancer enhancer-hijacking analogies support mechanism class but not necessarily frequency in LUAD.
  3. Protein-state output can identify pathway activation without uniquely identifying the upstream lesion.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If unresolved LUADs are prioritized by discordance between DNA lesion burden and RNA/protein output, the highest-yield hidden-driver set will be found among expression/phospho outliers rather than among mutation-rich but expression-neutral tumors.
  2. If broad WGS/RNA/epigenomic profiling is applied to non-CAGA or RPA(-)G tumors, recurrent regulatory lesions will cluster near lineage-active enhancers and promoter regions rather than being uniformly distributed across noncoding DNA.
  3. If amplicon targets in residual LUAD are reassigned using matched copy-number plus expression/protein consequence, a nontrivial fraction of currently presumed driver genes will be replaced by neighboring chromatin regulators or lineage factors.
  4. If MET exon 14 skipping, promoter/TSS deletions, and noncoding promoter mutations are jointly screened in panel-negative cases, they will explain more tumors than adding only more hotspot kinase exons.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If single-cell multi-omic maps are generated for unresolved RPA(-)G tumors, some double-minute or SE/SV lesions will prove cell-state-specific and confined to malignant subclones that are invisible in bulk assays.
  2. If long-read plus Hi-C profiling is performed on unresolved LUADs with expression outliers but no coding driver, a substantial subset will show neo-TADs, cryptic inversions, or enhancer-sharing ecDNA hubs not reconstructable by short-read data.
  3. If CN-independent ASE and promoter mutation states are integrated prospectively, they may identify a new therapeutically relevant hidden-driver subclass centered on epigenetic-allelic imbalance rather than kinase signaling.
  4. If hidden-driver outputs are the true organizing variable, drug sensitivity will correlate more strongly with output class—ERBB phospho-state, NRF2 state, MYC enhancer activation, MET stabilization—than with lesion class per se.

Negative Experiments

  1. Apply WGS/RNA-seq/epigenomic profiling to a large standard-test-negative LUAD cohort. If structural and regulatory lesions are not enriched relative to ordinary LUAD, the core theory is weakened.
  2. Test whether output validation truly improves target assignment in amplicons. If copy-number-proximal candidate genes outperform expression/protein-informed assignments, the output-first rule is weakened.
  3. Functionally perturb candidate regulatory lesions such as ILF2 promoter mutations or SE/SV-linked outliers. If they do not alter target expression or tumor phenotypes, the regulatory hidden-driver model is weakened.
  4. Use long-read and chromatin-conformation assays on unresolved expression-outlier tumors. If architectural lesions are largely absent, the role of enhancer rewiring and structural regulation is overstated.
