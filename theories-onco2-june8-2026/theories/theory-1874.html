Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1874

Name: Convergent State Attractor Theory of True Driver-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: A substantial fraction of apparently driver-negative lung adenocarcinomas are not unified by one missing gene class but by convergence onto a limited set of stable malignant states. In these tumors, multiple upstream routes—including rare coding lesions, tumor-suppressor co-loss, noncoding regulatory rearrangements, epigenetic silencing, or no currently detectable lesion—can converge on recurrent downstream attractors: a RAS/PI3K phospho-signaling attractor, a lineage-plasticity attractor, a metabolic persistence attractor, and an ecosystem-coupled angiogenic/immune/CAF attractor. Within these attractors, signaling state and epithelial–stromal organization become more predictive of prognosis and therapeutic vulnerability than the initiating lesion itself. This theory is intended for the residual population remaining after broad genomic driver testing, while explicitly acknowledging that many 'negative' tumors are later rescued by deeper sequencing.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Convergent phospho-state law

Statement: In LUAD that lacks an obvious canonical actionable oncogene, distinct upstream lesions or even currently unresolved lesions can converge onto recurring active phospho-signaling states—especially MAPK and PI3K/mTOR—and these downstream states can be more predictive of tumor behavior and therapeutic vulnerability than genotype labels alone.

Domain/Scope: Applies to lung adenocarcinoma analyzed with phosphoproteomics, RPPA, or strong pathway-state inference, especially oncogene-negative/no-known-actionable-driver cohorts, EGFR-wild-type tumors with unexplained signaling, and tumor-suppressor-driven LUAD models. It does not claim that all driver-negative LUAD is phospho-state driven, nor that phospho-state supersedes genotype in canonical kinase-addicted tumors where the genomic driver is already sufficient and therapeutically dominant.

Special Cases

  1. Canonical driver-positive tumors can share the same phospho-state as driver-negative tumors; this law concerns convergence, not exclusivity.
  2. Some pathway-active tumors are later reclassified by deeper sequencing as harboring rare canonical drivers, so the law is strongest for high-confidence residual negatives after broad testing.
  3. Expression-only cohorts support the law weakly; direct phosphoproteomic or RPPA evidence is the strongest support.
  4. MAPK and mTOR states need not be coupled in every tumor; some tumors show one dominant active axis.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. In East Asian never-smoker LUAD, coordinated phosphoproteomics showed EGFR-pY1197 correlated with MAP2K2-pT394 and MAPK3-pT198/pT202, and tumors ranked by MAPK-signaling score included EGFR-wild-type tumors with high MAPK activity and EGFR-mutant/TP53-mutant tumors with low MAPK activity, demonstrating that pathway activity is not determined by genotype alone. [e2427.1] [e2427.0]
  2. RPPA analysis in TCGA-like LUAD showed substantial phospho-MAPK activity in KRAS-wild-type tumors and concluded that multiple mechanisms and additional undetected alterations likely exist beyond known mutations. [e2431.5]
  3. RPPA also showed substantial mTOR pathway activation, with distinct mTOR states linked to PIK3CA mutation, STK11/AMPK loss, or basal-low state, indicating that pathway activity can be organized into recurrent states only partly explained by known genomic lesions. [e2431.6]
  4. Mouse and human oncogene-negative RAS/PI3K LUAD defined by NF1/RASA1/PTEN loss showed direct pERK and pAKT positivity, RNA-signature evidence of RAS and PI3K activation, and pathway-linked therapeutic response to SHP2 and AKT inhibition, establishing that a state-defined subtype can exist without canonical oncogene activation. [e2399.0] [e2399.1] [e2399.2] [e2399.3] [e2392.0] [e2392.1] [e2392.2]
  5. Combinatorial NF1/RASA1/PTEN inactivation was shown to create a potent oncogene-negative LUAD state with MAPK/PI3K signaling consequences, supporting the idea that different genomic routes can converge on the same downstream signaling regime. [e2399.0] [e2399.1] [e2392.0] [e2392.1]
  6. In broad proteogenomic LUAD studies, tumors lacking targetable oncogene mutations were repeatedly interpreted through proteome/phosphoproteome-defined pathway states rather than through one recurrent hidden gene, including PP-like proliferative/CDK states, EGFR-wild-type MAPK states, and kinase-substrate enrichment signatures. [e2433.0] [e2452.0] [e2473.0] [e2480.0] [e2480.1]
  7. The APOLLO/CPTAC-style LUAD expression-subtype framework identified PP tumors as lacking targetable oncogene mutations in the majority of cases while still showing strong phosphoproteomic evidence of CDK and MAP2K7 activity, illustrating that actionable pathway state can substitute for canonical oncogene dependence. [e2480.0] [e2480.1] [e2454.2]
  8. The proliferation-high and metabolism-high proteogenomic subgroups in EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD were defined primarily by pathway-state activation rather than one hidden lesion, again indicating convergence on recurrent signaling-output phenotypes. [e2408.1] [e2408.4] [e2408.0]
  9. Proteogenomic work in non-smoking East Asian LUAD argued that constitutive MAPK signaling can exist even in EGFR-wild-type tumors and may be only partly accounted for by KRAS mutation status, directly motivating a pathway-state interpretation of some apparently driver-negative tumors. [e2427.0] [e2427.1]
  10. This law was induced by combining the direct phosphoproteomic evidence of genotype–state mismatch with the oncogene-negative RAS/PI3K mouse-human subtype and the PP/NSLA proteogenomic subtype data into a single convergence principle.

Statement 1: Lineage-ecosystem coupling law

Statement: In driver-poor LUAD, unstable epithelial lineage states and local ecosystem programs co-stabilize one another: mesenchymal, alveolar-intermediate, mucinous/gastric, or stem-like epithelial states preferentially recruit CAF-, angiogenic-, or immune architectures, and those stromal/ecosystem states in turn preserve the malignant epithelial state even when no dominant canonical oncogene is evident.

Domain/Scope: Applies to transcriptomic, single-cell, spatial, and proteogenomic studies of driver-poor or non-oncogene-dominated LUAD, especially never-smoker LUAD, early progression states, histologic transition states, and subtypes defined by EMT/CAF/angiogenesis/immune programs. It is less applicable to tumors that are fully explained by a single strong kinase driver with minimal state plasticity.

Special Cases

  1. Some lineage states coexist with canonical drivers such as KRAS mutation or NKX2-1 loss; the law addresses stabilization of state, not absence of any genomic lesion.
  2. Immune-high states may be favorable rather than aggressive; the coupling claim concerns co-stabilization, not uniformly poor prognosis.
  3. Certain ecosystem programs are inferred from bulk deconvolution and need spatial or single-cell confirmation for strongest interpretation.
  4. The law is strongest where epithelial-state data and stromal/ecosystem data are measured together.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. The 'chaotic' transcriptomic subtype of never-smoker LUAD had no significant enrichment of known driver mutations or fusions, yet showed strong EMT/cell-movement programs, high CDH2 and TWIST1, mixed lineage fidelity, increased CAF proportions, advanced stage, and poor prognosis. [e2389.0] [e2389.2]
  2. The 60-gene NS-LUAD signature recapitulated the transcriptomic subtypes including the driver-poor chaotic class, supporting that lineage/plasticity states are coherent, clinically meaningful structures even when driver information is absent. [e2389.2]
  3. The piano subtype in never-smoker lung cancer had low SCNA burden, low TMB, long telomeres, high intratumor heterogeneity, high development score, and basal-cell/stem-like features; the authors proposed quiescence exit and lineage plasticity, potentially involving ARID1A/NOTCH1, as a mechanism for tumors lacking clear canonical drivers. [e2406.0] [e2406.2]
  4. KRT8-positive alveolar intermediate cells (KACs) were positioned between alveolar cells and tumor cells by scRNA-seq and spatial analysis, were enriched in tumor-adjacent and premalignant regions, associated with poor survival, and showed less differentiated, plastic transitional identity; this supports an epithelial-state bridge to malignancy. [e2437.0] [e2388.2] [e2437.3]
  5. The review citation on non-genetic evolution in LUAD explicitly argued that spatial heterogeneity and progression are driven primarily by plastic transcriptional state shifts and TME reprogramming rather than new driver mutations. [e2388.1] [e2388.5]
  6. Histologic progression from lepidic to solid LUAD was reported to follow a continuum driven primarily by non-genetic reprogramming of cancer-cell identity plus microenvironment remodeling, with no reproducible pattern-specific recurrent genomic drivers explaining the transition. [e2413.0]
  7. NKX2-1-negative mucinous/gastric-like LUAD was driven by lineage-state reprogramming in mouse and human studies, where loss of pulmonary fate enabled a gastric differentiation program and altered Foxa1/2 occupancy, showing that lineage circuitry itself can be an organizing driver-like force. [e2448.0] [e2448.1] [e2448.2] [e2446.0]
  8. The angiogenesis-high subgroup of EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD was defined by CAF-associated features, TGF-beta/cell migration programs, angiogenic ligand expression, immune exclusion, and poor prognosis, indicating a stable epithelial–stromal ecosystem state rather than one hidden mutation. [e2408.3] [e2408.0]
  9. The immune-high subgroup in the same cohort was marked by T/B-cell and TLS features, cytokine/chemokine signaling, and high checkpoint expression, demonstrating that ecosystem organization is itself a subtype-defining axis in driver-poor disease. [e2408.2] [e2408.0]
  10. THBS2-positive CAFs were highlighted as a progression-linked stromal biomarker associated with suppressed antitumor immunity and poor response, reinforcing that stromal state can act as a major organizing component of LUAD behavior. [e2388.4]
  11. FeSig-high EGFR-wild-type LUAD linked fibroblast-rich microenvironment, ferroptosis resistance, and poor prognosis, suggesting that fibroblast ecosystem state and epithelial metabolic state are coupled rather than independent. [e2420.0]
  12. The CLDN2-positive AT2/COL6A3-positive fibroblast subtype in multiple primary lung cancers showed explicit epithelial–fibroblast signaling and aggregation, providing an additional lung adenocarcinoma-related example of co-organized epithelial and stromal identity. [e2388.3]
  13. This law was inferred by integrating lineage-plasticity studies (chaotic, piano, KAC, NKX2-1-low, histologic progression) with ecosystem-state studies (angiogenesis-high, immune-high, THBS2+ CAF, FeSig-high), yielding a coupled-state rather than parallel-feature interpretation.

Statement 2: Metabolic persistence law

Statement: When canonical oncogene addiction is weak, absent, or therapeutically unresolved, LUAD persistence increasingly depends on stress-adaptive metabolic programs—glycolysis, cholesterol handling, oxidative phosphorylation, ferroptosis resistance, and ER-stress buffering—and these metabolic programs are conditioned jointly by epithelial lineage state and by stromal ecosystem context.

Domain/Scope: Applies to driver-negative, driver-poor, EGFR-wild-type, never-smoker, and early-stage/subsolid LUAD contexts in which metabolic or stress-response programs were identified by transcriptomics, proteomics, phosphoproteomics, or functional perturbation. It is not intended to claim that metabolism replaces oncogenic signaling in every LUAD, only that it becomes preferentially organizing in residual negative disease.

Special Cases

  1. Different metabolic states imply different interventions; glycolysis-high, OXPHOS-high, ferroptosis-resistant, and ER-stress-buffered tumors are not therapeutically equivalent.
  2. Some metabolic programs are strongest in early lesions or pre-invasive disease and may not dominate advanced LUAD.
  3. Certain metabolic states coexist with canonical drivers, especially STK11/KEAP1- or KRAS-associated backgrounds.
  4. PCSK9/cholesterol findings in early lesions are mechanistically important but therapeutically paradoxical in parts of the dataset.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Driver gene-negative LUAD showed strong glycolysis enrichment by bulk and single-cell analysis, with MIF-high AT2 cells having higher glycolysis scores and a proposed MIF–HIF-1alpha positive feedback loop; MIF overexpression and HIF-1alpha stabilization increased glucose uptake, lactate, LDH activity, and proliferation, while ISO-1 reversed these effects. [e2379.0] [e2379.1] [e2379.2]
  2. The metabolism-high subgroup of EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD showed enrichment for oxidative phosphorylation, lipid metabolism, carbon metabolism, ACAT1, ACACA, and CERS4, indicating a coherent metabolic-dependency subtype independent of one hidden driver gene. [e2408.4] [e2408.0]
  3. FeSig-high EGFR-wild-type LUAD linked fibroblast-rich ecosystem state to ferroptosis resistance and identified DDIT4/NOX4/GCLC as a ferroptosis-inhibitory signature; knockdown increased RSL3-induced ferroptosis and 6-mercaptopurine showed preclinical activity in vivo. [e2420.0]
  4. Early LUAD/subsolid-nodule proteogenomics identified cholesterol metabolism as a preneoplastic hallmark in AIS and used PCSK9 perturbation to show functional impact on transformation and organoid growth, implicating cholesterol handling in tumor initiation. [e2458.0] [e2482.0] [e2458.1]
  5. The same SSN studies identified sustained ER stress and unfolded protein response as a hallmark of progression from AIS to IAC, with higher PERK, ATF4, and phospho-eIF2S1 in rapidly progressing lesions and preclinical suppression of growth by PDI inhibition or Pdia3 knockdown. [e2458.0] [e2458.2] [e2482.0] [e2482.1]
  6. FGFR3 S408 phosphosite activation in early lesions activated PI3K/AKT/mTOR without change in total FGFR3 abundance, demonstrating that metabolic-survival signaling can be controlled at the phosphosite level rather than by classic genomic activation. [e2458.3] [e2482.2]
  7. Broad proteogenomic LUAD studies repeatedly nominated metabolic rewiring as the main actionable layer in tumors without clear genomic targets, especially the PP subtype with glycolysis/glutaminolysis programs and the environment/metabolism-high S-I-like states. [e2452.0] [e2473.0] [e2480.0] [e2480.1] [e2480.2]
  8. The STK11-KEAP1 axis in PP-like LUAD was explicitly linked to metabolic rewiring, oxidative-stress adaptation, and ferroptosis protection/SCD1 dependence, supporting the idea that metabolic persistence becomes central when canonical targetable drivers are absent. [e2480.2]
  9. This law was induced by integrating glycolysis-high, OXPHOS-high, ferroptosis-resistant, cholesterol-handling, and ER-stress progression studies into one persistence framework specific to driver-poor disease.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If high-confidence residual driver-negative LUADs are jointly profiled by DNA/RNA/phosphoproteomics/spatial transcriptomics, most tumors will cluster more strongly by phospho-state plus stromal architecture than by residual mutational burden alone.
  2. If paired epithelial-only organoids and fibroblast/endothelial co-cultures are generated from the same driver-negative LUAD, angiogenic/CAF-linked tumors will lose part of their state identity in epithelial-only culture and recover it in co-culture.
  3. If driver-negative LUADs are stratified by glycolysis-high versus OXPHOS-high versus ER-stress-high states, each class will show distinct ex vivo drug sensitivity patterns that are more reproducible than sensitivity stratified only by broad-negative genomic status.
  4. If residual driver-negative early lesions are followed longitudinally, increases in ER-stress and stromal-coupling markers will precede invasive progression more often than acquisition of a new canonical RTK/RAS/RAF driver.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If attractor states are real dynamical basins, then CRISPR perturbation of different upstream lesions in unrelated driver-negative models should collapse onto the same single-cell manifold if and only if they belong to the same phospho-lineage-ecosystem attractor class.
  2. If lineage-ecosystem coupling is obligatory in a subset of true negatives, some tumors will be poorly transplantable as epithelial-only xenografts but robustly sustained in alveolar niche, vascularized, or humanized stromal systems.
  3. If metabolic persistence is a defining principle of residual negatives, plasma metabolite/secretome profiles may classify driver-negative LUAD subtypes more accurately than ctDNA mutation panels in recurrence monitoring.
  4. If recurrence preserves attractor identity better than genotype, post-therapy relapses will often keep the same phospho-lineage-ecosystem state despite acquiring new genomic lesions.

Negative Experiments

  1. Test whether phospho-state plus spatial ecosystem variables outperform genotype in predicting ex vivo drug response among high-confidence residual driver-negative LUAD. If genotype consistently dominates, the attractor framing is weakened.
  2. Disrupt CAF/endothelial support in ecosystem-dominant driver-negative models. If epithelial state and tumor fitness remain unchanged, the lineage-ecosystem coupling law is undermined.
  3. Normalize glycolysis, ferroptosis resistance, or ER-stress buffering in metabolic-state negatives. If tumors remain viable without compensatory rewiring, metabolic persistence is not central.
  4. Perform longitudinal multi-region single-cell profiling of residual negative lesions. If progression is usually explained by later emergence of canonical actionable drivers rather than state transitions, the theory overstates true attractor-driven disease.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Many subtype papers provide only correlative evidence; immune-high, angiogenesis-high, and some ecosystem states are not functionally proven to be causal in tumor maintenance. [e2408.2] [e2408.3] [e2438.1] [e2378.0] [e2398.0]
  2. Several broad-negative cohorts may still contain technical false negatives or incompletely screened canonical drivers, making it difficult to cleanly isolate the true residual state-driven population. [e2438.0] [e2373.0] [e2383.0] [e2415.0] [e2404.0]
  3. Some theories in the evidence point toward rare gene discovery rather than state convergence, especially RAF-family mutants, RIT1, NTRK1, NRG1 fusions, MET exon 14 skipping, and KIF5B-RET. [e2432.0] [e2381.0] [e2412.0] [e2410.0] [e2405.0] [e2394.0]
