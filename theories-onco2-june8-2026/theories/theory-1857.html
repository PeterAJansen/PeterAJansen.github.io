Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1857

Name: Convergent Noncanonical Driver Architecture in Oncogene-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: A large fraction of ostensibly oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinomas are not biologically driverless. They arise through several noncanonical architectures that converge on a limited set of actionable output states: hidden kinase activation, wild-type oncogene overexpression by structural/epigenomic rewiring or copy gain, and combinatorial tumor suppressor loss that recreates mitogenic signaling without a canonical hotspot mutation. The practical implication is that 'driver-negative' LUAD should be reclassified by signaling-output architecture rather than treated as a single residual category.

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: False-negative depletion law

Statement: When lung adenocarcinomas are classified as driver-negative using limited hotspot panels, incomplete fusion testing, or exome-only workflows, broader orthogonal profiling systematically converts a substantial subset into driver-positive cases by revealing hidden fusions, noncanonical coding mutations, exon-skipping events, or cryptic copy-number/structural lesions.

Domain/Scope: Applies to clinically or genomically classified oncogene-negative/driver-negative lung adenocarcinomas, especially cohorts defined by EGFR/KRAS/ALK-centered testing, fixed actionable panels, or WES without comprehensive WGS plus RNA-seq/fusion analysis. It is strongest in never-smoker or clinically oncogene-enriched populations and weaker in cohorts already deeply profiled by WGS, RNA-seq, and structural-variant-aware methods.

Special Cases

  1. The reclassification fraction depends strongly on tumor purity, allele fraction, RNA quality, structural-variant coverage, and whether amplifications are counted only when accompanied by overexpression.
  2. Residual WGS-negative cases remain after deep profiling, so this law does not imply that all driver-negative LUADs are assay artifacts.
  3. The law is strongest for tumors initially screened only for canonical drivers and may be much weaker in cohorts already assessed by broad hybrid-capture DNA plus RNA fusion testing.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. In never-smoker LUAD initially called oncogene-negative by WES, 29 of 84 tumors (35%) were negative at first pass, but after deeper sequencing and orthogonal clinical testing only 16 of 84 (19%) remained unresolved; lower tumor cellularity characterized initially negative tumors. [e1901.0]
  2. In residual RTK/RAS/RAF-negative TCGA LUAD, 85 tumors initially classified as negative by WES/RNA-seq were reanalyzed by WGS and 28 of 85 were reassigned as pathway-positive, showing that about one-third of apparent negatives were false negatives or undercalled canonical-pathway lesions. [e1864.0]
  3. The review-level synthesis of RTK/RAS/RAF-negative LUAD states that 20-30% of WES-negative LUADs become positive after WGS, with missed KRAS mutations, RTK amplifications, RASA1/NF1 deletions, and NRG1 fusions accounting for many reclassifications. [e1861.0]
  4. In a clinical 'triple-negative' EGFR/KRAS/ALK cohort of 51 LUAD patients, comprehensive genomic profiling identified genomic alterations in 94% and actionable alterations in 31%, including occult EGFR and KRAS mutations plus ROS1, RET, MET, and ERBB2 alterations. [e1849.0]
  5. Within that same standard-test-negative cohort, 8 of 51 cases had EGFR mutations and 8 of 51 had KRAS mutations newly discovered by CGP, indicating that many tumors labeled negative by routine assays were actually canonical-driver-positive. [e1849.1] [e1849.2]
  6. The same CGP-negative cohort contained 7 ROS1-rearranged cases discovered after negative conventional testing, and these patients subsequently showed strong response to ceritinib, confirming that at least some 'driver-negative' calls were missed actionable fusions. [e1849.3]
  7. Statement 1: Convergent output law

Statement: Distinct noncanonical lesion classes in oncogene-negative LUAD converge onto a small number of recurrent output states: (i) RTK/RAS/RAF-MAPK activation by rare mutations, fusions, exon skipping, or dosage gain; (ii) dual RAS plus PI3K activation by combined loss of negative regulators; (iii) lineage-survival transcriptional dependence; and (iv) enhancer- or dosage-driven overexpression of wild-type oncogenes.

Domain/Scope: Applies to human oncogene-negative LUAD and validated mouse/cell-line models intended to represent this disease, including residual RTK/RAS/RAF-negative subsets, EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD, pan-negative mucinous LUAD, and oncogene-negative mouse models driven by tumor suppressor loss. It does not claim that every driver-negative tumor maps uniquely to one state, only that these are recurrent attractors.

Special Cases

  1. Some tumors occupy mixed states, especially when structural activation, lineage state, and microenvironmental signaling coexist.
  2. Phosphoproteomic or transcriptomic outputs may partly reflect stromal or immune contributions rather than purely tumor-intrinsic biology.
  3. Residual WGS-negative tumors may remain outside these classes due to currently unmeasured noncoding, epigenetic, or post-translational mechanisms.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

Statement 2: Recurrence-risk concentration law

Statement: Within ostensibly driver-negative LUAD, recurrence and poor outcome concentrate in tumors that exhibit noncanonical activation architectures or aggressive state programs—such as enhancer/SV-driven outlier expression, proliferative or angiogenic proteogenomic states, or non-genetic solid-like progression states—rather than in mutation absence alone.

Domain/Scope: Applies mainly to resected or early-stage LUAD cohorts where recurrence-free or overall survival was related to noncanonical genomic, transcriptomic, radiomic, or proteogenomic features. It is not intended for heavily pretreated metastatic cohorts where treatment history dominates natural history.

Special Cases

  1. Some biologically potent noncanonical drivers may not be prognostic if completely resected or if they primarily influence drug sensitivity rather than intrinsic recurrence risk.
  2. This law pertains to risk concentration after subclassification of driver-negative tumors, not to the binary fact of being driver-negative.
  3. State-defined risk can coexist with canonical oncogene drivers, so the law is strongest in canonical-driver-depleted cohorts but not exclusive to them.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. In non-CAGA LUAD, outlier expression of a 23-gene SE-SV-associated set was linked to significantly worse recurrence-free survival, and FGF3/FGF4/FGF19 outliers were particularly associated with recurrence risk. [e1868.5]
  2. In never-smoker NS-LUAD, the chaotic subtype had no significantly enriched canonical driver mutations or fusions yet showed the worst prognosis, including strong adverse effects even in stage I disease. [e1850.0]
  3. In EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD, the proliferation-high and angiogenesis-high proteogenomic subtypes had worse outcomes than immune-high or metabolism groups despite not being defined by a single dominant actionable oncogene. [e1857.0] [e1861.5] [e1857.4]
  4. The histologic lepidic-to-solid transition model showed that solid-like L2S-high states carry poorer prognosis even though no recurrent pattern-specific genetic lesion was identified, demonstrating that aggressive progression can be state-driven rather than mutation-defined. [e1895.0] [e1895.1] [e1895.2]
  5. A driver gene-negative radiomics-defined LUAD cohort separated into high- and low-risk groups, with the high-risk group showing substantially worse survival and enrichment of immune-related pathways, indicating that biologically meaningful risk heterogeneity exists within the canonical driver-negative label. [e1880.0] [e1880.1]
  6. FeSig-high EGFR-wild-type LUAD had poorer overall survival and a fibroblast-rich, ferroptosis-resistant microenvironment, showing that adverse prognosis in canonical-driver-depleted disease can concentrate in state-defined metabolic/TME programs. [e1904.0] [e1904.4] [e1904.5] [e1904.6]

Statement 3: Therapeutic-axis law

Statement: Therapeutic vulnerabilities in oncogene-negative LUAD align more reliably with the activated axis of dependence than with the nominal lesion class: kinase-output tumors respond to matched kinase/MAPK blockade, dual RAS-PI3K tumors to vertical pathway combinations, enhancer-overexpression tumors to target engagement against the overexpressed wild-type receptor, and microenvironmental/metabolic state tumors to state-directed interventions.

Domain/Scope: Applies to preclinical and limited clinical evidence in oncogene-negative or canonical-driver-depleted LUAD and closely related NSCLC subsets. It is strongest where the causal axis has functional validation and weaker where evidence is only correlative or prognostic.

Special Cases

  1. Some lesion classes remain prognostic rather than therapeutically validated, so axis assignment may overcall actionability in under-tested subtypes.
  2. Drug response can be modified by lineage state or co-mutations even when the same axis is active.
  3. In some settings, the nominal lesion and the dependency axis coincide; the law matters most when they do not, such as wild-type receptor overexpression or combined suppressor loss.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. NTRK1 fusions were transforming and sensitive to TRKA-active inhibitors, including ARRY-470 and CEP-701, showing that hidden kinase-output tumors can be treated by pathway-matched kinase blockade. [e1885.0] [e1885.1] [e1885.2] [e1885.3]
  2. RAF-family CR2 hotspot mutations enhanced MEK/ERK signaling and were inhibited by sorafenib and trametinib; the index ARAF S214C patient had a prolonged sorafenib response, validating axis-directed therapy for rare noncanonical MAPK lesions. [e1888.0] [e1888.1] [e1888.2] [e1854.3]
  3. RET fusions such as KIF5B-RET transformed model cells and were inhibited by RET-active multikinase inhibitors or vandetanib in preclinical systems, supporting treatment by the activated kinase axis rather than by the initial negative clinical label. [e1899.0] [e1899.1] [e1843.2] [e1869.0] [e1910.0]
  4. NRG1 fusions activated HER2/HER3 signaling and this output was suppressed by lapatinib and afatinib, showing that ligand-fusion tumors should be treated through their downstream receptor axis rather than as a miscellaneous fusion class. [e1915.2] [e1915.6] [e1876.0] [e1867.1]
  5. EZR-ERBB4 and TRIM24-BRAF fusion models were inhibited by HER-family or RAF/MEK-directed inhibitors respectively, reinforcing that noncanonical fusions are therapeutically organized by activated output pathway. [e1915.3] [e1915.4]
  6. The oncogene-negative RAS/PI3K mouse and cell-line models responded to SHP2 inhibition, and combined SHP2 plus AKT inhibition produced the strongest anti-tumor effects, directly supporting vertical pathway therapy for dual-output suppressor-loss tumors. [e1863.0] [e1863.4]
  7. ERBB2 overexpression driven by super-enhancer/structural rearrangement in non-CAGA LUAD responded in PDX models to poziotinib and trastuzumab deruxtecan despite lack of canonical ERBB2 mutation, validating target engagement against a structurally overexpressed wild-type receptor. [e1868.1] [e1868.0] [e1868.6]
  8. FeSig-high EGFR-wild-type LUAD responded preferentially to 6-mercaptopurine, with tumor suppression and reduced fibroblast infiltration in sensitive xenografts, supporting state-directed therapy for a metabolic/fibroblast-defined rather than mutation-defined subgroup. [e1904.0] [e1904.4] [e1904.5] [e1904.6]
  9. RASA1/NF1 co-inactivated NSCLC showed marked trametinib sensitivity and additional sensitivity to combination strategies, consistent with axis-based treatment of RAS-hyperactivated suppressor-loss disease. [e1911.0] [e1911.2] [e1911.3]

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If a rigorously defined modern driver-negative LUAD cohort is profiled by joint WGS, long-read SV calling, RNA-seq, phosphoproteomics, and H3K27ac profiling, more than half of currently unresolved cases will segregate into one of four output classes: kinase-output, dual RAS/PI3K, lineage-survival, or enhancer-overexpression.
  2. Among tumors with high phospho-ERK plus high phospho-AKT and evidence of impaired NF1/RASA1/PTEN axis integrity, SHP2 plus AKT inhibition will outperform either monotherapy in organoids or PDXs.
  3. A subset of tumors with very high ERBB2, EGFR, MET, or FGFR protein but no coding activation will show local structural rewiring or focal enhancer gain rather than random overexpression.
  4. Recurrence after resection in canonical-driver-depleted LUAD will be predicted better by architecture/state class assignment than by the binary label of driver-negative alone.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Some residual WGS-negative LUADs will be driven mainly by allele-specific promoter/enhancer dysfunction in lineage or stress-response genes and will only be detectable by single-cell multiome or allele-resolved epigenomics.
  2. A fraction of unresolved driver-negative LUADs will prove to be maintained by ecDNA-mediated amplification of wild-type receptors or transcription factors without stable chromosomal amplification, creating sensitivity to transcriptional hub disruption.
  3. Some driver-negative LUADs will be sustained by paracrine ligand circuits from fibroblasts or immune cells that maintain RTK phosphorylation without tumor-intrinsic kinase lesions; interrupting the ligand source will outperform tumor-cell-targeted kinase inhibition in those cases.
  4. There exists a cryptic subset of LUAD in which the decisive driver state is detectable only at phosphosite level, with little or no corresponding DNA, RNA, or total-protein abnormality.

Negative Experiments

  1. Profile a modern driver-negative LUAD cohort with comprehensive WGS, RNA-seq, long-read SV, phosphoproteomics, and H3K27ac; if most tumors still fail to map into recurrent output classes and show no convergence in signaling state, the theory is weakened.
  2. Compare therapy prediction by lesion label versus output-axis label in preclinical models and retrospective clinical cohorts; if axis-based assignment does not improve response prediction, the therapeutic-axis statement is weakened.
  3. In enhancer-overexpression candidates, disrupt the predicted enhancer-rewired contacts by CRISPRi or boundary restoration; if oncogene expression and tumor fitness do not decrease, the enhancer-overexpression branch is weakened.
  4. In dual RAS/PI3K candidates, restore RASA1/NF1/PTEN function or suppress both outputs; if growth is not dependent on combined pathway signaling, the convergence claim is weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not fully capture fibroblast-rich ferroptosis-resistant EGFR-wild-type LUAD where the dominant organizing biology may be TME- and ferroptosis-state-driven rather than one of the four principal genomic architectures. [e1904.0] [e1904.4] [e1904.5]
  2. The theory only partially explains precursor-state findings such as AIS3 tip-endothelial/mCAF programs and KAC transitional alveolar states, which may act before stable noncanonical driver architecture is established. [e1890.1] [e1898.1] [e1898.3] [e1898.4]
  3. The theory does not resolve whether mutational-process-defined subsets such as SBS40a-dominant or aristolochic-acid-associated never-smoker LUAD are purely etiologic states or true mechanistic driver classes. [e1914.0] [e1914.1] [e1914.5]
