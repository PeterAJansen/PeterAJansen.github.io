Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1852

Name: Multi-Layer Hidden Driver Theory of Oncogene-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: Oncogene-negative LUAD is not one disease but a mixture of mechanistically distinct driver classes distributed across assay layers. One class consists of canonical drivers missed for technical reasons; another consists of rare coding drivers outside routine panels; a third consists of structural/splice drivers requiring fusion- or RNA-aware assays; and a fourth consists of noncoding, copy-number, regulatory, proteomic, or phospho-signaling drivers that may show little or no explanatory signal at the DNA hotspot level. The apparent 30% 'driver-negative' fraction therefore reflects both assay incompleteness and genuine biological diversification beyond the RTK/RAS/RAF hotspot model.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Apparent driver negativity decomposes into assay-missed canonical, rare coding, structural/splice, and noncoding/proteomic driver classes

Statement: A lung adenocarcinoma called oncogene-negative after routine testing can belong to at least four mechanistic classes: (1) canonical drivers missed because of assay coverage, purity, or sampling; (2) rare coding drivers outside the standard panel; (3) structural or splice-altering drivers requiring RNA-, FISH-, or WGS-aware detection; and (4) regulatory/protein-state drivers whose oncogenic effect is not visible in hotspot DNA calls. Therefore, driver negativity should be treated as a provisional assay-state rather than a biologically uniform category.

Domain/Scope: Applies to lung adenocarcinoma cohorts initially defined as EGFR/KRAS/ALK-negative, RTK/RAS/RAF-negative, or panel-negative by targeted genotyping, WES, or limited fusion testing. It is most applicable to cohorts where broader orthogonal profiling was subsequently performed.

Special Cases

  1. A residual subset may remain unexplained even after broad profiling and may represent truly unresolved biology rather than assay failure.
  2. Some tumors contain more than one detectable driver-class lesion or a canonical driver plus a cooperative suppressor-loss state.
  3. The relative sizes of the four classes depend on the starting assay.

Statement 1: Structural and splicing assays deliver the highest marginal gain once hotspot DNA testing is exhausted

Statement: After routine hotspot mutation testing is negative, the next largest yield in hidden-driver discovery comes from assays sensitive to rearrangement, exon-skipping, and structural copy-number architecture—specifically RNA fusion calling, FISH, splice-aware analysis, and WGS—because these modalities recover both canonical and noncanonical drivers that are disproportionately missed by exon-focused testing.

Domain/Scope: Applies to panel-negative or WES-negative LUAD, especially never-smoker and low-purity cohorts, and to tumors lacking EGFR/KRAS/ALK-class lesions on first-pass testing. It is less directly applicable when broad RNA/WGS profiling was already part of the initial workflow.

Special Cases

  1. If the initial assay already includes deep RNA fusion analysis and structural variant calling, the marginal value of additional FISH/WGS may be smaller.
  2. Very low-expression fusions or very low-purity samples may still escape RNA-based detection and require orthogonal DNA or FISH assays.
  3. Some residual cases will require noncoding or proteomic analyses rather than more structural testing.

Statement 2: A residual biologic subset is driver-negative not because of missing sequence lesions alone, but because the causal state is regulatory, suppressor-combinatorial, or proteomic

Statement: After canonical, rare coding, and structural/splice rescues are removed, the remaining unresolved oncogene-negative LUADs are enriched for mechanisms in which no single activating proto-oncogene event is sufficient or visible: combinatorial suppressor loss activating RAS/PI3K, promoter/enhancer lesions, methylation-driven cis effects, protein-state or phospho-state outliers, and stemness/lineage-state programs. In these tumors, the driver is a systems-state rather than a single hotspot lesion.

Domain/Scope: Applies to the unresolved remainder after broad genomic rescue in oncogene-negative LUAD, including non-CAGA tumors, oncogene-negative RAS/PI3K tumors, proteogenomically profiled never-smoker LUAD, and genomically quiet piano-like tumors. It does not claim that all residual cases share one exact mechanism.

Special Cases

  1. Some residual tumors may still harbor undiscovered structural or noncoding events rather than true systems-state drivers.
  2. The statement encompasses several sub-mechanisms and should not be interpreted as a single homogeneous subtype.
  3. Some systems-state drivers may coexist with weak or partial canonical drivers.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. In a prospective driver-negative LUAD workflow, adding RNA fusion/splice analysis and WGS-based structural reconstruction after a negative hotspot panel will rescue more cases than simply expanding the hotspot panel to additional rare coding genes.
  2. Tumors remaining negative after broad structural rescue will show enrichment for pathway-state abnormalities such as PTEN protein loss, pERK/pAKT activation, or phosphoproteomic outliers even when no single activating coding event is identified.
  3. Low-purity and low-cellularity samples will be overrepresented among first-pass driver-negative tumors that are later rescued as harboring canonical drivers.
  4. Integrated methylation-plus-proteomics profiling of residual driver-negative LUAD will identify a recurrent subset with cis-regulatory silencing or activation not apparent from DNA mutation calls.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. A classifier combining assay history, tumor purity, histology, RNA fusion features, and phosphoproteomics will partition driver-negative LUAD into the four hidden-driver classes with clinically meaningful therapeutic prediction.
  2. Some currently unresolved driver-negative tumors will prove to depend on noncoding regulatory lesions that are clonally stable but invisible to short-read pipelines, and long-read plus chromatin-conformation assays will reveal them.
  3. Residual systems-state driver-negative LUADs will respond better to pathway-combination therapy selected by phosphoproteomics (for example SHP2 plus AKT, or NRF2-context therapies) than to any genotype-matched monotherapy.
  4. A measurable proportion of 'driver-negative' LUADs will have no single essential lesion even after complete genomic characterization and will instead require state-collapse therapies that alter lineage or transcriptional circuitry.

Negative Experiments

  1. If broad orthogonal profiling does not divide driver-negative LUAD into distinct rescued classes but instead reveals one dominant hidden mechanism, the first statement would be weakened.
  2. If expanded hotspot panels consistently rescue more negative tumors than fusion/splice/structural assays after first-pass negativity, the second statement would be weakened.
  3. If residual negative tumors show no enrichment for proteomic, phospho-signaling, methylation, or suppressor-combinatorial states compared with driver-positive tumors, the third statement would be weakened.
  4. If low-purity samples are not overrepresented among first-pass negative tumors later rescued as canonical-driver-positive, the assay-missed canonical component would be less important than proposed.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory is broad and does not by itself predict which specific hidden-driver class a given tumor will belong to without additional subtype information such as histology, smoking status, or proteomic state.
  2. Some studies defining driver-negative cohorts did not perform comprehensive enough follow-up to determine which residual cases were truly unexplained versus simply under-profiled. [e2045.1] [e2072.0] [e2129.0]
