Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1875

Name: Hidden Lesion Hierarchy Theory of Apparently Driver-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: Most clinically 'driver-negative' LUAD is not biologically driverless but reflects a hierarchy of hidden lesion classes missed by routine testing. The hierarchy is ordered by detectability: first, rare canonical coding drivers and overlooked domain variants; second, hidden fusions and exon-skipping events; third, focal amplifications and complex structural variants; fourth, noncoding enhancer-hijacking or super-enhancer mechanisms; and finally, a smaller residual class dominated by tumor-suppressor co-loss or pathway-state biology. This theory explains why broader DNA/RNA/structural/proteogenomic assays steadily shrink the negative pool and why residual negatives are enriched for noncoding regulation, chromosomal instability, and state-based dependencies.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Assay-depth rescue law

Statement: As molecular interrogation expands from hotspot panels to broad DNA/RNA/structural/noncoding analyses, the size of the 'driver-negative' LUAD pool monotonically decreases because many negatives are actually hidden-driver positives in less commonly assayed lesion classes.

Domain/Scope: Applies to clinically or research-defined driver-negative LUAD cohorts where sequential testing depth can be compared, including EGFR/KRAS/ALK-negative, RTK/RAS/RAF-negative, pan-negative mucinous LUAD, and never-smoker cohorts. It is most accurate in cohorts initially defined by limited panels and less dramatic in cohorts already assayed by WGS plus RNA-seq.

Special Cases

  1. The rescue rate depends strongly on the initial panel; broad WGS/RNA-defined negatives will show smaller rescue fractions than hotspot-panel negatives.
  2. Some cohorts confound true biological negativity with low purity or technical false negatives.
  3. Not every residual case is rescued even after WGS/RNA-seq; the law concerns majority trend, not completeness.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Comprehensive genomic profiling of EGFR/KRAS/ALK-negative LUAD reclassified many apparently triple-negative tumors by finding additional actionable mutations, fusions, amplifications, and deletions, with clinical benefit especially for ROS1-rearranged patients. [e2372.0]
  2. In never-smoker LUAD, many WES-defined oncogene-negative tumors were later reassigned after deeper or orthogonal testing, including ALK and ROS1 fusions, EGFR/ERBB2/KRAS mutations, MET exon 14 skipping, and rare EGFR and AKT1 lesions; low tumor cellularity contributed to false negatives. [e2438.0]
  3. Transcriptome sequencing of initial EGFR/KRAS/EML4-ALK-negative LUAD found additional EGFR point mutations, novel kinase fusions, and MET exon 14 skipping, demonstrating that initial negativity was often incomplete. [e2373.0]
  4. In S4-low and other broad-negative LADC cohorts, many apparently negative tumors were resolved by WGS/RNA-seq as fusion-oncogene or canonical-driver positive, leaving only a small residual set without known major oncogene alterations. [e2377.0] [e2477.0] [e2461.0]
  5. Whole-genome characterization of RTK/RAS/RAF-negative LUAD rescued 28 of 85 initially negative cases by identifying KRAS hotspot mutations, amplifications of EGFR/MAPK1/ARAF/SOS1, NF1/RASA1 deletions, and NRG1 fusion/mutation events. [e2383.0]
  6. Pan-negative Chinese LUAD cases were later rescued by deeper testing that identified EGFR extracellular-domain mutations, HER2 extracellular/transmembrane mutations, ARAF mutation, and CD74-NRG1 fusions. [e2386.0] [e2369.0] [e2432.0] [e2386.1] [e2386.2] [e2386.3]
  7. KRAS-negative/pan-negative invasive mucinous adenocarcinomas were reclassified by RNA-seq/RT-PCR/FISH as harboring NRG1, ERBB4, BRAF, and RET fusions, showing that histology-enriched negative cohorts can be strongly rescueable. [e2423.0] [e2410.0]
  8. NTRK1 rearrangements were discovered only after broader targeted DNA/FISH testing in clinically negative LUAD, showing that rare but actionable fusion drivers can reside beyond standard panels. [e2412.0]
  9. Rare RAF-family mutants (ARAF S214C and RAF1 S257/S259) were found in otherwise driver-negative LUAD after broad WGS/RNA analysis, with functional and therapeutic relevance. [e2432.0] [e2432.1]
  10. This law is induced directly from the repeated observation across multiple studies that deeper testing converts many negatives into positives, while the residual pool becomes smaller and biologically different.

Statement 1: Hidden lesion hierarchy law

Statement: The residual 'driver-negative' pool is structured by lesion class rather than by one missing pathway: rare coding oncogenes and noncanonical domain mutations are usually found first, hidden fusions and exon-skipping events second, focal amplifications/complex SVs third, noncoding enhancer-hijacking events fourth, and only then does a smaller residual class remain that is dominated by tumor-suppressor co-loss or unresolved pathway state.

Domain/Scope: Applies to LUAD cohorts with sufficiently broad lesion detection across coding mutations, fusions, splice events, CNAs/SVs, and noncoding structural/regulatory mechanisms. It is less reliable in studies that examine only one lesion class.

Special Cases

  1. Some lesions can belong to multiple classes, such as ligand fusions or amplifications with enhancer architecture.
  2. The hierarchy is about practical discovery order and residual frequency, not strict biological chronology in tumor evolution.
  3. Certain histologic subsets, especially invasive mucinous adenocarcinoma, are enriched for fusion/lipid-ligand classes earlier in the hierarchy.

Statement 2: Residual negative law

Statement: After exhaustive coding, fusion, splice, and structural interrogation, the remaining true driver-negative LUADs are enriched for tumor-suppressor co-loss, chromosomal instability, epigenetic or allele-specific dysregulation, and proteomic pathway-state dependence rather than for undiscovered high-frequency canonical oncogenes.

Domain/Scope: Applies to high-confidence residual negatives after broad WGS/RNA-seq/structural analysis, not to panel-negative cohorts before rescue. It is most relevant to RTK/RAS/RAF-negative LUAD, piano/chaotic-like never-smoker disease, and non-CAGA cohorts after multi-omic analysis.

Special Cases

  1. A minority of residual negatives may still harbor undiscovered rare coding or regulatory drivers not yet detectable.
  2. Some residual cohorts are still contaminated by technical false negatives due to purity or coverage issues.
  3. This law concerns enrichment, not absolute exclusivity; residual negatives can still contain rare private oncogenic events.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Residual WGS-negative RTK/RAS/RAF-negative LUAD was characterized by high TP53 mutation frequency, KEAP1 and SMARCA4 enrichment, high TMB, complex structural variation, focal deletions in STK11/KEAP1/TP53, and recurrent ILF2 promoter mutations associated with higher expression and SV burden. [e2383.0] [e2383.1]
  2. Piano tumors in never-smokers rarely harbored known recurrent drivers and instead showed low SCNAs, low TMB, long telomeres, high heterogeneity, and stemness/quiescence-exit features, suggesting a residual class not dominated by canonical oncogenes. [e2406.0] [e2406.2]
  3. The chaotic NS-LUAD subtype lacked enrichment of known drivers yet showed EMT, mixed-lineage features, and CAF-rich ecology, indicating that some residual negatives are better explained by state programs than by one missing kinase mutation. [e2389.0]
  4. CN-independent allele-specific expression in LUAD was associated with hypomethylation and mutations in chromatin modifiers such as SETD2, KDM5C, and KMT2B, supporting an epigenomic-dysfunction layer in residual unexplained tumors. [e2459.2] [e2459.3] [e2479.3] [e2479.4] [e2479.5]
  5. The hallmarks study proposed epigenetic and RNA deregulation as a missing explanatory hallmark for tumors not accounted for by canonical driver/hallmark mapping, explicitly nominating recurrent alterations in RBM10, U2AF1, ARID1A, SETD2, BRD3, and SMARCA4. [e2429.0] [e2429.2]
  6. Non-CAGA LUAD was explained by MAML2 enhancer loss and hypermethylation with broad transcriptomic consequences, showing that some residual negatives are hidden regulatory/epigenetic diseases rather than unrecognized hotspot-mutation diseases. [e2460.0] [e2460.1] [e2474.0]
  7. Oncogene-negative LUAD models and reviews repeatedly highlighted tumor-suppressor co-loss (NF1/RASA1/PTEN, RASA1/NF1, STK11/KEAP1, CDKN2A/RB1-related states) as a plausible central mechanism in residual negative disease. [e2392.0] [e2399.0] [e2422.0] [e2480.2] [e2454.0]
  8. This law is induced by noting that once broad lesion rescue is exhausted, the recurring explanatory motifs are instability, epigenetic dysregulation, tumor-suppressor loss, and pathway-state dependence, not one dominant new oncogene family.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If a clinically panel-negative LUAD cohort is re-profiled sequentially by broad DNA panel, RNA fusion/splice detection, WGS/SV calling, and noncoding enhancer mapping, the rescued cases will appear in the predicted order: rare coding lesions first, then fusions/splice events, then CNA/SV events, then regulatory events.
  2. If residual negatives after WGS/RNA-seq are compared with panel-negative but not deeply profiled tumors, the residual group will show higher rates of tumor-suppressor loss, chromatin dysregulation, complex SVs, and pathway-state dependence and lower rates of rescued kinase fusions.
  3. If invasive mucinous adenocarcinoma negatives are analyzed separately, their rescue fraction by RNA fusion discovery will exceed that of unselected broad-negative LUAD.
  4. If low-purity negative tumors are re-biopsied or sequenced at higher depth, a meaningful fraction will move upward in the hierarchy from 'residual negative' into coding/fusion/CNA classes.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If the hierarchy is biologically real and not only technical, each successive residual tier will have a distinct evolutionary architecture—coding-rescued tumors will be trunk-driver dominated, whereas final residuals will show more parallel evolution, ASE, and epigenetic diversity.
  2. If enhancer-hijacking is a major late-tier mechanism in residual negatives, prospective H3K27ac/Hi-C/long-read profiling of high-confidence residual-negative LUAD could reveal a clinically actionable rate of regulatory oncogene activation comparable to rare fusion frequencies.
  3. If the final residual class is truly suppressor/state dominated, ctDNA mutation assays may systematically underperform compared with phosphoproteomics or expression-state assays in therapy selection for that tier.
  4. If the lesion hierarchy reflects therapy evolution as well as diagnosis, resistant recurrences after targeted therapy may 'descend' the hierarchy, shifting from canonical oncogene dependence toward suppressor-loss/regulatory/state-based disease.

Negative Experiments

  1. Prospectively apply full multi-layer profiling to a broad panel-negative LUAD cohort. If the negative pool does not shrink materially with increasing assay depth, the theory is weakened.
  2. Test whether residual negatives after broad WGS/RNA are actually enriched for suppressor-loss/epigenetic/state features. If they instead reveal many undiscovered recurrent canonical oncogenes, the terminal-law component is wrong.
  3. Measure whether rescue events appear in the predicted hierarchy order across cohorts. If noncoding enhancer events are discovered as frequently and as early as coding lesions in standard negatives, the hierarchy needs revision.
  4. Control rigorously for purity and coverage. If most residual-negative explanations disappear after technical correction alone, the hierarchy overstates biological residual disease.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some studies define negative status very narrowly, such as EGFR-only or EGFR/ALK-only exclusion, making them hard to place cleanly within a universal hierarchy. [e2420.0] [e2457.0] [e2457.1] [e2457.2] [e2408.0]
  2. A few candidate mechanisms remain weakly supported and may not fit stably into the hierarchy, such as JAK2 V617F, some outlier-expression events without functional validation, and broad immune-only prognostic signatures. [e2441.2] [e2407.0] [e2416.0]
