Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1859

Name: Residual Ecosystem-Driven LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: After exhaustive subtraction of hidden canonical and rare genomic drivers, a substantial residual fraction of oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinoma is best explained as an ecosystem-maintained disease state rather than a single-lesion disease. In these tumors, stable combinations of lineage plasticity, stromal/vascular remodeling, immune architecture, and metabolic stress programs create self-reinforcing attractors that substitute for classical kinase addiction and determine prognosis and therapy response.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Residual state-dominance law

Statement: In residual driver-negative LUAD that remains unresolved after broad DNA/RNA profiling, tumor aggressiveness, recurrence risk, and progression are more consistently organized by transcriptional state and microenvironmental architecture than by recurrent shared genomic lesions.

Domain/Scope: Applies to lung adenocarcinoma cohorts explicitly or approximately depleted of canonical drivers, especially residual groups after comprehensive sequencing, never-smoker EGFR/ALK-wild-type cohorts, early-stage progression cohorts, and histologic-pattern/spatially profiled LUAD. Does not apply to tumors ultimately explained by clear kinase fusions, hotspot mutations, or focal oncogene activation after deeper profiling.

Special Cases

  1. This law excludes tumors reclassified by deeper profiling as RET, NTRK1, NRG1, MET exon 14, EGFR ECD, RAF-family, RIT1, ERBB2, or other rare genomic driver-positive cases.
  2. Some state programs may still be initiated or weakly constrained by noncanonical genomic events, so the law concerns dominant explanatory structure, not absolute genomic absence.
  3. The law is strongest for residual unresolved subsets, not for all LUAD.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  2. Histologic progression from lepidic to solid was not explained by recurrent pattern-specific mutations, copy-number events, or fusions; instead, progression followed a transcriptional and methylation continuum captured by the L2S signature and associated with prognosis across more than 2,000 LUADs. [e1895.0] [e1895.1] [e1895.2]

In never-smoker NS-LUAD, the chaotic subtype had no significantly enriched canonical driver mutations or fusions, yet showed the worst prognosis, strong EMT/cell-movement programs, mixed lineage, and abundant CAFs, indicating that poor-outcome biology can be organized without recurrent canonical genomic enrichment. EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD segregated into four proteogenomic states (proliferation, immune, angiogenesis, metabolism) with distinct outcomes, pathways, and vulnerabilities, reinforcing that residual non-EGFR/ALK disease is heterogeneous and state-structured rather than a single hidden-driver entity. Statement 1: Lineage-permissive initiation law

Statement: A subset of residual driver-negative or driver-negative-appearing LUAD originates from transitional or lineage-reprogrammed epithelial states that are not necessarily sufficient oncogenic lesions themselves but create high-plasticity, high-tolerance cellular substrates on which invasion, persistence, and later stabilization occur.

Domain/Scope: Applies to early-stage LUAD, never-smoker LUAD, precursor-to-invasive transitions, and lineage-reprogrammed or transitional epithelial contexts such as KRT8-positive alveolar intermediates, AIS3/MIA transitions, and NKX2-1-loss-associated mucinous/gastric programs. Not intended for clearly mutation-addicted kinase-fusion tumors.

Special Cases

  1. The transitional state may be obligatory in some developmental trajectories and optional in others.
  2. In some tumors, lineage states may be consequences of genomic lesions such as KRAS or NKX2-1 loss rather than fully prior conditions.
  3. Some transitional states may be field effects in adjacent lung rather than autonomous malignant clones.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. KRT8-positive alveolar intermediate cells (KACs) were tumor-proximal, less differentiated, enriched in tumor-adjacent normal regions, associated with KRAS signatures and poor survival, detected before overt tumors in carcinogen-exposed mice, and lineage-linked to tumors, supporting a transitional cell state permissive for tumorigenesis. [e1898.1] [e1898.2] [e1898.3] [e1898.4] [e1844.1]
  2. AIS3 represented a late preinvasive state associated with non-pGGO radiology, tip endothelial cells, matrix CAFs, angiogenesis, EMT, and proposed transition toward invasion, indicating that preinvasive progression is organized by a transitional ecosystem rather than a newly shown driver mutation. [e1890.1] [e1890.2]
  3. MIA_nonInv and paired MIA_Inv subtypes showed that noninvasive transcriptomic states already encode invasive-state organization and endothelial/stromal shifts, suggesting progression can be prefigured by state transitions before frank invasion. [e1890.2]
  4. NKX2-1 loss in Kras-driven mouse lung tumors induced gastric-lineage/mucinous reprogramming rather than a simple proliferative phenotype; human NKX2-1-negative mucinous LUAD expressed gastric markers, showing that lineage-state conversion can define oncogenic behavior. The piano subtype of never-smoker lung cancer was genomically quiet, stemness-like, long-telomere, high-ITH, and often lacked recurrent canonical drivers; the authors explicitly hypothesized quiescence-exit biology involving ARID1A/NOTCH1-like regulators as a mechanism for tumors with no detected known drivers.   6. The quiescence-exit hypothesis proposed that mutations in ARID1A, NOTCH1, or similar genes could drive tumors lacking known cancer-driver mutations or fusions by promoting exit from quiescence and increasing intratumor heterogeneity. [e1887.4]
  7. The MIF+AT2 single-cell population in driver gene-negative LUAD showed elevated glycolysis and malignancy-associated features, implying that a specific altered epithelial state exists within this clinically driver-negative setting. [e1848.0] [e1848.1] [e1848.3]
  8. This law was logically inferred through abduction from the coexistence of preinvasive transitional states, tumor-adjacent KAC field states, and lineage-reprogrammed mucinous states that repeatedly precede or define malignant behavior without requiring one shared recurrent genomic driver.

Statement 2: Microenvironmental lock-in law

Statement: Once residual driver-negative LUAD enters a high-risk state, stromal fibroblasts, tip endothelial programs, suppressive immune topology, and ligand-mediated intercellular signaling stabilize that state and often become more predictive of outcome and therapy response than any single tumor-cell mutation.

Domain/Scope: Applies to aggressive residual noncanonical LUAD states with strong stromal, vascular, or immune architecture, including FeSig-high EGFR-wild-type tumors, chaotic never-smoker LUAD, AIS3/MIA transition states, and solid/L2S-high lesions. Not intended for purely cell-autonomous fusion-driven tumors with weak stromal dependence.

Special Cases

  1. Immune-hot subtypes may still benefit from immunotherapy despite high-risk state features.
  2. Different high-risk tumors may be locked by different dominant axes: CAF-IGF signaling, endothelial/tip-EC programs, or immune exclusion.
  3. Some stromal signatures may partly reflect reactive response rather than causal maintenance.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. FeSig-high EGFR-wild-type LUAD showed abundant fibroblasts, stronger fibroblast-epithelial communication, a negative correlation between communication score and ferroptosis score, higher IGF-binding pathway activity, and worse survival; 6-MP reduced both tumor growth and alpha-SMA-positive fibroblast infiltration, indicating a stromal maintenance axis. [e1904.0] [e1904.4] [e1904.5] [e1904.6]
  2. Single-cell and bulk analyses showed fibroblasts correlated positively with FeSig across multiple datasets, while fibroblast-rich FeSig-high tumors had nest-like CAF barriers and stronger ligand-receptor communication. [e1904.4] [e1904.5]
  3. AIS3 and invasive MIA regions were enriched for tip endothelial cells and matrix CAFs, and spatial transcriptomics confirmed endothelial enrichment in invasive regions, supporting an angiogenic-stromal lock-in mechanism before invasion. [e1890.1] [e1890.2]
  4. Solid histologic LUAD regions showed immune exclusion with immune cells concentrated at the periphery and residual core infiltrates enriched for suppressive markers such as FOXP3, CD25, Tim3, CTLA4, VISTA, and ICOS, indicating that aggressive state can be maintained by microenvironmental architecture. [e1895.2]
  5. The chaotic never-smoker LUAD subtype had high CAF abundance, EMT/cell-movement programs, and poor prognosis without significantly enriched canonical drivers, consistent with microenvironment-dependent state maintenance. [e1850.0]
  6. The angiogenesis-high EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD subtype was enriched for CAF and angiogenic ligands such as FGF2, CXCL12, PDGFB, and LGALS3, reinforcing that a stromal-vascular program can define a noncanonical disease state. [e1857.0] [e1861.5]
  7. The STK11-mutant immune-cold subtype showed low immune infiltration and a neutrophil-degranulation proteomic program, providing another example of a state stabilized by immune-microenvironment configuration rather than a single druggable oncogene. [e1900.4]
  8. This law was logically inferred from the repeated association between fibroblast/endothelial/immune topology and prognosis across multiple noncanonical LUAD cohorts, plus the therapeutic reversibility of at least one fibroblast-rich state.

Statement 3: Residual state-therapy law

Statement: In residual driver-negative LUAD, the most productive therapies will preferentially target state-maintenance circuitry—metabolic stress adaptation, ferroptosis resistance, stromal ligands, angiogenic support, lineage-state regulators, or immune topology—rather than relying exclusively on continued search for a single hidden kinase lesion.

Domain/Scope: Applies after broad genomic, fusion, copy-number, and splice-event profiling has failed to reveal a clear tractable genomic driver. Best suited to residual unresolved tumors displaying reproducible state signatures or ecosystem-defined subtypes.

Special Cases

  1. Comprehensive profiling should precede state-directed therapy, because many apparently driver-negative tumors are later reclassified as rare genomic-driver-positive.
  2. Some state vulnerabilities may require combinations with chemotherapy, immunotherapy, or pathway inhibitors.
  3. This law does not imply kinase discovery should stop; it implies therapy should branch once residual status is confirmed.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. 6-MP showed preferential efficacy in FeSig-high EGFR-wild-type LUAD, with stronger activity in A549 than H3122 and reduction of fibroblast infiltration in vivo, demonstrating that a state-defined metabolic/stromal program can be therapeutically actionable without a new canonical driver lesion. [e1904.0] [e1904.6] [e1904.4]
  2. In driver gene-negative LUAD, MIF-HIF-1alpha activation increased glycolysis and proliferation in AT2 cells, while the MIF inhibitor ISO-1 reversed these effects, showing that a metabolic-state node can be directly perturbed in a canonical-driver-negative context. [e1848.0] [e1848.1] [e1848.2] [e1848.3]
  3. EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD subtypes mapped to subtype-specific dependencies (CDK9, TRAF2, GRB2, ACACA) and PRISM-aligned candidate drugs, supporting the principle that state-defined vulnerabilities can be mapped independently of canonical driver identity. [e1857.0] [e1857.4] [e1861.5]
  4. The immune-risk signature in driver-negative LUAD separated tumors by checkpoint expression and predicted therapy-response differences, implying that treatment allocation can be guided by state composition even when no new oncogene is identified. [e1879.0] [e1879.1]
  5. The radiomics nomogram in driver-negative LUAD linked low-risk tumors to metabolic pathways and high-risk tumors to immune-related pathways, suggesting orthogonal non-genomic treatment axes within the driver-negative clinical group. [e1880.0] [e1880.1]
  6. TTF1-negative LUAD and STK11/FGL1-rich subtype analyses suggested sensitivity to DNA-repair targeting, mTOR inhibition, docetaxel, or LAG3/FGL1-axis intervention based on proteogenomic state rather than canonical driver mutation. [e1861.3] [e1896.0]
  7. This law was logically inferred through induction from multiple examples where residual or noncanonical LUAD subtypes yielded actionable dependencies without discovery of a unifying hidden oncogene.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If residual driver-negative LUADs are clustered jointly by spatial transcriptomics, phosphoproteomics, and cell-cell communication scores, subtype reproducibility will exceed clustering by private DNA lesions alone.
  2. In residual driver-negative LUAD organoid-fibroblast cocultures, perturbing CAF-epithelial ligand axes or ferroptosis-resistance programs will reduce growth more often than broad RTK inhibitor screening.
  3. Adjacent histologically normal lung from residual driver-negative cases will more often contain KAC-like, AIS3-like, or lineage-reprogrammed epithelial states than adjacent lung from clear fusion-driven tumors.
  4. In residual driver-negative resected tumors, prognosis will correlate more strongly with fibroblast abundance, endothelial-tip scores, immune-exclusion topology, or ferroptosis-resistance signatures than with total mutation burden alone.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Forced restoration of pulmonary lineage stability, such as reactivation of NKX2-1-like differentiation programs in selected residual tumors, will collapse some driver-negative states even without targeting a conventional oncogene.
  2. A subset of residual driver-negative LUAD will depend on airway microbiome-derived inflammatory metabolites that reinforce MIF-HIF-1alpha or fibroblast-IGF signaling; antibiotic or microbiome-modulating intervention will selectively alter state stability.
  3. Longitudinal ctDNA methylation plus circulating protein measurements will reveal preinvasive tipping points corresponding to state transitions (for example KAC-like to invasive or fibroblast-rich lock-in) before radiographic invasion becomes apparent.
  4. Residual driver-negative LUADs will show mechanically dependent survival such that removal of alveolar stromal geometry or matrix stiffness cues causes disproportionate collapse ex vivo relative to clear oncogene-driven comparators.

Negative Experiments

  1. If residual comprehensively profiled driver-negative LUADs cluster more reproducibly by recurrent genomic lesions than by ecosystem/state variables, the central state-dominance law is weakened.
  2. If perturbation of MIF, ferroptosis genes, CAF ligands, IGF-related communication, endothelial programs, or lineage-state regulators has minimal effect across patient-derived residual driver-negative models, the state-maintenance claim is weakened.
  3. If tumor-adjacent normal tissue from residual driver-negative LUAD lacks transitional epithelial states more often than matched oncogene-driven tumors, the lineage-permissive initiation law is weakened.
  4. If stromal depletion or ecosystem perturbation is generally less effective than empiric kinase inhibition in residual driver-negative models despite absent genomic drivers, the residual state-therapy law is weakened.
