Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1864

Name: State-Driven Tumorigenesis Theory of Residual LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: A biologically distinct fraction of oncogene-negative or driver-incomplete LUAD is sustained not by a single dominant genomic lesion but by stable cell-state programs that become tumor-maintaining systems. These states include lineage-survival programs, stem-like/quiescence-exit programs, non-genetic histologic progression states, immune-excluded stromal states, and ferroptosis- or glycolysis-centered survival programs. In this view, the driver is the attractor state of the tumor ecosystem rather than one altered gene.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: State-dominance law

Statement: When no single canonical oncogene is sufficient to explain LUAD behavior, stable transcriptional and microenvironmental states can become the dominant determinants of prognosis, progression, and therapy response, even in the absence of recurrent pattern-specific genetic lesions.

Domain/Scope: Applies to LUAD subsets defined by transcriptomic, proteogenomic, single-cell, spatial, or immune-state analyses rather than by one recurrent hotspot mutation; strongest in never-smoker, early-stage, EGFR/ALK-wild-type, or histologically heterogeneous cohorts.

Special Cases

  1. These states may coexist with canonical oncogenic mutations and modulate rather than replace genomic drivers.
  2. Bulk transcriptomic signatures may partly reflect stromal composition rather than tumor-cell-intrinsic states.
  3. Some state variables may be maintenance states that arise after initiation.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. The lepidic-to-solid progression study explicitly found no recurrent mutation, copy-number event, or fusion enriched by histologic pattern and concluded that LUAD progression is driven primarily by non-genetic transcriptional and microenvironmental reprogramming. [e1895.0] [e1844.0] [e1844.3]
  2. The EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker proteogenomic cohort split into four subtypes—proliferation, immune, angiogenesis, and metabolism—with distinct outcomes and dependencies despite the absence of a single shared substitute driver. [e1857.0] [e1857.4] [e1861.5]
  3. The never-smoker NS-LUAD study found a chaotic subtype with poor prognosis and no significant enrichment of canonical driver mutations or fusions, but with strong EMT, CAF, mixed-lineage, and immune-evasion features. [e1850.0] [e1850.1] [e1850.2] [e1850.3]
  4. The ferroptosis EGFRWT study defined FeSig-high tumors by a survival-linked ferroptosis-resistant/fibroblast-rich state rather than by a canonical driver lesion. [e1904.0] [e1904.1] [e1904.2] [e1904.3] [e1904.4] [e1904.5] [e1904.6]
  5. The driver gene-negative MIF/HIF study proposed a glycolysis-centered MIF+ AT2 state in which metabolic signaling, not canonical driver mutation, best explains the aggressive phenotype. [e1848.0] [e1848.1] [e1848.2] [e1848.3]
  6. Piano tumors in never-smoker lung cancer were genomically quiet, had long telomeres, high subclonal fractions, and few recurrent canonical drivers, leading the authors to hypothesize a stem-cell/quiescence-exit origin state. [e1887.0] [e1887.2] [e1887.3] [e1887.4]

Statement 1: Lineage-constraint law

Statement: Loss, amplification, or rewiring of lineage regulators can create LUAD-driving states by altering permissible differentiation programs; in these tumors, lineage identity itself becomes a dependency or a route to alternative differentiation and invasion.

Domain/Scope: Applies to LUADs with altered lineage programs, especially NKX2-1/TTF1-amplified, NKX2-1-low or -negative mucinous tumors, HNF4A-associated gastric-like states, KC subtype tumors, and related lineage-defined models.

Special Cases

  1. NKX2-1 can behave oppositely depending on oncogenic context: oncogenic dependency in some adenocarcinomas but suppressor of mucinous conversion in KRAS-driven settings.
  2. Lineage rewiring may require cooperating events such as CDKN2A/B loss or HNF4A activation/suppression.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. NKX2-1 focal amplification acts as a lineage-survival dependency in a subset of LUAD, with knockdown reducing viability and colony formation in NKX2-1-expressing cells. [e1884.0]
  2. In KRAS-driven mouse models, reduced or lost NKX2-1 permitted mucinous/gastric differentiation, AP-1 derepression, and mucinous adenocarcinoma formation, while NKX2-1 restoration suppressed these programs. [e1909.0] [e1909.2] [e1889.0] [e1889.2]
  3. The KC subgroup of KRAS-mutant LUAD was marked by low NKX2-1, CDKN2A/B inactivation, mucinous/GI-like differentiation, and altered signaling without a single canonical co-driver explaining the full state. [e1851.0]
  4. TTF1-negative LUAD showed fewer actionable mutations and a distinct KEAP1/DNA-repair/cell-cycle program, implying that lineage marker loss can define a biologically coherent, therapeutically relevant state even without a canonical substitute oncogene. [e1861.3]

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Residual driver-negative LUADs with weak genomic explanation but strong expression-state signatures will cluster more reproducibly by phosphoproteome and single-cell ecology than by exome features.
  2. In prospective EGFR/ALK-wild-type never-smoker LUAD, subtype-specific dependencies such as CDK9, TRAF2, GRB2, and ACACA will validate in patient-derived organoids more robustly than mutation-based candidate drivers.
  3. Tumors with high L2S, chaotic, or FeSig-high state scores will show greater plasticity under therapy and higher risk of recurrence than genomically matched but state-low tumors.
  4. Spatial transcriptomics of unresolved driver-negative tumors will reveal that tumor-edge and stromal-interface states, not bulk mutation burden, best predict relapse.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Some unresolved driver-negative LUADs may be reversibly maintained by epigenetic attractors; short-course chromatin remodeling therapy could collapse the malignant state without targeting a single oncogene.
  2. Lineage-rewired NKX2-1-low and HNF4A-high tumors may cycle between gastric-like and embryonal HMGA2-positive states under stress, creating a metastasis-prone plastic reservoir.
  3. Piano-like stem/quiescence-exit tumors may prove especially resistant to standard cytotoxics yet vulnerable to therapies that force terminal differentiation or disrupt stem-cell niche signaling.

Negative Experiments

  1. If residual driver-negative LUADs are better stratified by genomic lesions than by expression/proteomic/spatial states in prospective datasets, the state-dominance law would be weakened.
  2. If lineage regulator perturbation does not materially alter tumor fitness or differentiation state in patient-derived models, the lineage-constraint law would be weakened.
  3. If recurrent-risk differences disappear after controlling for canonical driver status and stage in state-defined cohorts, the claim that cell state is an independent driver variable would be weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. This theory does not directly explain clearly targetable cryptic-fusion or noncanonical kinase-driver tumors such as KIF5B-RET, NTRK1 fusions, or EGFR ECD mutants, where a discrete genomic lesion is probably dominant. [e1869.0] [e1899.0] [e1885.0] [e1856.0]
  2. It also does not fully explain onc-negative RAS/PI3K tumors driven by combinatorial NF1/RASA1/PTEN loss, which appear strongly pathway-genomic rather than state-primary. [e1863.0] [e1911.0]
