Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1861

Name: Convergent Driver Substitution in Oncogene-Negative LUAD

Type: general

Theory Query: Build theories about what drives oncogene negative lung adenocinomas. About 30 percent of lung adenos do not demonstrate readily identifiable drivers in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes. Is it rare genes we are missing? Are we missing something at the proteomic level? Is it a ligand? Different pathway? Microbiome? Different biology? What are some novel hypotheses and how can we validate these? Are there parallels in other cancers? Can we categorize driver negative cancers into different subtypes? Will there be therapeutic implications for this, based on the following results.

Description: A substantial fraction of oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinomas are not truly driverless; they are driven by noncanonical substitutes for canonical RTK/RAS/RAF oncogenes. These substitutes fall into recurrent mechanistic classes: hidden kinase fusions or rare coding mutations missed by limited assays, wild-type oncogene activation by copy-number or 3D-regulatory rewiring, and combinatorial loss of negative regulators that jointly recreate RAS/MAPK and PI3K signaling. The theory predicts that the 30% 'driver-negative' pool is a mixture of assay-limited false negatives and true noncanonical driver states rather than a single biology.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Hidden-driver recovery law

Statement: When lung adenocarcinomas are classified as driver-negative using limited hotspot panels or incomplete testing, a sizable fraction will be reclassified as driver-positive after deeper DNA/RNA/structural profiling, with the recovered events enriched for fusions, exon-skipping alterations, noncanonical receptor mutations, and low-frequency kinase-pathway lesions.

Domain/Scope: Applies to clinically or research-defined driver-negative lung adenocarcinoma cohorts initially screened by limited canonical-driver assays, especially EGFR/KRAS/ALK-centered panels or WES without high-sensitivity fusion/noncoding/CNV detection. Does not claim that all residual cases are assay failures; it applies to the reclassifiable subset.

Special Cases

  1. Reclassification is strongest when initial testing lacks RNA fusion detection, deep sequencing depth, or broad copy-number/noncoding analysis.
  2. Some cohorts define driver negativity only relative to EGFR/ALK or a small marker set, so recovered 'new' drivers may actually be canonical events missed technically.
  3. Even after comprehensive profiling, a residual subset remains unexplained.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. In a Korean triple-negative LUAD cohort initially negative for EGFR/KRAS/ALK by standard testing, comprehensive genomic profiling found genomic alterations in 94% and actionable alterations in 31%, including previously missed EGFR and KRAS mutations plus ROS1, RET, MET, ERBB2, and BRAF alterations. [e1849.0] [e1849.1] [e1849.2] [e1849.3]
  2. Among 51 standard-test-negative LUADs, 8 EGFR mutations and 8 KRAS mutations were uncovered by CGP after prior negative conventional testing; some missed EGFR/KRAS variants were outside primer coverage and some were inferred to have been below prior assay sensitivity. [e1849.1] [e1849.2]
  3. In never-smoker LUADs initially called oncogene-negative by WES, 35% were negative at first pass but only 19% remained unresolved after additional RNA-seq, clinical sequencing, FISH, and deeper sequencing; lower tumor cellularity was associated with initially unresolved cases. [e1901.0]
  4. In the TCGA RPA-negative LUAD analysis, 118/501 tumors were initially negative for RTK/RAS/RAF pathway alterations by WES/RNA-seq, but WGS recovered pathway alterations in 33% of these apparently negative cases, including KRAS mutations, amplifications, RASA1/NF1 deletions, and NRG1 alterations. Statement 1: Pathway-substitution convergence law

Statement: True residual oncogene-negative LUADs preferentially converge on a small set of pathway-level substitutes—RAS/MAPK activation, PI3K/AKT activation, lineage-survival transcriptional support, or receptor/ligand signaling—through heterogeneous genomic mechanisms rather than one recurrent gene mutation.

Domain/Scope: Applies to LUADs that remain unresolved or only partially resolved after broad profiling, including residual RPA-negative/non-CAGA/oncogene-negative subsets and biologically similar EGFR/ALK-wild-type or never-smoker subsets. The law concerns pathway convergence, not complete mutual exclusivity or universal recurrence of specific genes.

Special Cases

  1. Different tumors may reach the same pathway state through mutation, fusion, amplification, promoter/enhancer rewiring, exon skipping, or combined tumor suppressor loss.
  2. A substantial subset may show pathway activation at the phosphoprotein or expression level without a single identifiable DNA lesion.
  3. Some residual tumors may instead be microenvironment-dominant or state-dominant rather than strongly signaling-addicted.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  2. A distinct oncogene-negative LUAD study found enrichment of rare Ras/Rho pathway regulators in the negative subset, including SOS1, RASA1, VAV1, and ARHGAP35, plus focal amplifications near FGFR1/WHSC1L1, PDGFRA/KIT/KDR, and MAPK1. [e1859.0] [e1859.1] [e1859.2] [e1859.3] [e1859.4] [e1859.5]

Statement 2: Subtype decomposition law

Statement: Oncogene-negative LUAD is best decomposed into mechanistically distinct subtypes—cryptic kinase/fusion-driven, wild-type overexpression/lineage-driven, pathway-brake-loss RAS/PI3K-driven, and state/microenvironment-driven—each with different validation assays and therapeutic implications.

Domain/Scope: Applies to the clinically unresolved ~10–30% of LUAD after routine testing and to broader EGFR/ALK-wild-type or non-CAGA subsets used as proxies for driver-negative disease. It is a classification law for heterogeneity within driver-negative-like LUAD, not a claim that every tumor cleanly belongs to one category.

Special Cases

  1. Some tumors may be hybrid cases, e.g. a hidden kinase fusion plus an immune- or fibroblast-dominant state.
  2. Subtype assignment depends on assay modality; DNA-only pipelines under-detect fusion-driven and enhancer-driven classes.
  3. Early lesions and advanced tumors may not map identically because progression can add non-genetic state changes.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If routine panel-negative LUADs undergo joint deep DNA sequencing, RNA fusion calling, long-read structural profiling, and phosphoproteomics, the residual unresolved fraction will fall substantially below panel-based estimates and segregate into the four mechanistic classes described here.
  2. Tumors in the cryptic kinase/fusion-driven class will show the strongest response to matched kinase inhibition and the weakest dependence on tumor microenvironment remodeling markers.
  3. Tumors in the wild-type overexpression/lineage-driven class will be better predicted by RNA/protein outlier expression plus structural/epigenomic architecture than by coding mutation status alone.
  4. Tumors in the pathway-brake-loss class will show combined pERK and pAKT activation more often than fusion-driven or lineage-driven classes and will be enriched for sensitivity to SHP2/MEK/AKT combinations rather than single-agent RTK inhibitors.
  5. Tumors in the state/microenvironment-driven class will show weaker recurrence of single DNA drivers but stronger reproducibility of stromal, ferroptosis, immune, or glycolytic programs across independent cohorts.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. A subset of residual driver-negative LUADs will prove dependent on enhancer-promoter topology that can be therapeutically disrupted by targeted transcriptional or chromatin-loop perturbation even when kinase inhibition fails.
  2. Some apparently state-driven driver-negative LUADs will convert into kinase-dependent states after microenvironment depletion, revealing masked oncogenic addictions.
  3. Longitudinal sampling from preneoplasia to invasive disease will show that some driver-negative tumors begin as lineage- or metabolic-state lesions and only later acquire structural or pathway-brake-loss drivers.
  4. Single-cell multi-omic profiling will reveal that a fraction of 'unresolved' tumors are mosaics composed of two cooperating compartments: a non-neoplastic stromal signaling source and a weakly transformed epithelial recipient clone.

Negative Experiments

  1. If comprehensive multimodal profiling of a large panel-negative LUAD cohort fails to reduce the unresolved fraction or fails to identify enrichment of hidden fusions/noncanonical lesions, the hidden-driver recovery component would be weakened.
  2. If residual oncogene-negative tumors do not show convergence on a limited number of pathway states at the phosphoprotein or transcriptomic level, the pathway-substitution law would be weakened.
  3. If the proposed subtype classes do not separate therapeutically or biologically in independent cohorts, the subtype decomposition law would be weakened.
  4. If combined RAS/PI3K pathway inhibition does not preferentially affect tumors with pathway-brake-loss signatures, the pathway-brake subtype would be weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some panel-negative tumors may truly lack a dominant oncogenic dependency and instead reflect broad genomic instability or passenger-heavy evolution that this theory does not fully resolve. [e1864.5] [e1907.2] [e1907.3]
  2. The theory does not fully explain geographically specific mutagenic subgroups such as SBS22a/aristolochic-acid-associated never-smoker LUAD or HRD/MSI-like rare subsets. [e1914.2] [e1914.3] [e1914.5]
