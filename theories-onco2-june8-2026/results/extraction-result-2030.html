Paper Title: RASA1 and NF1 are preferentially co-mutated and define a distinct genetic subset of smoking-associated non-small cell lung carcinomas sensitive to MEK inhibition.

Paper Venue: Clinical Cancer Research

Paper TL;DR: Cancer genomic and functional data nominate concurrent RASA1/NF1 loss-of-function mutations as a strong mitogenic driver in NSCLC, which may sensitize to trametinib.

Paper Abstract: Purpose: Ras-GTPase–activating proteins (RasGAP), notably NF1 and RASA1, mediate negative control of the RAS/MAPK pathway. We evaluated clinical and molecular characteristics of non–small cell lung carcinoma (NSCLC) with RASA1 mutations in comparison with NF1-mutated cases. Experimental Design: Large genomic datasets of NSCLC [MSK-IMPACT dataset at MSKCC (n = 2,004), TCGA combined lung cancer dataset (n = 1,144)] were analyzed to define concurrent mutations and clinical features of RASA1-mutated NSCLCs. Functional studies were performed using immortalized human bronchial epithelial cells (HBEC) and NSCLC lines with truncating mutations in RASA1, NF1, or both. Results: Overall, approximately 2% of NSCLCs had RASA1-truncating mutations, and this alteration was statistically, but not completely, mutually exclusive with known activating EGFR (P = 0.02) and KRAS (P = 0.02) mutations. Unexpectedly, RASA1-truncating mutations had a strong tendency to co-occur with NF1-truncating mutations (P < 0.001). Furthermore, all patients (16/16) with concurrent RASA1/NF1-truncating mutations lacked other known lung cancer drivers. Knockdown of RASA1 in HBECs activated signaling downstream of RAS and promoted cell growth. Conversely, restoration of RASA1 expression in RASA1-mutated cells reduced MAPK and PI3K signaling. Although growth of cell lines with inactivation of only one of these two RasGAPs showed moderate and variable sensitivity to inhibitors of MEK or PI3K, cells with concurrent RASA1/NF1 mutations were profoundly more sensitive (IC50: 0.040 μmol/L trametinib). Finally, simultaneous genetic silencing of RASA1 and NF1 sensitized both HBECs and NSCLC cells to MEK inhibition. Conclusions: Cancer genomic and functional data nominate concurrent RASA1/NF1 loss-of-function mutations as a strong mitogenic driver in NSCLC, which may sensitize to trametinib. Clin Cancer Res; 24(6); 1436–47. ©2017 AACR. See related commentary by Kitajima and Barbie, p. 1243

driver_negative_definition The driver-negative context is defined by absence of other known lung cancer mitogenic drivers in tumors carrying concurrent RASA1/NF1 co-inactivation. In the MSK-IMPACT and TCGA lung datasets, these tumors were mutually exclusive with activating EGFR and KRAS mutations and with other common drivers/fusions or alterations including ALK/RET/ROS1 fusions, ERBB2 exon 20 insertions, BRAF mutations, and MET exon 14 skipping. The alterations counted as negative were truncating mutations and/or homozygous/deep deletions of RASA1 and NF1, with emphasis on truncating events because of high background mutation rates in smoking-associated NSCLC; these were supported by low mRNA expression when copy-number loss or truncation was present.
candidate_driver_pathway_or_mechanism The main hypothesis is tumor suppressor loss of two redundant RasGAPs producing a hidden mitogenic driver through excessive RAS pathway activation. The resulting biology is activation of downstream MAPK and PI3K/AKT signaling, with the co-loss state proposed as a strong mitogenic driver in NSCLC.
alteration_or_activation_mode Combinatorial co-loss of negative regulators of RAS signaling: truncating mutations and deep/homozygous deletions in RASA1 and NF1. The paper also mentions that some missense mutations in RasGAP domains may be functionally inactivating, but the core analysis focuses on loss-of-function truncations/deletions.
implicated_gene_axis_or_pathway RASA1/NF1-RAS axis, with downstream MAPK/ERK and PI3K/AKT signaling. Trametinib-sensitive dependence points specifically to MAPK pathway reliance, while PI3K signaling is also modulated.
pathway_activation_evidence Evidence includes increased RAS-GTP after RASA1 knockdown, increased phosphorylation of MAPK and PI3K pathway-associated kinases on phospho-kinase arrays, increased pERK and pAKT by western blot after RASA1 suppression, and reduced MAPK/AKT signaling after RASA1 restoration. In co-mutated cells, trametinib produced potent dose-dependent suppression of pERK1/2, and combined MEK plus PI3K or EGFR blockade further reduced pERK and pAKT. The activated state is genetically explained by loss of RasGAP function.
driver_negative_subtype A distinct genetic subset of smoking-associated NSCLC, including both adenocarcinoma and squamous histologies, enriched for concurrent RASA1/NF1 loss. The subtype is genomically defined by co-inactivation of RASA1 and NF1 and is not a transcriptomic or immune subtype.
clinical_or_exposure_context These tumors are strongly smoking-associated: patients with concurrent RASA1/NF1 truncating mutations were entirely former or current heavy smokers. The co-mutated cases were older on average (mean/median 69 years, range 46-81), 50% female, and histologically split about evenly between squamous and adenocarcinoma; survival appeared shorter (median overall survival 18 months) but was not statistically significant.
cross_cancer_parallel The paper compares this logic to other cancers where co-loss of negative regulators creates pathway dependence and drug sensitivity, including TSC1/NF2 co-mutation with everolimus sensitivity in bladder cancer, LATS2/NF2 co-inactivation in malignant pleural mesothelioma with mTOR sensitivity, and ARID1A/RNF43 co-inactivation with aurora kinase sensitivity. It also cites combined NF1 and RASA1 loss causing T-ALL in T cells as a parallel to redundant tumor suppressor loss producing transformation.
therapeutic_vulnerability_or_implication The main therapeutic implication is sensitivity to MEK inhibition, especially trametinib, with profound in vitro sensitivity in RASA1/NF1 co-mutated cells (IC50 about 0.040 μM). The paper also reports enhanced effects with combined MEK + PI3K inhibition and MEK + EGFR inhibition, and suggests patients with this genotype should be considered for clinical trials of MEK inhibitors; evidence is preclinical.
validation_evidence_and_model_system Validated through large clinical genomic datasets (MSK-IMPACT n=2900 NSCLC; TCGA combined n=1144), plus functional experiments in immortalized HBEC3KT cells and multiple NSCLC cell lines. Validation included shRNA knockdown of RASA1 and NF1, ectopic re-expression of RASA1, RAS-GTP assays, phospho-kinase/RTK/apoptosis arrays, western blots for pERK/pAKT and cell-cycle/apoptosis markers, and drug-growth inhibition assays showing enhanced sensitivity to trametinib. The strongest evidence comes from recurrence in patients plus mechanistic and pharmacologic validation in cell models.
