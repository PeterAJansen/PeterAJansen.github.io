Paper Abstract: SUMMARY Lung adenocarcinoma, the most common subtype of non-small cell lung cancer, is responsible for over 500,000 deaths per year worldwide. Here, we report exome and genome sequences of 183 lung adenocarcinoma tumor/normal DNA pairs. These analyses revealed a mean exonic somatic mutation rate of 12.0 events/megabase and identified the majority of genes previously reported as significantly mutated in lung adenocarcinoma. In addition, we identified statistically recurrent somatic mutations in the splicing factor gene U2AF1 and truncating mutations affecting RBM10 and ARID1A. Analysis of nucleotide context-specific mutation signatures grouped the sample set into distinct clusters that correlated with smoking history and alterations of reported lung adenocarcinoma genes. Whole genome sequence analysis revealed frequent structural re-arrangements, including in-frame exonic alterations within EGFR and SIK2 kinases. The candidate genes identified in this study are attractive targets for biological characterization and therapeutic targeting of lung adenocarcinoma.

driver_negative_definition In the hallmarks analysis, tumors were effectively 'negative' for a given driver-hallmark assignment when no activating event in a dominant cancer gene and no inactivating event in a recessive cancer gene mapping to that hallmark was detected. The authors explicitly note that less than half of the cohort (47%) had a mutation in a known driver gene for sustaining proliferative signaling, and only 55% did when including high-level amplification in proliferative signaling genes such as EGFR, ERBB2, and MYC; around half of the cohort lacked a mutation supporting sustained proliferative signaling, and a majority lacked a genetic alteration explaining invasion/metastasis or angiogenesis.
candidate_driver_pathway_or_mechanism The main hidden-driver hypothesis is that oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinomas are driven by less common recurrent genes and by alterations in pathways not fully captured by canonical driver lists, especially epigenetic and RNA-deregulation programs. The paper also points to potential unidentified alterations underlying invasion/metastasis, angiogenesis, immune evasion, and tumor-promoting inflammation, but it most concretely proposes a novel '11th hallmark' of epigenetic and RNA deregulation.
alteration_or_activation_mode Rare recurrent mutation or loss-of-function alteration in noncanonical genes; structural rearrangement; in-frame exon deletion/fusion; amplification for some hallmark genes; and a broader pathway-state alteration inferred from mutation patterns. Specifically, the study reports recurrent nonsynonymous mutations in U2AF1, truncating mutations in RBM10 and ARID1A, and in-frame activating rearrangements in EGFR and SIK2, all of which expand the set of possible noncanonical driver mechanisms.
implicated_gene_axis_or_pathway Most specifically: splicing machinery and RNA processing (U2AF1, RBM10, U2AF2, SF3B1, PRPF40B), chromatin remodeling/epigenetic regulation (ARID1A, SMARCA4, SETD2, BRD3), and proliferative signaling (EGFR, KRAS, STK11-associated signaling, PI3K/AKT). The paper also maps unreconciled tumors to hallmarks that remain genomically unexplained, especially invasion/metastasis and angiogenesis.
pathway_activation_evidence For the driver-negative context, the evidence is mostly negative/absence-of-evidence at the genotype level rather than direct pathway activation readouts. The paper shows that many tumors lack a known driver mutation for sustained proliferative signaling, and it uses cluster-level mutation signatures, copy-number profiles, and hallmarks mapping to infer that several hallmark states are not explained by known lesions; however, it does not provide pERK/pAKT/pS6 or phosphoproteomic evidence for driver-negative tumors as a class.
driver_negative_subtype No formal molecular subtype named 'driver-negative' is defined. The closest implicit subtypes are mutation-spectrum clusters and hallmark categories: Cluster 1 (low mutation rate, CpG->T enriched, associated with never/light smokers), Cluster 4 (advanced-stage enriched), and the proposed epigenetic/RNA-deregulation hallmark group (10% of cases if only SMARCA4; 22% including nominated genes).
clinical_or_exposure_context Driver-negative-leaning tumors are indirectly associated with never/light-smoker status, lower mutation burden, and in some analyses specific mutation-spectrum clusters. The cohort overall included never smokers, light smokers, and heavy smokers; Cluster 1 was enriched in never and light smokers, while Cluster 4 was enriched in advanced stage (IIIB/IV). The hallmarks analysis also notes that many tumors lack explanatory lesions despite clinical heterogeneity.
cross_cancer_parallel The paper explicitly draws a parallel to lung squamous cell carcinoma for CDKN2A inactivation by rearrangement and to glioblastoma for the oncogenic EGFR C-terminal deletion variant. It also compares the RNA-splicing mutations to myelodysplastic syndrome, where U2AF1 mutations were first described.
therapeutic_vulnerability_or_implication The major therapeutic implication is that additional, previously unrecognized driver genes and pathways in lung adenocarcinoma could be actionable targets. The paper frames EGFR alterations as therapeutically relevant and shows that the novel EGFR exon 25/26 deletion is sensitive to erlotinib in cells; it also suggests that identifying epigenetic/RNA-deregulation lesions may open new clinical translation opportunities, although no drug is directly proposed for the driver-negative group as a whole.
validation_evidence_and_model_system Validation is strongest for individual candidate drivers rather than a driver-negative subtype per se. The paper validates somatic calls by WES/WGS cross-comparison and ultra-deep targeted resequencing, confirms genic rearrangements by PCR and Illumina sequencing, demonstrates oncogenicity of the EGFR exon 25/26 deletion in NIH-3T3 soft-agar and signaling assays, and shows erlotinib sensitivity in Ba/F3 cells. The driver-negative interpretation itself is supported by statistical hallmarks mapping and the observation that many tumors lack known driver alterations.
