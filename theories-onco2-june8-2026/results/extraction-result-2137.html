Paper Abstract: Spatial and temporal dissection of the genomic changes occurring during the evolution of human non–small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) may help elucidate the basis for its dismal prognosis. We sequenced 25 spatially distinct regions from seven operable NSCLCs and found evidence of branched evolution, with driver mutations arising before and after subclonal diversification. There was pronounced intratumor heterogeneity in copy number alterations, translocations, and mutations associated with APOBEC cytidine deaminase activity. Despite maintained carcinogen exposure, tumors from smokers showed a relative decrease in smoking-related mutations over time, accompanied by an increase in APOBEC-associated mutations. In tumors from former smokers, genome-doubling occurred within a smoking-signature context before subclonal diversification, which suggested that a long period of tumor latency had preceded clinical detection. The regionally separated driver mutations, coupled with the relentless and heterogeneous nature of the genome instability processes, are likely to confound treatment success in NSCLC. Different regions of a human lung tumor harbor different mutations, possibly explaining why the disease is so tough to treat. [Also see Perspective by Govindan] Space, time, and the lung cancer genome Lung cancer poses a formidable challenge to clinical oncologists. It is often detected at a late stage, and most therapies work for only a short time before the tumors resume their relentless growth. Two independent analyses of the human lung cancer genome may help explain why this disease is so resilient (see the Perspective by Govindan). Rather than take a single “snapshot” of the cancer genome, de Bruin et al. and Zhang et al. identified genomic alterations in spatially distinct regions of single lung tumors and used this information to infer the tumor's evolutionary history. Each tumor showed tremendous spatial and temporal diversity in its mutational profiles. Thus, the efficacy of drugs may be short-lived because they destroy only a portion of the tumor. The study sequenced 25 spatially distinct regions from seven operable NSCLCs (including LUAD and LUSC) using multiregion whole-exome sequencing and/or whole-genome sequencing of tumor and matched normal DNA; tumors were not categorized as driver-negative or oncogene-negative by a canonical clinical test panel.
assay_strategy_and_detection_limits The discovery pipeline was multiregion WES/WGS (25 regions from 7 resected NSCLCs, mean coverage ~107x for WES and ~54x for WGS), with copy-number inference, structural-variant calling, and phylogenetic reconstruction across regions. The paper explicitly states that single-region analysis can miss drivers: the estimated probability of missing a category 1 high-confidence driver gene by analyzing one region was 42% on average (0-67%), and 83% for all potential driver genes (categories 1-3); only <5% of each tumor was sampled on average, so the full extent of heterogeneity could be underestimated.
missed_driver_reclassified_after_broader_testing Yes, in the sense of multiregional reclassification rather than reclassification from a formal negative screen. The paper shows that driver events can be absent from one region but present in another, meaning a single biopsy could label a tumor as lacking a given driver even when another region harbors it. Examples include an activating PIK3CA (E542K) mutation present only in region R3 of L008, and heterogeneous driver mutations/CNVs that were entirely absent in other regions. The rescue modality was broader multiregion WES/WGS plus copy-number and SV analysis, and the reason for being missed initially was spatial heterogeneity and limited sampling, not a specific technical failure of a targeted assay.
