Paper Title: Genomic Profiling of Lung Adenocarcinoma in Never-Smokers

Paper Venue: Journal of Clinical Oncology

Paper TL;DR: A potential role for germline variants in DNA repair genes and passive exposure to cigarette smoke in the pathogenesis of a subset of never-smoker LUADs is observed.

Paper Abstract: PURPOSE Approximately 10%-40% of patients with lung cancer report no history of tobacco smoking (never-smokers). We analyzed whole-exome and RNA-sequencing data of 160 tumor and normal lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) samples from never-smokers to identify clinically actionable alterations and gain insight into the environmental and hereditary risk factors for LUAD among never-smokers. METHODS We performed whole-exome and RNA-sequencing of 88 and 69 never-smoker LUADs. We analyzed these data in conjunction with data from 76 never-smoker and 299 smoker LUAD samples sequenced by The Cancer Genome Atlas and Clinical Proteomic Tumor Analysis Consortium. RESULTS We observed a high prevalence of clinically actionable driver alterations in never-smoker LUADs compared with smoker LUADs (78%-92% v 49.5%; P < .0001). Although a subset of never-smoker samples demonstrated germline alterations in DNA repair genes, the frequency of samples showing germline variants in cancer predisposing genes was comparable between smokers and never-smokers (6.4% v 6.9%; P = .82). A subset of never-smoker samples (5.9%) showed mutation signatures that were suggestive of passive exposure to cigarette smoke. Finally, analysis of RNA-sequencing data showed distinct immune transcriptional subtypes of never-smoker LUADs that varied in their expression of clinically relevant immune checkpoint molecules and immune cell composition. CONCLUSION In this comprehensive genomic and transcriptome analysis of never-smoker LUADs, we observed a potential role for germline variants in DNA repair genes and passive exposure to cigarette smoke in the pathogenesis of a subset of never-smoker LUADs. Our findings also show that clinically actionable driver alterations are highly prevalent in never-smoker LUADs, highlighting the need for obtaining biopsies with adequate cellularity for clinical genomic testing in these patients. Potentially targetable alterations are present in 78% of lung cancers from never-smokers

driver_negative_definition The paper operationalized driver-negative status as failure to identify a known activating alteration in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes by WES (and RNA-seq when available) in the institutional cohort, after also considering targeted clinical molecular testing and FISH. Driver alterations were defined as known activating mutations or fusions in RTK/RAS/RAF pathway genes; samples without a readily identifiable driver by these methods were termed oncogene negative (ON) by WES.
candidate_driver_pathway_or_mechanism The main hidden-driver explanation is not a new recurrent pathway-state mechanism but rather under-detection of canonical oncogenic drivers due to low tumor cellularity and assay limitations, with rescue of hidden drivers by deeper sequencing or clinical testing. The paper also highlights a subset of apparently driver-negative tumors with smoking-related mutational signatures, suggesting passive tobacco exposure as an etiologic contributor rather than a specific oncogenic pathway.
alteration_or_activation_mode Rescued lesions included rare kinase-domain mutation (EGFR p.A955R), activating AKT1 mutation (p.E17K), ALK and ROS1 fusions by FISH, EGFR/ERBB2/KRAS mutations by clinical sequencing, and MET exon 14 skipping by high-depth WES; the hidden-driver mechanism is therefore a mixture of rare coding oncogene mutation, kinase fusion, and exon-skipping/splicing activation. The broader ON call itself was strongly influenced by low tumor purity/cellularity.
implicated_gene_axis_or_pathway RTK/RAS/RAF signaling is the dominant implicated pathway, with individual rescued or identified drivers in EGFR, ALK, ROS1, MET, KRAS, ERBB2, AKT1, BRAF, and other LUAD-recurrent genes. The paper also notes CTNNB1/WNT signaling as a correlate of immune-cold biology, but not as the primary hidden driver of the driver-negative state.
pathway_activation_evidence There is no phosphoproteomic or direct pathway-activation readout for the ON tumors themselves. Evidence is genomic: reclassification after deeper WES, clinical sequencing, and FISH showed that many initially negative tumors actually carried canonical activating alterations; co-occurrence patterns also supported known driver biology (for example, EGFR with TP53/CDKN2A/RB1).
driver_negative_subtype The paper does not propose a dedicated molecular subtype defined by true driver absence. Instead, it describes an operationally ON group that is largely resolved into canonical RTK/RAS/RAF-driven tumors after broader testing, plus a separate immune-subtype structure (IM-1, IM-2, IM-3) that is not specific to ON tumors.
clinical_or_exposure_context All analyzed cases were never-smoker LUADs; the ON subset arose in a cohort enriched for women and with substantial Asian ancestry representation. Low tumor cellularity was more common among initially negative cases, and a small subset of never-smoker samples showed smoking-related mutational signatures suggestive of passive cigarette smoke exposure.
cross_cancer_parallel The paper does not provide a strong cross-cancer analog specifically for driver-negative LUAD, but it frames LUAD as generally characterized by RTK/RAS/RAF activation, with the same logic used in other oncogene-driven cancers where deeper testing can reveal hidden actionable lesions.
therapeutic_vulnerability_or_implication The major implication is that many seemingly driver-negative never-smoker LUADs are still targetable once hidden drivers are found; the authors emphasize the need for comprehensive molecular testing and adequate biopsy cellularity to enable use of currently available targeted therapies. No novel therapy is proposed for truly driver-negative tumors.
