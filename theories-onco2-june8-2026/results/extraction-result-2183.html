Paper Abstract: Mutational processes giving rise to lung adenocarcinomas (LADCs) in non-smokers remain elusive. We analyzed 138 LADC whole genomes, including 83 cases with minimal contribution of smoking-associated mutational signature. Genomic rearrangements were not correlated with smoking-associated mutations and frequently served as driver events of smoking-signature-low LADCs. Complex genomic rearrangements, including chromothripsis and chromoplexy, generated 74% of known fusion oncogenes, including EML4-ALK, CD74-ROS1, and KIF5B-RET. Unlike other collateral rearrangements, these fusion-oncogene-associated rearrangements were frequently copy-number-balanced, representing a genomic signature of early oncogenesis. Analysis of mutation timing revealed that fusions and point mutations of canonical oncogenes were often acquired in the early decades of life. During a long latency, cancer-related genes were disrupted or amplified by complex rearrangements. The genomic landscape was different between subgroups-EGFR-mutant LADCs had frequent whole-genome duplications with p53 mutations, whereas fusion-oncogene-driven LADCs had frequent SETD2 mutations. Our study highlights LADC oncogenesis driven by endogenous mutational processes.

assay_strategy_and_detection_limits Discovery used matched-tumor/normal whole-genome sequencing (median ~42x tumor, ~32x normal; up to 119-130x in select cases), RNA-seq in 79/138 tumors to confirm in-frame fusions, and integrated copy-number/structural-variant timing analyses. Earlier clinical genotyping used targeted panels (MG-LCDx: 17 hotspot mutations and 7 known fusion oncogenes; FIRST-cancer: 316 cancer-related genes and 30 fusion oncogenes) on 350 resected LADCs. Structural variants were called by Delly and filtered for mapping quality, read support, and normal-sample artifacts; short deletions/duplications without soft-clipped reads and some unbalanced inversions were removed as likely library artifacts. The paper emphasizes blind spots of panel-based testing for complex rearrangements, post hoc structural complexity, low purity (which required deeper sequencing in some tumors), and the fact that some events were only resolved after manual reconstruction of derivative chromosomes.
missed_driver_reclassified_after_broader_testing Yes, in the broader WGS/RNA-seq workup some lesions initially not apparent from limited hotspot/fusion testing or from coarse structural interpretation were resolved as drivers. Examples include ERBB3-BCAR4 recurrent fusions in two S4-low tumors that lacked known major oncogene alterations initially; one was formed by chromoplexy and the other had RNA-seq-confirmed overexpression of the fusion transcript. The paper also describes other hidden driver-relevant rearrangements uncovered by WGS, such as AXL-MBIP high-level amplification (LU-F13), KIF5B-RET fusion formation timing in LU-FF76 and LU-4, and fusion-oncogene-associated complex rearrangements that required manual derivative-chromosome reconstruction. The main reason these were missed initially was limited assay scope and/or structural complexity rather than absence of a driver.
