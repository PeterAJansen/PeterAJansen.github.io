Paper Abstract: Mutational processes giving rise to lung adenocarcinomas (LADCs) in non-smokers remain elusive. We analyzed 138 LADC whole genomes, including 83 cases with minimal contribution of smoking-associated mutational signature. Genomic rearrangements were not correlated with smoking-associated mutations and frequently served as driver events of smoking-signature-low LADCs. Complex genomic rearrangements, including chromothripsis and chromoplexy, generated 74% of known fusion oncogenes, including EML4-ALK, CD74-ROS1, and KIF5B-RET. Unlike other collateral rearrangements, these fusion-oncogene-associated rearrangements were frequently copy-number-balanced, representing a genomic signature of early oncogenesis. Analysis of mutation timing revealed that fusions and point mutations of canonical oncogenes were often acquired in the early decades of life. During a long latency, cancer-related genes were disrupted or amplified by complex rearrangements. The genomic landscape was different between subgroups-EGFR-mutant LADCs had frequent whole-genome duplications with p53 mutations, whereas fusion-oncogene-driven LADCs had frequent SETD2 mutations. Our study highlights LADC oncogenesis driven by endogenous mutational processes.

The study first performed targeted deep sequencing panels (hotspot mutations plus selected fusions) in an early-stage cohort, then WGS/RNA-seq to identify whether tumors had known fusion oncogenes or activating alterations in EGFR, KRAS, BRAF, ERBB2, or MET; among S4-low cases, 8 tumors had no known alterations in major oncogenes, and these were the cases most directly resembling driver-negative LUAD in this paper.
candidate_driver_pathway_or_mechanism The paper does not propose a single hidden driver mechanism for the few S4-low cases with no known major oncogene alterations. Instead, it argues more broadly that non-smoker, driver-positive LADCs are initiated by canonical oncogene activation followed by secondary driver events, and that some apparently driver-negative tumors may still harbor recurrent but less established alterations such as ERBB3-BCAR4 fusions, SETD2 loss, PTEN loss, or amplified cancer-related genes discovered on deeper profiling.
alteration_or_activation_mode Not a single mechanism; candidate alterations include fusion oncogene formation, copy-number amplification, and tumor-suppressor loss. implicated_gene_axis_or_pathway ERBB3-BCAR4, BCAR4, and more generally oncogenic RTK/MAPK-related signaling are implicated as candidate hidden axes in the few tumors without known major oncogene alterations. The paper also highlights SETD2 loss, PTEN loss, and amplification of genes such as TERT, MDM2, CDK4, MET, KIT, and ERBB2 as secondary or alternative drivers in non-smoker/S4-low disease.
pathway_activation_evidence For the residual driver-negative-like cases, direct pathway activation evidence is limited. The strongest evidence in the paper is that one ERBB3-BCAR4 case had overexpression of the fusion transcript verified by RNA-seq and preserved ERBB3 kinase domain in the chimeric protein, implying potential ERBB signaling activation; however, the paper does not report pERK/pAKT/pS6 or phosphoproteomic validation for these specific tumors.
driver_negative_subtype A very small subset of S4-low LADCs with no known alterations in major oncogenes. The broader S4-low class is genomically heterogeneous and includes never-smokers, fusion-oncogene cases, and canonical driver-mutation cases; the paper does not define a dedicated transcriptomic or proteomic 'driver-negative' subtype.
clinical_or_exposure_context The relevant context is lung adenocarcinoma in clinical never-smokers or tumors with minimal smoking-associated mutational signature, often in younger patients and disproportionately in non-smokers. The paper also notes East Asian enrichment within S4-low cases, but finds no major genomic landscape difference by ethnicity within subgroup.
cross_cancer_parallel The paper compares chromoplexy and chromothripsis mechanisms to prostate cancer, NUT midline carcinoma, Ewing sarcoma, germline balanced rearrangements, and other cancers where catastrophic rearrangements generate gene fusions or driver events. It also cites BCAR4 fusion in uterine cervical cancer as support for BCAR4 as an oncogenic partner in another cancer type.
therapeutic_vulnerability_or_implication The paper states that more than 70% of LADC cases overall have alterations that are clinically targetable or have selective inhibitors under development. For the specific residual driver-negative-like cases, the implication is biomarker-driven consideration of ERBB-family/RTK-targeted strategies if a hidden fusion is found, but no direct therapeutic response data are shown. The broader discussion also suggests that SETD2 mutations in fusion-oncogene-driven LADCs may confer selective vulnerability, and that amplified oncogenes such as MET or KIT in treatment-naive tumors could mediate rapid resistance to EGFR or ALK inhibitors.
