Paper Title: Combinatorial tumor suppressor inactivation efficiently initiates lung adenocarcinoma with therapeutic vulnerabilities

Paper Venue: bioRxiv

Paper TL;DR: It is demonstrated that oncogene-negative tumors and cell lines with activated RAS/MAPK and PI3K pathways are vulnerable to pharmacological inhibition of these signaling axes and transform the understanding of this prevalent yet understudied subtype of lung adenocarcinoma.

Paper Abstract: Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer death worldwide, with lung adenocarcinoma being the most common subtype. Many oncogenes and tumor suppressor genes are altered in this cancer type and the discovery of oncogene mutations has led to the development of targeted therapies and better clinical outcomes. However, a large fraction of lung adenocarcinomas lacks mutations in known oncogenes, and the genesis and treatment of these oncogene-negative tumors remain enigmatic. Here, we perform iterative in vivo functional screens with quantitative autochthonous mouse model systems to uncover the genetic and biochemical changes that enable efficient lung tumor initiation in the absence of oncogene alterations. Through the generation of hundreds of diverse combinations of tumor suppressor alterations, we demonstrate that inactivation of suppressors of the RAS and PI3K pathways drive the development of oncogene- negative lung adenocarcinoma. Pathway-level human genomic data analysis and histology identified RAS/MAPK and PI3K pathway activation as a common event in oncogene-negative human lung adenocarcinomas. We demonstrate that oncogene-negative tumors and cell lines with activated RAS/MAPK and PI3K pathways are vulnerable to pharmacological inhibition of these signaling axes. Collectively, these results transform our understanding of this prevalent yet understudied subtype of lung adenocarcinoma. STATEMENT OF SIGNIFICANCE Many lung adenocarcinomas do not have mutations in known proto-oncogenes, and as a result, targeted therapies are unavailable for treating these patients. Here, we uncover driver pathways in a subset of these oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinomas and demonstrate the therapeutic value of inhibiting these pathways.

candidate_driver_pathway_or_mechanism The main driver hypothesis is combinatorial loss of tumor suppressors that de-represses driver signaling pathways rather than activation of a classic oncogene. Specifically, concurrent inactivation of negative regulators of RAS and PI3K signaling is proposed to initiate oncogene-negative LUAD efficiently.
alteration_or_activation_mode Combinatorial co-loss/inactivation of tumor suppressors, including genomic deletion/flox-based knockout and CRISPR/Cas9-mediated inactivation; in human tumors, the pathway state may also arise through non-genomic mechanisms such as epigenetic silencing and protein loss (especially PTEN downregulation).

pathway_activation_evidence Mouse Nf1/Rasa1/Pten tumors showed pERK positivity and pAKT positivity by immunohistochemistry, with RNA-seq/ssGSVA confirming elevated RAS and PI3K-AKT pathway signatures. Activation was often not fully explained by identifiable mutations in NF1, PTEN, or other panel genes, implying unexplained/non-genomic activation in many cases.
driver_negative_subtype Onc-negative^RAS/PI3K^ lung adenocarcinoma. This subtype is defined by oncogene-negative status together with biochemical activation of both RAS and PI3K pathways, and is supported by integrated genomic, histologic, immunohistochemical, and transcriptomic similarity between mouse models and human tumors.
clinical_or_exposure_context The paper reports that oncogene-negative, oncogene-indeterminate, and oncogene-positive LUADs had broadly similar mutational burden and clinical characteristics in TCGA/GENIE. It also notes that RASA1 and NF1 co-mutated tumors have been described in smoking-associated NSCLC in prior work, but this study does not establish a specific exposure or demographic enrichment for the Onc-negative^RAS/PI3K^ subtype.
cross_cancer_parallel The paper contrasts this LUAD biology with other cancers where oncogene discovery enabled targeted therapy, and cites parallel logic for pathway convergence in cancer more broadly. It also notes prior descriptions of NF1/RASA1-mutant lung cancer as a distinct subset sensitive to MEK inhibition, aligning with the broader idea that pathway-level dependencies can define hidden driver biology.
therapeutic_vulnerability_or_implication Onc-negative^RAS/PI3K^ tumors are vulnerable to pathway inhibition, especially SHP2 inhibition (RMC-4550) and AKT1/2 inhibition (capivasertib), with the combination showing the strongest preclinical activity. The paper suggests that additional targeting of RAS-pathway nodes (SOS, MEK, ERK) and PI3K-pathway nodes (PI3K, mTOR) may be rational future strategies. This is supported preclinically in mouse tumors, cell lines, and allografts, and the selected drugs were chosen partly because of ongoing clinical development.
validation_evidence_and_model_system Validated using quantitative autochthonous mouse models with Cre/loxP plus somatic CRISPR/Cas9 editing, Tuba-seq barcoding, histology, IHC, RNA-seq, whole-exome sequencing, and inhibitor response studies. Evidence includes: low tumor yield with single/pairwise suppressor loss, strong tumor initiation with triple Nf1/Rasa1/Pten loss comparable to KrasG12D, concordant pERK/pAKT and expression signatures in mouse tumors, replication of pathway activation in 35 human oncogene-negative LUADs, and drug sensitivity in murine and human oncogene-negative cell lines plus murine allografts.
