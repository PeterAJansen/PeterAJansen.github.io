Paper Title: Co-occurring genomic alterations in non-small cell lung cancer biology and therapy

Paper Venue: Nature Reviews. Cancer

Paper TL;DR: The effects of co-mutations on the pathogenesis, biology, microenvironmental interactions and therapeutic vulnerabilities of non-small-cell lung cancer are discussed and the challenges and opportunities presented for personalized anticancer therapy, as well as the expanding field of precision immunotherapy are assessed.

Paper Abstract: The impressive clinical activity of small-molecule receptor tyrosine kinase inhibitors for oncogene-addicted subgroups of non-small-cell lung cancer (for example, those driven by activating mutations in the gene encoding epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) or rearrangements in the genes encoding the receptor tyrosine kinases anaplastic lymphoma kinase (ALK), ROS proto-oncogene 1 (ROS1) and rearranged during transfection (RET)) has established an oncogene-centric molecular classification paradigm in this disease. However, recent studies have revealed considerable phenotypic diversity downstream of tumour-initiating oncogenes. Co-occurring genomic alterations, particularly in tumour suppressor genes such as TP53 and LKB1 (also known as STK11), have emerged as core determinants of the molecular and clinical heterogeneity of oncogene-driven lung cancer subgroups through their effects on both tumour cell-intrinsic and non-cell-autonomous cancer hallmarks. In this Review, we discuss the impact of co-mutations on the pathogenesis, biology, microenvironmental interactions and therapeutic vulnerabilities of non-small-cell lung cancer and assess the challenges and opportunities that co-mutations present for personalized anticancer therapy, as well as the expanding field of precision immunotherapy. Co-occurring genomic alterations contribute to the heterogeneity of driver oncogene-defined non-small-cell lung cancer subgroups. Conceptually, it refers to LUAD that is not explained by a single canonical dominant oncogenic event and where broad profiling shows either multiple co-alterations or, in the case of some fusion-driven or KRAS/EGFR subgroups, an apparent lack of the expected mutually exclusive 'single-driver' simplicity. In particular, the paper argues that combinations such as KRAS+LKB1/STK11 loss, KRAS+KEAP1 loss, TP53 co-mutation, CDKN2A/CDKN2B loss, RB1 loss, MET/ERBB2 amplification, and NKX2-1 lineage-state changes can function as the hidden determinants of aggressiveness, immune phenotype, and drug response.
alteration_or_activation_mode The proposed mechanisms include co-occurring point mutations, bi-allelic deletions, amplifications, rearrangements, exon skipping, and lineage-state/epigenetic rewiring. In driver-negative or apparently weakly driven tumors, the functional state is often inferred to arise from loss of tumor suppressors, pathway activation downstream of the canonical driver, and epigenetic/metabolic reprogramming rather than a single additional dominant oncogene.

pathway_activation_evidence Evidence is drawn from transcriptomic clustering, mouse models, cell lines, and pathology/clinical associations rather than phosphoproteomics of driver-negative LUAD. Examples include LKB1-loss-associated epigenetic repression of STING, increased myeloid recruitment, cold T-cell-inflamed microenvironments, NRF2-driven metabolic signatures, and KRAS allele-specific downstream effector usage (RALA/RALB, PI3K-AKT, MAPK/ERK). driver_negative_subtype No standalone 'driver-negative subtype' is formally named in the paper. However, the review emphasizes co-mutation-defined subtypes that may appear driver-negative if only the dominant oncogene is considered: KL (KRAS + LKB1/STK11 loss, often KEAP1-enriched, immune-cold, metabolically rewired), KP (KRAS + TP53), and KC (KRAS + CDKN2A/CDKN2B loss with NKX2-1 loss and mucinous/gastrointestinal lineage program). It also discusses fusion-driven groups with relatively few co-drivers and weakly annotated biology, which can appear simpler by comparison.
clinical_or_exposure_context The review links these patterns to stage, metastatic disease, and prior therapy exposure; it also notes that mutational processes such as tobacco exposure and APOBEC mutagenesis, and immune selection, shape the landscape. For example, LKB1 loss is enriched in ~25% of KRAS-mutant LUAD and is associated with a cold immune microenvironment and poor response to PD-1/PD-L1 therapy; KEAP1 co-mutations are associated with worse outcomes with platinum-doublet and chemo-immunotherapy; TP53 co-mutation is associated with more inflamed tumors and better immunotherapy outcomes in KRAS-mutant disease.
cross_cancer_parallel The paper explicitly compares this logic to myeloproliferative neoplasms, where mutation order affects phenotype, and to other cancers in which pathway alterations drive immune escape or lineage programs. It also references melanomas and urothelial bladder cancer as examples of oncogenic pathway-immune microenvironment interactions and PTEN-mediated immunotherapy resistance.
therapeutic_vulnerability_or_implication Therapeutically, the paper argues for biomarker-driven stratification beyond single drivers. It cites vulnerabilities in KL tumors to COPI-complex depletion, lysosomal acidification inhibition, biguanides (metformin/phenformin), ER stress induction, DTYMK depletion, gemcitabine plus CHK1 inhibition, cardiac glycosides, and HSP90 inhibitors, while noting MEK inhibitor resistance in KL models and poor benefit from bevacizumab addition. KRAS/KEAP1 co-altered tumors may be sensitive to glutaminase inhibition, whereas TP53 co-mutation can predict poorer EGFR TKI outcomes but may correlate with better PD-1 response in KRAS-mutant LUAD. The paper also highlights ATM loss as a sensitizer to PARP/ATR inhibitors and radiation in preclinical models.
validation_evidence_and_model_system Validation comes from multiple levels: TCGA and large clinical sequencing cohorts, independent profiling studies, multiregion sequencing, affinity propagation and NMF consensus clustering, GEMMs (for example KrasG12D;Lkb1Fl/Fl, KrasG12D;Trp53 models, Keap1;Pten models, Egfr and Alk models), cell-line functional studies, CRISPR/Cas9 depletion, and treatment-response associations in patients. For driver-negative-like biology, the strongest evidence is indirect and integrative: co-mutation-defined transcriptomic subtypes, immune profiling, and preclinical drug sensitivity studies rather than a single dedicated validation experiment.
