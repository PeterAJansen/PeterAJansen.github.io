driver_negative_definition The paper operationalized "driver-negative"/"triple-negative" lung adenocarcinoma as tumors negative for EGFR mutation, KRAS mutation, and EML4-ALK fusion by clinical pathology testing, and in a broader replication set as EGFR-, KRAS-, and EML4-ALK-negative primary lung adenocarcinomas. Negativity was established using PCR and DNA sequencing for EGFR/KRAS hotspot regions and FISH for EML4-ALK, then deeper DNA/RNA sequencing to search for hidden drivers.
candidate_driver_pathway_or_mechanism The main hidden driver hypothesis is a kinase-fusion oncogene mechanism, specifically RET activation by fusion to KIF5B. The authors argue this explains at least a subset of oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinomas and represents a hidden actionable RTK driver rather than pathway-state-only biology.
alteration_or_activation_mode Chromosomal inversion generating an in-frame fusion transcript; the fusion juxtaposes KIF5B coiled-coil dimerization sequences with RET kinase domain, leading to dimerization-dependent autophosphorylation and oncogenic activation. The transcript is highly expressed, and RET exons downstream of the breakpoint are overexpressed.
implicated_gene_axis_or_pathway RET receptor tyrosine kinase signaling, activated through KIF5B-RET fusion. The mechanistic axis is KIF5B coiled-coil-mediated dimerization of RET kinase, analogous to RET fusion activation in papillary thyroid carcinoma.
pathway_activation_evidence Evidence includes RNA-seq showing 34 discordant paired-end reads and 60 spanning reads across the fusion junction, RET exon 12-20 expression exclusively downstream of the breakpoint, approximately 10-fold higher expression of RET exons 12-20 than in RET-rearrangement-negative lung cancers, and inferred autophosphorylation/dimerization biology from domain structure. The activation is genetically explained by the fusion/inversion rather than being an unexplained pathway-state change.
driver_negative_subtype A triple-negative lung adenocarcinoma subtype defined by lack of EGFR, KRAS, and EML4-ALK alterations, enriched among a young never-smoker patient and additional primary adenocarcinomas. The paper implies a molecular subtype of driver-negative LUAD that can harbor rare kinase fusions such as KIF5B-RET.
clinical_or_exposure_context Index case was a 33-year-old never-smoker with no family cancer history and poorly differentiated lung adenocarcinoma with liver and bone metastases. The broader context emphasized never-smoker lung adenocarcinoma and young age; the replication cohort included primary lung adenocarcinomas, some triple-negative and some double-negative.
cross_cancer_parallel RET fusions and RET activation were previously established in papillary thyroid carcinoma, and RET alterations were also noted in multiple endocrine neoplasia, prostate cancer, pancreatic cancer, and melanoma. The paper also parallels EML4-ALK and other fusion-driven lung cancers as precedent for oncogenic kinase fusions in NSCLC.
therapeutic_vulnerability_or_implication The fusion is proposed as a promising molecular target for personalized diagnosis and treatment of lung cancer. The authors specifically suggest development of agents targeting KIF5B-RET and imply RET kinase inhibition as the relevant drug class, but provide no direct drug-sensitivity data in this paper.
validation_evidence_and_model_system Validated by whole-genome sequencing, transcriptome sequencing, PCR, and Sanger sequencing in the index case; recurrence was shown in two additional primary lung adenocarcinomas out of 20 tested, with cDNA PCR and Sanger confirmation. Functional inference is based on domain architecture and known RET biology; no cell-line, xenograft, or inhibitor-response experiments were reported.
