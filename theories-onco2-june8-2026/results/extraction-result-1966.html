Paper Abstract: Lung cancer in never smokers (LCINS) is a common cause of cancer mortality but its genomic landscape is poorly characterized. Here high-coverage whole-genome sequencing of 232 LCINS showed 3 subtypes defined by copy number aberrations. The dominant subtype (piano), which is rare in lung cancer in smokers, features somatic UBA1 mutations, germline AR variants and stem cell-like properties, including low mutational burden, high intratumor heterogeneity, long telomeres, frequent KRAS mutations and slow growth, as suggested by the occurrence of cancer drivers’ progenitor cells many years before tumor diagnosis. The other subtypes are characterized by specific amplifications and EGFR mutations (mezzo-forte) and whole-genome doubling (forte). No strong tobacco smoking signatures were detected, even in cases with exposure to secondhand tobacco smoke. Genes within the receptor tyrosine kinase–Ras pathway had distinct impacts on survival; five genomic alterations independently doubled mortality. These findings create avenues for personalized treatment in LCINS. driver_negative_definition The paper does not define 'driver-negative' by a formal exclusion list, but the closest equivalent is the 'piano' LCINS subtype and related LUAD cases in which there is a scarcity of known recurrent driver alterations/fusions. These tumors were identified by high-coverage WGS after excluding cases with poor quality; recurrent drivers were called with IntOGen/CGC/boostDM, and SCNA clustering was used to define subtypes. The paper notes that LUAD in piano 'rarely harbored a known recurrent driver' except NKX2-1, SETD2, UBA1, and some RET/NKX2-related events, and explicitly states that some piano tumors may have 'no detected known cancer driver gene mutations or fusions.'
candidate_driver_pathway_or_mechanism The main proposed hidden driver mechanism is a stem cell-like or quiescent adult stem/progenitor cell state, potentially promoted by mutations in genes such as KRAS, UBA1, RET, ARID1A, NOTCH1, NKX2-1, or SETD2, rather than a single canonical oncogenic pathway lesion. The authors hypothesize that exit from quiescence and stemness-related lineage programs could explain low mutation burden, high ITH, and the presence of cases with no obvious driver.
alteration_or_activation_mode For the proposed biology, the operative mode is not a single recurrent amplification or fusion but a combination of rare mutations, occasional fusions, germline variants, and stemness-associated transcriptional programs; the paper also discusses possible mutation-induced exit from quiescence. In the broader piano subtype, alterations include rare driver mutations (e.g., KRAS, UBA1, SETD2, NKX2-1), RET fusions unique to piano, and LOH/gain events, but no dominant classical driver class is established for all cases.
implicated_gene_axis_or_pathway Stem cell/quiescence and lineage-state programs; KRAS; UBA1; RET/NKX2-1 axis; ARID1A; NOTCH1; SETD2; SOX2/SOX9/HMGA2 development score; basal cell/lineage infidelity markers. The paper also frames piano as having relative paucity of RTK-RAS and TP53 pathway alterations.
pathway_activation_evidence Evidence is indirect and largely inferential rather than based on phosphoproteomics or pathway reporter assays. The strongest evidence is genomic/evolutionary: low TMB, long telomeres, high subclonality/ITH, delayed MRCA timing, scarcity of known drivers, and RNA-seq-based higher 'development score' and basal-cell marker expression in piano, interpreted as consistent with stem cell-like state. There is no direct pERK/pAKT/pS6 readout reported for piano as a driver-negative state.
driver_negative_subtype Piano is the explicit subtype most relevant to driver-negative LCINS/LUAD. It is defined genomically by low arm-level SCNAs, low WGD frequency, low TMB, fewer SVs/kataegis, and enrichment of long telomeres and high subclonal mutation ratio; transcriptomic validation suggests a stem cell-like/developmental state. It is primarily a genomics-defined subtype with supporting transcriptomic evidence.
clinical_or_exposure_context Observed in never-smokers, including both LUAD and carcinoids, with piano being particularly enriched among carcinoids. Piano tumors showed significantly longer latency from inferred MRCA to diagnosis than other subtypes and overall better survival; passive smoking did not produce a distinct mutational-signature pattern. The subtype also had a lower estimated MRCA age, especially in carcinoids, though this required confirmation.
cross_cancer_parallel The paper explicitly connects the stem-like/quiescent-cell hypothesis to adult stem cell biology in other tissues and notes that mutations such as ARID1A and NOTCH1 have been found in normal or near-normal bronchial epithelial cells from former smokers. It also compares the biology to lung adenocarcinomas from smokers and discusses broad cancer stemness/regeneration concepts, but does not provide a specific cross-cancer driver-negative subtype analogous to a named entity in another cancer.
therapeutic_vulnerability_or_implication Therapeutic options are described as limited because piano has a scarcity of actionable drivers. The authors suggest potential strategies involving KRAS targeting, stem cell-associated signaling pathways, or modulation of the stem cell microenvironment, but these are presented as promising rather than validated. They also state that low TMB and HLA-LOH may reduce benefit from immunotherapy.
validation_evidence_and_model_system Validation is primarily cohort-based and computational: high-coverage WGS of 232 LCINS samples, RNA-seq in 35 adenocarcinomas, mutational-signature analysis, timing/evolutionary reconstruction, and comparison to TCGA/PCAWG and passive-smoking subgroups. The stemness hypothesis is supported by RNA-seq-derived development score and basal-cell marker enrichment, but the authors explicitly state that larger studies and orthogonal methods such as single-cell RNA-seq and methylome analyses are needed; no cell-line, organoid, or in vivo functional validation is reported.
