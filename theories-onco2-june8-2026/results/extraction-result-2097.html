Paper Venue: PLoS Medicine

Paper TL;DR: The results suggest extracellular missense mutations as a novel mechanism for oncogenic EGFR activation and may help identify patients who can benefit from EGFR kinase inhibitors for treatment of glioblastoma.

Paper Abstract: Background Protein tyrosine kinases are important regulators of cellular homeostasis with tightly controlled catalytic activity. Mutations in kinase-encoding genes can relieve the autoinhibitory constraints on kinase activity, can promote malignant transformation, and appear to be a major determinant of response to kinase inhibitor therapy. Missense mutations in the EGFR kinase domain, for example, have recently been identified in patients who showed clinical responses to EGFR kinase inhibitor therapy. Methods and Findings Encouraged by the promising clinical activity of epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) kinase inhibitors in treating glioblastoma in humans, we have sequenced the complete EGFR coding sequence in glioma tumor samples and cell lines. We identified novel missense mutations in the extracellular domain of EGFR in 13.6% (18/132) of glioblastomas and 12.5% (1/8) of glioblastoma cell lines. These EGFR mutations were associated with increased EGFR gene dosage and conferred anchorage-independent growth and tumorigenicity to NIH-3T3 cells. Cells transformed by expression of these EGFR mutants were sensitive to small-molecule EGFR kinase inhibitors. Conclusions Our results suggest extracellular missense mutations as a novel mechanism for oncogenic EGFR activation and may help identify patients who can benefit from EGFR kinase inhibitors for treatment of glioblastoma.

oncogene_negative_definition Glioma samples (143 fresh-frozen tumors plus 8 glioblastoma cell lines; 151 total with near-complete EGFR coding coverage) were sequenced across the entire EGFR coding region by bidirectional Sanger sequencing of PCR amplicons spanning each exon and flanking intronic sequence. Candidate variants were then validated by allele-specific MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry genotyping. In this study, the relevant 'negative' comparator for canonical lung-cancer-like EGFR activation was the absence of kinase-domain mutations in most gliomas, with the lone kinase-domain mutation observed being L861Q; the paper also states that no glioma-related ectodomain mutations were detected in 119 primary lung tumors, while those lung tumors did harbor kinase-domain EGFR mutations.
assay_strategy_and_detection_limits Discovery used near-complete targeted resequencing of the full EGFR coding sequence rather than only hotspot exons, followed by orthogonal MALDI-TOF genotyping of all samples. Copy number was assessed with Affymetrix 100K SNP arrays and FISH, and EGFRvIII with RT-PCR/IHC/immunoblotting. Explicit blind spots include incomplete exon coverage in some samples (at least 21 of 28 exons, 75%, covered for all 151 samples), possible contamination by stromal tissue because tumors were not microdissected, and reliance on DNA-level exon sequencing that would not by itself detect noncoding regulatory lesions, cryptic rearrangements, or low-level subclonal alterations outside sequenced regions.

The authors also reexamined a glioblastoma erlotinib clinical trial and found an R108K case among responders and among nonresponders, with PTEN loss offered as a possible explanation for failure in the latter.
