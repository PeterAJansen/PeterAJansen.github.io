Paper Title: Combinatorial tumor suppressor inactivation efficiently initiates lung adenocarcinoma with therapeutic vulnerabilities

Paper Venue: bioRxiv

Paper TL;DR: It is demonstrated that oncogene-negative tumors and cell lines with activated RAS/MAPK and PI3K pathways are vulnerable to pharmacological inhibition of these signaling axes and transform the understanding of this prevalent yet understudied subtype of lung adenocarcinoma.

Paper Abstract: Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer death worldwide, with lung adenocarcinoma being the most common subtype. Many oncogenes and tumor suppressor genes are altered in this cancer type and the discovery of oncogene mutations has led to the development of targeted therapies and better clinical outcomes. However, a large fraction of lung adenocarcinomas lacks mutations in known oncogenes, and the genesis and treatment of these oncogene-negative tumors remain enigmatic. Here, we perform iterative in vivo functional screens with quantitative autochthonous mouse model systems to uncover the genetic and biochemical changes that enable efficient lung tumor initiation in the absence of oncogene alterations. Through the generation of hundreds of diverse combinations of tumor suppressor alterations, we demonstrate that inactivation of suppressors of the RAS and PI3K pathways drive the development of oncogene- negative lung adenocarcinoma. Pathway-level human genomic data analysis and histology identified RAS/MAPK and PI3K pathway activation as a common event in oncogene-negative human lung adenocarcinomas. We demonstrate that oncogene-negative tumors and cell lines with activated RAS/MAPK and PI3K pathways are vulnerable to pharmacological inhibition of these signaling axes. Collectively, these results transform our understanding of this prevalent yet understudied subtype of lung adenocarcinoma. STATEMENT OF SIGNIFICANCE Many lung adenocarcinomas do not have mutations in known proto-oncogenes, and as a result, targeted therapies are unavailable for treating these patients. Here, we uncover driver pathways in a subset of these oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinomas and demonstrate the therapeutic value of inhibiting these pathways.

validation_result The mouse-derived subtype signature was highest in oncogene-negative human LUAD relative to oncogene-driven tumors. pERK and pAKT staining confirmed pathway activation in mouse and human oncogene-negative tumors. Drug inhibition of SHP2 and AKT reduced growth of mouse and human Onc-negative RAS/PI3K cell lines, and combination therapy was synergistic in vitro and effective in vivo.
therapeutic_implication Oncogene-negative RAS/PI3K LUAD may be treatable with inhibitors of SHP2 and AKT, with broader implications for targeting other RAS and PI3K pathway nodes such as SOS, MEK, ERK, PI3K, or mTOR.
therapeutic_evidence In vivo, RMC-4550 (SHP2 inhibitor) reduced tumor burden; capivasertib (AKT1/2 inhibitor) alone was ineffective in vivo; the combination trended toward the best efficacy and reduced tumor burden by ~30% relative to RMC-4550 alone. In mouse and human oncogene-negative cell lines, RMC-4550 and capivasertib synergized to inhibit growth, increased apoptosis, reduced proliferation, and regressed subcutaneous allografts.
clinical_or_demographic_association The paper reports broadly similar mutational burden and clinical characteristics among oncogene-negative, oncogene-indeterminate, and oncogene-positive LUAD in TCGA, and does not identify a strong demographic signature. It does note that oncogene-negative LUAD is clinically common, affecting more than 150,000 patients per year worldwide.
study_limitations Human driver-negative classification relied on curated known proto-oncogenes and incomplete targeted panels in GENIE/STAMP, so occult drivers could remain undetected. The paper explicitly notes that epigenetic and other non-genomic mechanisms likely contribute, and that many possible mutational combinations remain untested.
