Paper Title: Combinatorial inactivation of tumor suppressors efficiently initiates lung adenocarcinoma with therapeutic vulnerabilities

Paper Venue: Cancer Research

Paper TL;DR: Generation of hundreds of diverse combinations of tumor suppressor alterations demonstrates that inactivation of suppressors of the RAS and PI3K pathways drives the development of oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinoma.

Paper Abstract: Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer death worldwide, with lung adenocarcinoma being the most common subtype. Many oncogenes and tumor suppressor genes are altered in this cancer type, and the discovery of oncogene mutations has led to the development of targeted therapies that have improved clinical outcomes. However, a large fraction of lung adenocarcinomas lacks mutations in known oncogenes, and the genesis and treatment of these oncogene-negative tumors remain enigmatic. Here, we perform iterative in vivo functional screens using quantitative autochthonous mouse model systems to uncover the genetic and biochemical changes that enable efficient lung tumor initiation in the absence of oncogene alterations. Generation of hundreds of diverse combinations of tumor suppressor alterations demonstrates that inactivation of suppressors of the RAS and PI3K pathways drives the development of oncogene-negative lung adenocarcinoma. Human genomic data and histology identified RAS/MAPK and PI3K pathway activation as a common event in oncogene-negative human lung adenocarcinomas. These Onc-negativeRAS/PI3K tumors and related cell lines are vulnerable to pharmacological inhibition of these signaling axes. These results transform our understanding of this prevalent yet understudied subtype of lung adenocarcinoma.

In human oncogene-negative LUAD, pERK and pAKT IHC found ~45% with moderate-to-strong activation of both pathways. In TCGA, >60% had alterations in either RAS or PI3K pathway components and 22% had alterations in both. PTEN IHC showed low PTEN protein in 13/20 tumors lacking genomic PTEN mutations. The paper argues that activation is often not fully explained by canonical hotspot oncogene mutations.
driver_negative_subtype Onc-negative^RAS/PI3K^ lung adenocarcinoma subtype. Defined by concurrent activation of RAS and PI3K pathways, frequent NF1/RASA1/PTEN alterations or loss, pERK/pAKT positivity, and a mouse-derived transcriptional signature enriched in oncogene-negative human LUAD. This is an integrated genomic, histologic, and transcriptomic subtype.
clinical_or_exposure_context The paper states patients with oncogene-negative, oncogene-indeterminate, and oncogene-positive LUAD had broadly similar mutational burden and clinical characteristics in the analyzed cohorts. It notes oncogene-negative LUAD is clinically common but does not assign a strong smoking/sex/age-specific enrichment here; it does mention that some historical references link RASA1/NF1-mutant lung cancer to smoking-associated disease, but this paper's main subtype is not defined by exposure.
cross_cancer_parallel The paper explicitly compares its logic to other cancers where multiple lesions converge on shared pathways and notes that pathway activation can arise through diverse genomic and epigenomic events. It also cites prior work on RASA1/NF1-mutant lung cancer sensitive to MEK inhibition and broader cancer pathway mapping, but the main parallel is the pathway-centric rather than gene-centric model used across cancers.
therapeutic_vulnerability_or_implication Onc-negative^RAS/PI3K^ tumors were vulnerable to pharmacologic inhibition of RAS/PI3K-axis nodes. In mice, SHP2 inhibitor RMC-4550 reduced tumor burden, capivasertib monotherapy was ineffective in vivo, and the RMC-4550/capivasertib combination was the most effective, reducing burden by about 30% relative to RMC-4550 alone. In cell lines and allografts, the combination synergized to inhibit growth, induce apoptosis, and cause regression. The paper also suggests other pathway nodes such as SOS, MEK, ERK, PI3K, and mTOR as potential therapeutic targets, supported preclinically.
validation_evidence_and_model_system Validated in quantitative autochthonous mouse models using Cre/loxP and CRISPR/Cas9 combinatorial editing with Tuba-seq, histology, IHC, and RNA-seq; confirmed in mouse oncogene-negative cell lines and subcutaneous allografts; extended to human LUAD using TCGA/GENIE analysis and IHC on 35 oncogene-negative and 18 oncogene-positive tumors; further supported by two human oncogene-negative cell lines (NCI-H1838, NCI-H1623) showing pathway activation and drug synergy.
