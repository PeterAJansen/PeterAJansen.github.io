Paper Title: A functional taxonomy of tumor suppression in oncogenic KRAS-driven lung cancer

Paper Venue: Cancer Discovery

Paper TL;DR: A multiplexed mouse model of oncogenic KRAS-driven lung cancer is used to quantify the impact of forty-eight known and putative tumor suppressor genes on diverse aspects of carcinogenesis at an unprecedented scale and resolution and uncover many previously understudied functional tumor suppressors that constrain cancer in vivo.

Paper Abstract: Cancer genotyping has identified a large number of putative tumor suppressor genes. Carcinogenesis is a multi-step process, however the importance and specific roles of many of these genes during tumor initiation, growth and progression remain unknown. Here we use a multiplexed mouse model of oncogenic KRAS-driven lung cancer to quantify the impact of forty-eight known and putative tumor suppressor genes on diverse aspects of carcinogenesis at an unprecedented scale and resolution. We uncover many previously understudied functional tumor suppressors that constrain cancer in vivo. Inactivation of some genes substantially increased growth, while the inactivation of others increases tumor initiation and/or the emergence of exceptionally large tumors. These functional in vivo analyses revealed an unexpectedly complex landscape of tumor suppression that has implications for understanding cancer evolution, interpreting clinical cancer genome sequencing data, and directing approaches to limit tumor initiation and progression.

driver_negative_definition The paper does not operationalize a formal 'driver-negative' LUAD cohort. Instead, it uses autochthonous KRASG12D-driven mouse lung tumors in a Cas9-enabled Tuba-seq platform, with Cas9-negative KT mice as technical controls; 'negative' in this context refers to inert sgRNAs and to tumors lacking an obvious strong growth effect from a given targeted tumor suppressor rather than to absence of canonical oncogenic drivers.
candidate_driver_pathway_or_mechanism The main hidden-driver logic is not a hidden oncogene but loss of tumor suppressor function across distinct pathway states: early tumor initiation, overall growth, and rare exceptional growth are driven by inactivation of specific suppressors such as PTEN/PI3K, LKB1, NF1/RASA1, COMPASS/chromatin regulators, and STAG2/cohesin. The authors emphasize that the landscape is multifaceted and context-dependent, with different genes constraining different carcinogenic phases.
alteration_or_activation_mode Tumor suppressor loss via CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene inactivation; in the human correlative component, low/negative protein expression is highlighted for STAG2, suggesting protein loss as a pathway-altering state. For other genes, the paper discusses truncating or dispersed mutations in human LUAD as consistent with tumor suppressor behavior.

For the broader screen, pathway involvement is inferred from genotype-specific increases in tumor number, burden, and heavy-tailed size distributions rather than direct phosphoproteomic readouts.

therapeutic_vulnerability_or_implication The paper is mainly hypothesis-generating rather than therapeutically directed, but it implies pathway-level vulnerabilities: RNF43 deficiency has been linked to porcupine inhibitors in other cancers, PTEN/LKB1/NF1 and COMPASS-pathway alterations may shape signaling dependencies, and STAG2 loss may identify an important but mechanistically unresolved class of lung tumors. The strongest explicit therapeutic framing is that understanding which suppressors control initiation, growth, or exceptional growth could inform prevention, early detection, and therapeutic targeting.
